# Single-Issue Campaign Strategy: Immigration Policy in the 2026 New Zealand Election

**Prepared: March 2026**
**Objective: Force all political parties to publish detailed immigration policy platforms before the 2026 general election.**

---

## 1. LESSONS FROM HISTORY

### 1.1 The Brexit Campaign (UK, 2015-2016)

The Leave campaign succeeded by connecting immigration to tangible daily concerns: NHS waiting times, school places, housing pressure, wage stagnation. The key mechanism was relentless reframing. Immigration wasn't presented as an abstract cultural issue but as the hidden cause behind things people already cared about. Vote Leave's central tactic was the "£350 million a week" bus, a single provocative claim that forced the entire media apparatus to discuss EU funding and, by extension, immigration. The Remain side could never escape the frame.

**Transferable to NZ:** The NZ housing crisis, hospital waitlists, infrastructure strain, and wage stagnation are all tangible daily concerns. The campaign should consistently connect immigration volume to these existing frustrations. Every conversation about hospital wait times is a conversation about population planning. Every conversation about housing is a conversation about demand-side pressure. The tactic is to make immigration the upstream explanation for problems voters already feel.

### 1.2 The Australian Marriage Equality Campaign (2015-2017)

The Coalition for Marriage Equality spent years building a broad coalition that made same-sex marriage a mainstream, cross-partisan issue rather than a left-wing cause. The key mechanism was personalisation. The campaign centred on stories of real couples and families, making it emotionally impossible for politicians to hide behind procedural delays. When the postal survey was announced, the infrastructure was already in place to mobilise. The campaign also used corporate endorsements (Qantas, ANZ Bank) to signal that this wasn't radical but mainstream.

**Transferable to NZ:** Immigration policy needs to be depersonalised from race and personalised around impact. The campaign should centre on the nurse who can't afford rent, the young couple locked out of homeownership, the small town whose infrastructure hasn't kept pace with growth. Corporate and institutional endorsements (from housing charities, medical associations, teacher unions) signal mainstream legitimacy.

### 1.3 March for Our Lives / Gun Control (US, 2018)

Parkland students forced gun control onto the agenda through moral urgency and generational voice. The key mechanism was creating a moment of maximum political discomfort: "You took NRA money and children died. What will you do?" The candidate scorecard and grading system (rating every politician on their gun record) created a simple, shareable accountability tool. Politicians who refused to engage were graded "F" and that grade followed them through the election.

**Transferable to NZ:** A candidate/party scorecard on immigration policy is one of the most powerful forcing mechanisms available. Parties that refuse to release policy get graded "No Position" or "Refuses to Engage," which is politically more damaging than any actual position they could take. The scorecard becomes media content in itself.

### 1.4 The NZ Flag Referendum Campaign (2015-2016)

This is instructive as a cautionary tale. The flag change campaign was driven top-down by John Key rather than bottom-up by public demand. It failed because there was no genuine grassroots energy and it was seen as a vanity project. However, the Keep the Flag camp succeeded by framing change as wasteful and unnecessary, using a simple emotional hook (respect for veterans, identity).

**Transferable to NZ:** The immigration campaign must be genuinely grassroots, or at least appear so. It cannot be associated with a single political party or leader. It must have authentic voices from affected communities. The flag referendum also showed that NZ's small media market means a well-organised campaign can dominate coverage for weeks at relatively low cost.

### 1.5 The UK Poll Tax Campaign (1989-1990)

The anti-poll tax movement forced Margaret Thatcher's government to reverse course on a flagship policy. The key mechanism was local organising at extraordinary scale: anti-poll tax unions were formed in almost every council ward in Britain. The campaign combined mass non-payment (civil disobedience) with a huge demonstration that escalated into the Trafalgar Square riot. The political cost became unbearable.

**Transferable to NZ:** The structural lesson is local organising. NZ has 72 electorates. A campaign that can demonstrate organised supporters in every electorate, or even 20-30 key electorates, creates a political reality that parties cannot ignore. In MMP, electorate-level organising also matters for the party vote, which is where smaller parties like NZ First live or die.

### 1.6 GetUp! Australia (2005-present)

GetUp! built a permanent digital campaigning infrastructure that could be deployed rapidly on any issue. The key mechanism was a large email/SMS list (over 1 million members) that could flood politicians' offices with calls, generate media stories about public outrage, and fund targeted advertising in marginal seats. GetUp!'s innovation was treating political campaigning like a tech startup: rapid A/B testing of messages, real-time data on what resonated, and ruthless focus on conversion metrics.

**Transferable to NZ:** NZ's small population means that even a list of 50,000 engaged supporters would be enormous by local standards. The campaign should build digital infrastructure from day one. Every petition signature, every email open, every event attendee should be captured in a database that can be activated for targeted pressure at key moments.

### 1.7 The New Zealand Anti-Springbok Tour Movement (1981)

The largest civil protest movement in NZ history demonstrated that New Zealanders will mobilise passionately on issues that are framed as matters of national identity and values. The key mechanism was that the protests forced every New Zealander to take a side. There was no neutral position. The movement also showed that NZ's small, concentrated media market means that sustained protest action dominates the news cycle.

**Transferable to NZ:** The immigration campaign should aspire to create a similar dynamic where every party, every candidate, and every voter is expected to have a position. The goal isn't to create the same level of social disruption but to create the same level of political unavoidability.

## 2. THE CORE CAMPAIGN DESIGN

### 2.1 Theory of Change

The theory of change works through four pressure points that operate simultaneously in an MMP system:

**Polling pressure.** NZ parties are exquisitely sensitive to polling. If immigration consistently polls as a top-three voter concern, parties must respond. The campaign's first job is to ensure that polling organisations are asking about immigration and that the results are publicised.

**Coalition arithmetic.** In MMP, governing is about coalition formation. NZ First already owns immigration as an issue (polling around 11-12%). If immigration becomes more salient, NZ First's vote share rises, which changes the coalition arithmetic for both National and Labour. Both major parties need to either co-opt the issue (by releasing their own policy) or risk losing voters to NZ First. This is the core leverage point.

**Media pressure.** NZ's small media market means a well-run campaign can generate disproportionate coverage. If immigration is in the news every week, parties face constant questions about it. The cost of "no comment" rises with each news cycle.

**Electorate-level pressure.** In MMP, each voter has two votes. Electorate candidates face direct pressure from constituents in a way that party leaders insulated in Wellington do not. Town halls, local media, and doorstep conversations create bottom-up pressure.

The chain is: Campaign activity generates media coverage, which generates polling movement, which changes coalition arithmetic, which forces party policy.

### 2.2 Campaign Name and Brand

**Name: "Plan for Growth NZ"**

The name is deliberately constructive, not oppositional. It's not "Stop Immigration" or "Reduce Immigration." It says: New Zealand is growing, and we need a plan. This framing does three things:

1. It's unobjectionable. Nobody can argue against having a plan.
2. It reframes immigration as a planning and infrastructure issue rather than a race issue.
3. It puts the burden on parties to produce a plan, which is the campaign's core demand.

**Tagline: "How many people? What's the plan?"**

**Visual identity:** Clean, modern, data-driven aesthetic. Blues and greens (NZ colours, not party-affiliated). Maps, charts, and infographics as primary visual language. This signals seriousness and evidence, not anger or fear.

**Central demand:** Every party contesting the 2026 election must publish a detailed immigration policy that answers five questions:

1. What is your target range for annual net migration?
2. How will you match immigration to housing supply, infrastructure capacity, and public service provision?
3. What visa categories will you prioritise, and which will you reduce?
4. How will you enforce immigration law, including overstayers and work visa conditions?
5. What is your plan for refugee and humanitarian intake?

These five questions become the campaign's scorecard. They are specific enough to be meaningful but broad enough to capture the full scope of immigration policy.

### 2.3 Framing and Emotional Core

The greatest risk to this campaign is being dismissed as racist or xenophobic. This kills it before it starts. The framing must be relentlessly economic, infrastructural, and planning-focused.

**The frame is: "We welcome immigration. We oppose poor planning."**

This is not an anti-immigration campaign. It's a pro-planning campaign. The emotional core is frustration with a system that seems to operate on autopilot, with no democratic input and no accountability. People are angry about housing costs, hospital waits, congested roads, and stagnant wages. The campaign connects these to population growth without targeting any ethnic or national group.

**Key messages:**

- "New Zealand has added the population of Christchurch in the last few years, with almost no new infrastructure to show for it."
- "No other country lets immigration run on autopilot. Why does New Zealand?"
- "Every new Kiwi deserves a house, a doctor, and a school for their kids. Where's the plan?"
- "This isn't about who comes. It's about how many, how fast, and whether we're ready."

**Voices:** The campaign should be fronted by people who are personally affected by the consequences of rapid population growth: young renters, healthcare workers, teachers in overcrowded schools, tradies stuck in traffic, recent immigrants who feel the system failed them too. Crucially, include recent immigrants who are frustrated by the strain on the same services they were promised.

## 3. FORCING MECHANISMS

### 3.1 The Party Scorecard

Build a public, regularly updated scorecard for every party and every electorate candidate. The scorecard rates each party on whether they have answered the five core questions. Possible ratings:

- **Detailed policy released** (green)
- **Partial position stated** (yellow)
- **No policy / refuses to engage** (red)

The scorecard is published on the campaign website, shared on social media, and sent to media before every debate and major political event. It becomes a running tally of accountability. Journalists can reference it. Voters can share it. The visual simplicity (green/yellow/red) makes it instantly shareable.

NZ First will likely score green immediately. This creates competitive pressure: if NZ First has a plan and you don't, you look unprepared.

### 3.2 The Five Questions Pledge

Create a formal, public pledge document. Ask every party leader and every electorate candidate to sign a commitment to release detailed immigration policy before election day. The pledge is hand-delivered to party offices with media present. Refusal to sign is itself a news story.

The pledge should be designed as a physical document, not just a digital form. Photos of it being delivered, being signed (or refused), and being displayed at public events create visual media content.

### 3.3 Town Hall Events

Organise public town hall events in 15-20 key electorates. Invite all local candidates. The format is simple: each candidate has 5 minutes to present their party's immigration plan, followed by audience Q&A. Candidates who don't attend are represented by an empty chair with their name on it. The empty chair is the photograph that makes the news.

Target electorates where immigration pressure is most visible: South Auckland (Mangere, Manukau East), Hamilton, Tauranga, and fast-growing regional centres. Also target swing electorates where the party vote is competitive.

### 3.4 Petition with Threshold Escalation

Launch a petition demanding that all parties release immigration policy. Set public thresholds with escalation:

- **10,000 signatures:** Formal letter to all party leaders.
- **25,000 signatures:** Full-page newspaper advertisements.
- **50,000 signatures:** Delegation to Parliament, media event on the steps of the Beehive.
- **100,000 signatures:** This represents 2% of the population, or roughly 3% of eligible voters. At this point, the campaign has undeniable democratic legitimacy.

Each threshold crossing is a media moment. The petition itself is a list-building tool: every signer can be contacted for future campaign actions.

### 3.5 Targeted Advertising in Key Electorates

Run social media and local newspaper advertising in electorates held by senior party figures. The ads are simple: "[Candidate Name] has no immigration plan. Ask them why." This creates direct pressure on individuals who can influence their party's position. In NZ, targeting a sitting minister in their own electorate gets noticed.

### 3.6 Polling Data Releases

Commission regular polling on immigration attitudes and release the results strategically. Commission a reputable NZ pollster (Curia, Talbot Mills, Reid Research) to ask:

- "How important is immigration policy to your vote?"
- "Do you think net migration is too high, about right, or too low?"
- "Which party do you trust most on immigration?"
- "Should parties be required to state their immigration targets?"

Release results in tranches timed to maximum media impact: before party conferences, before budget announcements, during slow news weeks. The data becomes the story.

### 3.7 Coalition Leverage

In MMP, the question "Who will you go into coalition with?" is always live. The campaign should publicly ask every major party: "Will you go into coalition with a party that has no immigration plan?" This forces the coalition question and immigration question together. If NZ First has the strongest immigration policy, both National and Labour face the question of whether they'll match it as a condition of coalition.

## 4. COALITION BUILDING

### 4.1 Natural Allies

The campaign's coalition must be broad enough to resist the "racist fringe" label. Here are the key groups:

**Housing advocates.** Organisations like Community Housing Aotearoa, Habitat for Humanity NZ, and various tenants' rights groups. The connection between population growth and housing demand is direct and evidence-based. Housing advocates are already mainstream and politically respected. They provide credibility.

**Healthcare worker unions.** The NZ Nurses Organisation (NZNO) and the Resident Doctors' Association have been vocal about staffing shortages and system strain. Framing: "We can't recruit enough nurses to serve the population we already have, and it's growing by 100,000+ a year."

**Education unions.** The NZEI and PPTA represent teachers dealing with overcrowded classrooms, particularly in high-growth areas like South Auckland, Hamilton, and Tauranga. Teachers are trusted voices.

**Infrastructure and transport advocates.** Groups like Greater Auckland, transport commentators, and infrastructure engineers who can speak to the gap between population growth and infrastructure investment. The visual of Auckland traffic is powerful.

**Regional development advocates.** Provincial chambers of commerce and regional councils that are either overwhelmed by growth they can't serve, or frustrated that immigration is concentrated in Auckland while regions miss out on skilled workers. Both angles work.

**Small business owners.** Businesses that compete for labour against larger firms that use work visas to access cheaper labour. Also businesses in hospitality and agriculture that depend on migrant workers and want a more predictable system.

**Environmental groups.** NZ's clean-green brand is strained by rapid population growth. Freshwater quality, urban sprawl, carbon emissions, and biodiversity loss all have population dimensions. Environmental groups can speak to the sustainability angle without any racial overtone.

**Recent immigrants and migrant community leaders.** This is critical. The campaign must include voices from migrant communities who want a better-managed system: people who waited years for visas, who were exploited by employers, who arrived to find the infrastructure couldn't support them. These voices inoculate the campaign against racism accusations.

**Maori voices.** Te Tiriti o Waitangi implications of immigration are real and underexplored. Immigration affects Maori access to housing, healthcare, and resources. Some iwi leaders and Maori academics have raised concerns about the Treaty implications of rapid population growth without Maori input. These voices must be invited respectfully, not co-opted.

### 4.2 Coalition Structure

Create a formal "Plan for Growth Alliance" with the following tiers:

**Endorsing organisations:** Groups that formally endorse the five-question demand. Their logos appear on the website and campaign materials. This is low-commitment and high-visibility.

**Contributing voices:** Individuals from various sectors who provide quotes, op-eds, and media appearances. They speak from their own expertise (housing, health, education) and connect it to immigration planning.

**Steering group:** A small (7-10 person) group that guides campaign strategy. Must include Maori representation, migrant representation, and at least one person with mainstream political credibility (a former MP, a respected academic, a well-known public figure).

### 4.3 Inoculation Against Racism Accusations

This is the campaign's greatest vulnerability. Inoculation strategies:

- **Diverse leadership.** The steering group and public spokespeople must visibly include Maori, Pasifika, and immigrant voices.
- **Pro-immigrant framing.** The campaign consistently says: "Immigrants deserve better. They deserve to arrive in a country that's ready for them."
- **Reject extremists publicly.** If far-right groups try to associate with the campaign, reject them loudly and publicly. Issue a clear statement of values early that draws the line.
- **Focus on numbers and systems, never on national origins.** The campaign discusses net migration targets, visa categories, and infrastructure capacity. It never discusses which countries people come from.
- **Academic backing.** Commission or publicise research from respected NZ economists and demographers (people like Michael Reddell, Julie Fry, or Massey University researchers) who have studied immigration's economic impacts.

## 5. MEDIA STRATEGY

### 5.1 Understanding the NZ Media Landscape

NZ has a concentrated media market, which is an advantage. There are essentially six outlets that matter for political coverage:

- **RNZ** (public broadcaster, high credibility, Morning Report and Checkpoint are key)
- **NZ Herald** (largest newspaper, Auckland-focused, Audrey Young and Claire Trevett on politics)
- **Stuff** (second-largest, Wellington-based, strong political bureau)
- **Newshub/Three** (TV, The AM Show for morning reach)
- **1News/TVNZ** (TV, Q+A for long-form political interviews)
- **Newsroom** (digital-only, high-quality, Bernard Hickey on economics)
- **The Spinoff** (digital, younger audience, politics and culture)

The small market means that if you can generate one strong story, it cascades across all outlets. A Stuff exclusive today becomes an RNZ interview tomorrow and a Q+A panel topic on Sunday.

### 5.2 Story Generation Tactics

**Data drops.** Release immigration data analysis that tells a story. For example: "Net migration since 2020 equals the population of [specific NZ city]." Or: "There are X new residents per new house built in [region]." Data visualisations travel well on social media and give journalists easy stories. Use Stats NZ data (publicly available) and present it in accessible formats.

**OIA requests.** File Official Information Act requests with Immigration NZ, MBIE, the Ministry of Health, the Ministry of Education, and local councils. Ask for: internal projections of population growth, infrastructure capacity assessments, hospital demand modelling linked to population, school enrollment projections. OIA responses take time but often produce newsworthy documents. File 20-30 OIA requests in the first month. Some will produce gold.

**Electorate-level data.** Produce immigration impact reports for specific electorates. "In the Maungakiekie electorate, population has grown X% but GP clinics have grown Y%." Electorate-specific data is catnip for local media and puts direct pressure on local MPs.

**Academic research amplification.** NZ economists and demographers have published work on immigration impacts that gets minimal media coverage. Repackage and promote it. Host a public seminar or webinar with three respected academics discussing immigration and economic impacts. Record it, clip it, distribute it.

**International comparisons.** Australia, Canada, and the UK have all had recent public debates about immigration levels. Commission a comparison: "Here's what Australia's migration plan looks like. Here's what Canada's looks like. New Zealand doesn't have one." The comparison format is easy to cover and makes NZ's lack of planning look abnormal.

### 5.3 Op-Ed Strategy

Place op-eds on a rotation across outlets. Each op-ed comes from a different voice and angle:

- A young couple on housing and immigration (Stuff)
- A nurse on hospital overcrowding and population growth (NZ Herald)
- A Maori academic on Treaty implications (Newsroom)
- A recent immigrant on the broken system (The Spinoff)
- An economist on the fiscal impacts (RNZ's The Detail or NZ Herald)
- A small-town mayor on regional infrastructure (Stuff regional)
- A former immigration official on system failures (NZ Herald)

Aim for one op-ed every two weeks across the campaign, escalating to weekly as the election approaches.

### 5.4 Social Media

Build a presence on Facebook (still dominant for NZ political discussion among 35+), Instagram (for younger voters and visual content), TikTok (for reach and virality), and X/Twitter (for journalists and political insiders).

Content types:
- **Data graphics** showing immigration numbers and impacts.
- **Short video testimonials** from affected people (30-60 seconds).
- **Scorecard updates** every time a party releases or fails to release policy.
- **Town hall clips** especially empty-chair moments.
- **Petition milestone celebrations** with each threshold.

Budget for paid social advertising targeting key demographics: 25-44 year olds in Auckland, Hamilton, Tauranga, and Wellington who are interested in housing, politics, or New Zealand current events.

### 5.5 Podcast and Long-Form

Pitch the campaign's spokespeople as guests on NZ's political podcasts: Gone By Lunchtime, When the Facts Change (Bernard Hickey), The Detail (RNZ), and others. Long-form conversations allow the nuanced, pro-planning framing to come through in a way that short news hits don't.

## 6. AUTOMATION AND CODE

This is where the campaign gains a structural advantage. Most NZ political campaigns are run on spreadsheets and manual effort. A code-first approach creates capability that punches well above its weight.

### 6.1 Campaign Website and Scorecard Platform

**What to build:** A public-facing website (planforgrowth.nz) with:

- The five questions and campaign rationale.
- A live party scorecard showing each party's status (detailed policy / partial / no policy). Colour-coded, visually clean.
- Electorate lookup: enter your address or electorate name, see local candidates and their party's scorecard rating.
- Petition integrated directly into the site with a live counter.
- A data dashboard showing key immigration statistics, updated automatically.

**Tech stack:** Next.js or Astro for the frontend, hosted on Vercel or Cloudflare Pages. A simple Postgres database (Supabase or Neon) for petition signatures and supporter data. Content managed via a headless CMS or simple markdown files.

**Claude Code can build:** The full website scaffold, scorecard display components, electorate lookup (using NZ Electoral Commission data), petition form with database integration, and data visualisation components using a charting library like Chart.js or D3.

### 6.2 Automated Media Monitoring

**What to build:** A system that monitors NZ media outlets for mentions of immigration, migration, visa, population growth, and related terms. It should:

- Scrape or use RSS feeds from RNZ, NZ Herald, Stuff, Newsroom, The Spinoff.
- Classify articles by topic, sentiment, and which parties are mentioned.
- Send a daily digest email to the campaign team summarising coverage.
- Flag any party policy announcements or immigration-related statements immediately.

**Tech stack:** A Python script running on a cron job (or a serverless function on AWS Lambda). RSS parsing with feedparser, simple keyword matching initially, with Claude API for more nuanced classification. Results stored in a database and sent via email (SendGrid or AWS SES).

**Claude Code can build:** The RSS scraper, keyword matching logic, Claude API integration for article classification, email digest template, and deployment configuration.

### 6.3 Supporter Database and CRM

**What to build:** A lightweight CRM that tracks:

- Every petition signer (name, email, electorate, postcode).
- Event attendees.
- Volunteers and their skills/availability.
- Supporter engagement history (emails opened, events attended, actions taken).
- Segmentation by electorate for targeted campaigns.

**Tech stack:** Airtable for simplicity, or a custom solution with Supabase. For email campaigns, integrate with Mailchimp, Brevo (formerly Sendinblue), or a self-hosted solution. For SMS, use a provider like Twilio or a NZ-based provider like Modica.

**Claude Code can build:** Database schema, API integrations, import/export scripts, and segmentation queries. It could also build a simple admin dashboard for the campaign team.

### 6.4 Data Dashboard: Immigration by Electorate

**What to build:** An interactive dashboard that shows immigration-related data at the electorate level:

- Population growth rate by electorate.
- Housing consents vs population growth.
- GP and hospital capacity vs population.
- School enrollment growth vs capacity.
- Infrastructure spending per capita.

Data sources: Stats NZ (population, migration), MBIE (building consents), Ministry of Health (provider data), Ministry of Education (school rolls). Most of this is publicly available via Stats NZ's APIs and data portals.

**Tech stack:** A web dashboard built with React/Next.js, using D3.js or Mapbox for geographic visualisations. Data pulled from Stats NZ's open data API and stored locally for fast access.

**Claude Code can build:** The data ingestion pipeline from Stats NZ APIs, the dashboard frontend with interactive maps, electorate-level comparison tools, and shareable report generators that create social-media-ready graphics.

### 6.5 Candidate Tracker

**What to build:** A database of every confirmed candidate for the 2026 election, linked to their party, electorate, public statements on immigration, and scorecard rating. As candidates are announced throughout 2026, the tracker updates. The public-facing version lets voters look up their electorate and see where candidates stand.

**Claude Code can build:** The database schema, a scraper that monitors party websites and NZ Electoral Commission announcements for new candidates, and a simple admin interface for the campaign team to update candidate positions as statements are made.

### 6.6 Email/SMS Campaign Automation

**What to build:** Automated campaign sequences:

- Welcome sequence for new petition signers (3 emails over 2 weeks introducing the campaign).
- Electorate-specific action alerts ("Your MP [Name] still hasn't answered the five questions. Here's their office number.").
- Event invitations targeted by location.
- Milestone celebration emails when petition thresholds are crossed.
- Pre-election mobilisation sequence.

**Claude Code can build:** Email templates, automation logic, integration with the CRM and email/SMS provider, A/B testing framework for subject lines and content.

### 6.7 Polling Aggregation Tool

**What to build:** A simple tool that aggregates NZ political polls and tracks:

- Party polling trends over time.
- Immigration salience in polling.
- NZ First vote share as a proxy for immigration concern.
- Seat projection modelling under MMP.

This helps the campaign team make strategic decisions and provides data for media releases.

**Claude Code can build:** A scraper for NZ polling data (from Wikipedia's polling tables or direct from pollsters), trend calculation and visualisation, a simple MMP seat calculator, and an internal dashboard.

### 6.8 AI-Assisted Content Generation

Use the Claude API to assist with:

- Drafting op-eds and press releases (with human editing and approval).
- Generating electorate-specific fact sheets from the data dashboard.
- Summarising OIA responses (which can be hundreds of pages).
- Creating social media post variations for A/B testing.
- Analysing media coverage patterns and sentiment.

This is a force multiplier for a small campaign team.

## 7. CAMPAIGN TIMELINE

### Phase 1: Foundation (March-April 2026)

**March 2026 -- Weeks 1-2: Build**
- Register the domain planforgrowth.nz and set up the website.
- Build the scorecard, petition, and basic data dashboard.
- Draft the five questions, the pledge document, and core campaign messaging.
- Recruit the initial steering group (7-10 people from diverse backgrounds).
- File the first batch of OIA requests (20-30 across government agencies).
- Set up the media monitoring system.
- Create social media accounts and begin posting foundational content.

**March-April 2026 -- Weeks 3-6: Soft Launch**
- Launch the petition with a target of 10,000 signatures in the first month.
- Send the five questions formally to all party leaders via registered mail.
- Brief friendly journalists on background.
- Place the first op-ed (from the most credible, least controversial voice, probably an economist or housing advocate).
- Begin building the supporter email list.
- Commission the first poll.

**Key milestone:** First 10,000 petition signatures. Formal letter to party leaders. First media coverage.

### Phase 2: Escalation (May-June 2026)

**May 2026: Go Public**
- Hold a formal launch event (Wellington, Parliament grounds). Announce the campaign, the five questions, the scorecard, and the petition progress.
- Release the first commissioned poll showing public support for parties releasing immigration plans.
- Publish the first version of the party scorecard. NZ First likely green, all others red.
- Begin the op-ed rotation (one every two weeks).
- Host the first town hall event in a high-profile electorate (probably Auckland).
- Release the first electorate-level data reports.

**June 2026: Build Pressure**
- Announce the Plan for Growth Alliance with endorsing organisations.
- 25,000 petition signature milestone. Run full-page newspaper advertisements.
- Release second poll. Track changes in immigration salience.
- Hold 3-4 more town hall events across different regions.
- First OIA responses start arriving. Release newsworthy findings.
- Begin paid social media advertising in target electorates.
- Pitch long-form interviews and podcast appearances.

**Key milestone:** 25,000 signatures. Full-page ads. Alliance launch. Multiple media cycles.

### Phase 3: Confrontation (July-August 2026)

This is where the campaign becomes harder to ignore. Parties will have held their annual conferences (typically mid-year), and the election will be 3-4 months away.

**July 2026: Party Conference Season**
- Attend party conferences as observers. Ask immigration questions from the floor.
- If a party releases policy at its conference, update the scorecard publicly and praise them. Positive reinforcement matters.
- If a party avoids immigration at its conference, release a statement calling it out.
- Release the international comparison report ("Australia has a plan. Canada has a plan. NZ doesn't.").
- 50,000 petition signatures. Delegation to Parliament. Media event at the Beehive.
- Commission third poll with electorate-level breakdowns.

**August 2026: Peak Pressure**
- Launch targeted advertising in electorates of senior party figures who haven't released policy. "[Minister's name] still hasn't answered. Ask them why."
- Hold town halls in remaining target electorates. Aim for 15+ total by this point.
- Release the most newsworthy OIA findings (save the best for this phase).
- Begin asking the coalition question publicly: "Will you go into coalition with a party that has no immigration plan?"
- Celebrity/public figure endorsements if available.
- Media blitz: multiple op-eds, podcast appearances, data releases in rapid succession.

**Key milestone:** 50,000 signatures. Parliament delegation. Targeted advertising. Coalition question live.

### Phase 4: Election Sprint (September-November 2026)

**September 2026: Final Push**
- Release the final, comprehensive party scorecard. Maximum media push.
- If the 100,000 signature threshold is reached, this is a major media moment.
- Release a "voter guide" based on the scorecard for every electorate.
- Hold a major public event or debate focused on immigration, inviting all party spokespeople.
- Final poll release showing immigration as a top-three voter issue (if the campaign has succeeded).
- Intensify social media advertising.
- Send electorate-specific action alerts to the full supporter list.

**October 2026: Election Week(s)**
- Final scorecard update.
- "Have your say" messaging encouraging supporters to consider immigration policy in their vote.
- The campaign does NOT endorse any party. It simply presents the scorecard and lets voters decide.
- Live social media coverage on election night tracking results in the context of immigration policy.

**Post-Election:**
- Release a post-election analysis: did immigration policy matter? How did parties that engaged perform vs those that didn't?
- If parties formed a government, hold them to their stated policies. The campaign infrastructure becomes a permanent accountability tool.

**Key milestone:** Final scorecard. Voter guide. Election night coverage. Post-election accountability.

## BUDGET CONSIDERATIONS

For a small campaign in NZ, the budget ranges are roughly:

- **Shoestring (under $50,000):** Volunteer-driven, free digital tools, organic social media, earned media only. This is viable if the steering group has strong media contacts and the volunteer base is engaged.
- **Competitive ($50,000-$200,000):** Paid polling (2-3 polls at $15,000-$25,000 each), some paid advertising ($20,000-$50,000), a part-time campaign manager, professional design and web development.
- **High-impact ($200,000-$500,000):** Full-time campaign staff (2-3 people), regular polling, significant paid advertising across digital and print, professional video production, town hall events with proper AV and venues.

In NZ, a campaign at the $100,000-$200,000 level can punch well above its weight given the small media market. Much of the code infrastructure described above can be built at minimal cost using free-tier services and Claude Code.

## RISKS AND MITIGATIONS

**Risk: Labelled as racist/xenophobic.**
Mitigation: Diverse leadership, pro-immigrant framing, immediate rejection of extremist support, focus on planning and numbers rather than origins.

**Risk: Co-opted by NZ First.**
Mitigation: The campaign is explicitly non-partisan. It praises any party that releases policy. It does not endorse or preference any party. If NZ First tries to claim ownership, the campaign responds: "We welcome NZ First's policy. Now we're waiting for everyone else."

**Risk: Parties release vague, non-committal policy to get a green rating.**
Mitigation: The scorecard criteria are specific. The five questions require concrete answers (numerical targets, specific visa categories). Vague statements get a yellow rating, not green.

**Risk: Burnout and volunteer fatigue.**
Mitigation: Phase the campaign with clear escalation points. Give volunteers defined, time-limited tasks rather than open-ended commitments. Celebrate milestones publicly.

**Risk: Legal challenges (election advertising rules).**
Mitigation: Register as a third-party promoter with the Electoral Commission if spending exceeds $100,000. Ensure all advertising meets Electoral Act requirements (promoter statements, etc.). Get legal advice early.

## SUCCESS METRICS

The campaign succeeds if, by election day:

1. At least 4 of 6 major parties have published detailed immigration policy (answering all five questions).
2. Immigration is consistently polling as a top-three voter issue.
3. The petition has exceeded 50,000 signatures.
4. The campaign has generated 50+ distinct media stories across major outlets.
5. At least 10 town hall events have been held.
6. The data dashboard and scorecard have been cited by mainstream media and used by voters.

The ultimate success metric is structural: immigration policy becomes a permanent, expected part of every party's platform, not something that can be avoided. If the 2029 election campaign begins with immigration policy already on the table for every party, the campaign has achieved lasting change.
