# Forcing Immigration onto New Zealand's 2026 Election Agenda

## A Multi-Expert Strategic Analysis

---

## 1. PARTY COMPETITION THEORY: Issue Salience in an MMP System

**How issues move from fringe to centre**

The academic literature on issue salience -- particularly the work of Carmines and Stimson on "issue evolution" and Riker's "heresthetics" -- tells us that issues don't move to the centre of political competition gradually. They move in punctuated bursts, usually triggered by a combination of real-world events, entrepreneurial political actors, and a failure of the existing party cartel to contain the topic.

In MMP systems specifically, the mechanism is well-documented. Minor parties act as "issue entrepreneurs." They raise a topic that major parties have tacitly agreed to suppress. If the issue resonates with voters, the minor party gains vote share. At some threshold, major parties face a choice: continue to ignore the issue and lose voters, or respond with their own position. This is the "contagion effect" described by Meguid (2005) in her work on niche party competition. The major party can respond in three ways: adopt a similar position (accommodative), attack the minor party's position (adversarial), or try to dismiss the issue entirely (dismissive). Dismissal only works when salience is low. Once salience crosses a threshold, dismissal becomes electorally costly.

The critical question is: where is that threshold? Research on European MMP and PR systems suggests the tipping point is roughly when 15-20% of voters rank the issue as their top concern AND a credible party is actively campaigning on it. In New Zealand, immigration already meets both conditions. Polling from Taxpayers' Union/Curia and other sources consistently places immigration in the top 3-5 voter concerns. NZ First, polling at nearly 12%, is the credible vehicle.

**Why the major parties are still avoiding it**

The reason National, Labour, and the Greens can still avoid detailed immigration positions is that the issue hasn't yet created a direct electoral penalty for them specifically. National voters who care about immigration can park their party vote with NZ First under MMP, knowing NZ First will be in coalition with National anyway. Labour voters who care about immigration have nowhere obvious to go -- NZ First is culturally alien to most Labour voters. This creates a perverse stability where major parties can free-ride on NZ First's issue ownership.

Breaking this stability requires making the cost of avoidance exceed the cost of engagement. That happens when:

- Immigration becomes so salient that it defines vote choice (not just concern)
- Media frames shift from "immigration as culture war" to "immigration as governance failure"
- Intra-party pressure builds (candidates in marginal electorates demanding the party take a position)
- Coalition dynamics force the issue (NZ First making immigration policy a public precondition for coalition)

**The NZ First leverage point**

NZ First's position is uniquely powerful here. As a coalition partner, it can do something no opposition party can: it can publicly demand policy from within government. Every time Winston Peters or Shane Jones makes a public statement about immigration that is more aggressive than the government's agreed position, it forces National to either agree or visibly disagree. This is the classic "coalition partner as issue entrepreneur" dynamic seen with the Danish People's Party, the Dutch PVV, and the Swiss SVP.

The academic literature calls this "blackmail potential" (Sartori). NZ First's blackmail potential is currently underutilised because the party is being a relatively disciplined coalition partner. The strategic question is whether NZ First -- or external actors who share its immigration concerns -- can increase the pressure without destabilising the coalition.

## 2. SMALL-COUNTRY DYNAMICS: New Zealand's Unique Landscape

**The concentrated media advantage**

New Zealand has roughly 5 million people and a media landscape dominated by a handful of outlets: Stuff, NZ Herald, RNZ, Newshub (now reduced), and a small number of political journalists. There are perhaps 20-30 journalists who regularly cover parliamentary politics. This concentration is a massive advantage for issue-forcing campaigns.

In a large country, you need sustained national campaigns to shift media coverage. In New Zealand, a single well-placed story, a single compelling OIA release, or a single dramatic select committee moment can dominate the news cycle for days. The political media ecosystem is small enough that a well-resourced campaign can effectively "flood the zone" with immigration-related stories, data, and angles.

The podcast and newsletter ecosystem matters too. When The Platform, Kiwiblog, The Working Group, or interest.co.nz run immigration content, it feeds into the mainstream media cycle within 24-48 hours. Political journalists read these outlets. The information loop is tight.

**The small political class**

New Zealand's political class is extraordinarily small. There are 120 MPs. Most political staffers, lobbyists, journalists, and think tank researchers know each other personally. Party conferences are small enough that an outsider with a clear agenda can have meaningful conversations with a dozen MPs in a single weekend. This creates leverage that simply doesn't exist in larger democracies.

In Denmark, the transformation of immigration politics was partly driven by a small network of public intellectuals, journalists, and politicians who knew each other and reinforced each other's messaging. The same dynamic is possible in New Zealand, and the network required is even smaller.

**Lessons from comparable countries**

Denmark is the most instructive case. In the late 1990s, immigration was a taboo topic in Danish politics. The Social Democrats and centre-right parties had an implicit consensus to avoid it. The Danish People's Party (Dansk Folkeparti), founded in 1995, broke this consensus. By 2001, the issue was so salient that the centre-right Liberal Party (Venstre) won the election partly by adopting a harder immigration stance. By 2019, even the Social Democrats had adopted restrictive immigration policies. The entire Overton window shifted over 20 years.

Key lessons from Denmark:
- The shift started with data. Researchers who published credible statistics on integration costs, housing pressure, and demographic projections gave politicians cover to engage.
- Think tanks mattered enormously. CEPOS (centre-right) and later others provided a steady stream of policy-relevant research.
- The framing shifted from "anti-immigrant" to "pro-welfare state." The Social Democrats eventually adopted restriction by arguing that high immigration threatened the welfare model. This is directly transferable to NZ.
- Local government was a key battleground. Mayors and local councils dealing with housing and service pressure became vocal, which gave national politicians permission to engage.

The Netherlands followed a similar path, where Pim Fortuyn and later Geert Wilders forced the issue, and eventually mainstream parties (including Labour, the VVD) adopted more restrictive stances.

Australia's experience is also relevant. The Howard government's successful reframing of immigration control as "sovereignty" and "orderly migration" provided a template that both major parties eventually adopted. In Australia, the shift was aided by the fact that both the Coalition and Labor had internal factions that wanted to engage on the issue.

**Constraints specific to NZ**

The main constraint is the Treaty of Waitangi and the unique role of Maori in NZ politics. Any immigration discussion in NZ immediately intersects with questions about tangata whenua, biculturalism, and the Maori-Crown relationship. This is both a constraint and an opportunity, discussed further in the framing section.

The other constraint is New Zealand's self-image as a welcoming, tolerant country. This is deeply embedded in national identity. Campaigns that appear to attack this self-image will face strong resistance. The framing must work with this identity, not against it.

## 3. PRACTICAL TACTICS: Forcing Policy Positions

**Parliamentary mechanisms**

Written parliamentary questions are the single most underrated tool for forcing transparency on immigration. Any MP can submit written questions requiring a ministerial response. A sustained campaign of detailed written questions -- on visa numbers by category, overstayer statistics, infrastructure spending per capita in high-growth areas, health system capacity relative to population growth, housing consents versus population growth -- creates two effects. First, the answers themselves generate newsworthy data. Second, the volume of questions signals to media that immigration is a live political issue.

NZ First MPs are best placed to do this, but ACT MPs sympathetic to immigration reform could also be recruited. The key is volume and specificity. Don't ask "what is the government's immigration policy?" Ask "how many residence visas were granted in the Auckland region in 2025, broken down by category, and what was the corresponding increase in primary school enrolments in the same period?"

Select committee submissions are another lever. When any bill touches on population, housing, infrastructure, health, or education, submissions can be made that explicitly connect the issue to immigration-driven population growth. This forces committee members to engage with the immigration dimension even when the bill is ostensibly about something else. The Health Select Committee considering hospital capacity? Submit evidence on population growth projections. The Transport Select Committee considering road funding? Submit evidence on per-capita infrastructure spending relative to population growth rates.

OIA (Official Information Act) requests are the third parliamentary-adjacent tool. Targeted OIA requests to Immigration New Zealand, MBIE, Treasury, and the Ministry of Housing can surface data that is not routinely published. Response times are legally mandated (20 working days), and refusals can be challenged through the Ombudsman. A systematic OIA campaign -- say, 5-10 carefully crafted requests per month -- can generate a steady stream of newsworthy data points.

**Think tank and research infrastructure**

New Zealand's think tank landscape is thin. The NZ Initiative leans centre-right and is generally pro-immigration (aligned with business interests). There is no equivalent of Denmark's CEPOS or Australia's Centre for Independent Studies that has taken a serious analytical position on immigration levels and their effects. This is a gap that could be filled.

Commissioning independent research is high-leverage. A single well-designed study on the fiscal impact of immigration in New Zealand -- similar to the work done by the Migration Advisory Committee in the UK or the Productivity Commission in Australia -- would become a reference point for all subsequent debate. The research needs to be credible, peer-reviewable, and ideally conducted by someone with academic standing. It doesn't need to reach anti-immigration conclusions. It just needs to take the question seriously and produce data. Data forces engagement.

The New Zealand Productivity Commission (now part of the NZ Infrastructure Commission, Te Waihanga) has touched on immigration-related issues in its work on housing and urban planning. A formal request for a Productivity Commission inquiry into the economic and fiscal effects of immigration would be powerful. If the government refuses, the refusal itself is newsworthy.

**Candidate and electorate-level pressure**

In MMP, electorate candidates in high-growth areas (South Auckland, Hamilton, Tauranga, Christchurch) face direct constituent pressure on housing, infrastructure, and services. Identifying candidates in these electorates -- across all parties -- and providing them with localised data on immigration-driven growth creates bottom-up pressure on party leadership.

For Labour especially, the pressure point is working-class electorates in South Auckland and provincial cities where housing costs and service pressure are most acute. If Labour electorate candidates are publicly asked "what is your party's position on immigration levels?" and cannot answer, that becomes a story.

**Private polling**

Commissioning private polling is expensive but high-impact. A well-designed poll that asks not just "is immigration important to you?" but "would you be more or less likely to vote for a party that proposed reducing net migration to X?" gives precise data on electoral incentives. If that polling is then leaked or published, it forces party strategists to respond.

In Australia, the role of private polling by the Australian Population Research Institute (TAPRI) was significant in shifting both major parties. Their polls consistently showed majority support for lower immigration, which gave politicians cover to engage.

**Coalition negotiation leverage**

Looking ahead to post-election 2026 coalition negotiations, immigration policy can be positioned as a non-negotiable coalition bottom line. If NZ First signals publicly -- well before the election -- that any coalition agreement must include specific immigration targets and mechanisms, it forces every other party to either develop a position or explain why they refuse to.

This is exactly what the Danish People's Party did. They published their coalition demands on immigration before elections, which forced the mainstream parties to engage with specific proposals rather than vague platitudes.

## 4. FRAMING AND NARRATIVE

**The core framing challenge**

The central problem is that immigration discussion in New Zealand is trapped in a binary: you're either "pro-immigration" (open, tolerant, modern) or "anti-immigration" (racist, xenophobic, backward). This binary serves the interests of parties that want to avoid the topic, because anyone who raises concerns can be dismissed as belonging to the second category.

Breaking this binary requires a third frame. The most successful third frames in comparable countries have been:

**"Population policy, not immigration policy."** This is the single most powerful reframe available. New Zealand does not have a population policy. It has no official target or range for population growth. It has no mechanism for assessing whether infrastructure, housing, and services can absorb a given level of population increase. Framing the demand as "New Zealand needs a population policy" transforms the conversation from "do you like immigrants?" to "should we plan for growth?" This is the frame used successfully by Dick Smith in Australia and by the Sustainable Australia Party. It's almost impossible to argue against planning.

**"Managed migration, not mass migration."** This frame positions the speaker as pro-immigration in principle but demanding competence in execution. It implicitly criticises the current system as chaotic and unplanned without requiring the speaker to say anything negative about immigrants themselves. This was the dominant frame used by the Brexit Leave campaign's more moderate voices, and it resonates because it aligns with a broadly shared value: competence in governance.

**"Workers vs. wages."** This is the frame that gives Labour and the left permission to engage. The argument is straightforward: high immigration suppresses wages for the lowest-paid workers, who are disproportionately Maori, Pasifika, and existing migrants. This is the frame that allowed the Australian Labor Party to tighten immigration policy. It's supported by economic research (Borjas in the US context, the MAC in the UK context) and it aligns with traditional left-wing values of worker protection.

**"Infrastructure-first."** This frame says: we're not against people coming to New Zealand, but we need to build the hospitals, roads, schools, and houses before we invite more people. It's the frame of responsible governance. It has the advantage of being concretely demonstrable -- you can point to specific hospital waiting times, specific housing shortages, specific traffic congestion.

**Neutralising the "racist" counter-frame**

This is where Maori voices are strategically critical. Shane Jones has been the most important single figure in legitimising immigration discussion in New Zealand precisely because he is Maori, from Northland, and speaks with the authority of someone whose people have the most to say about who comes to these islands.

Every time Shane Jones talks about immigration, it becomes harder to dismiss immigration concern as "white racism." This is not cynical -- Jones's concerns are genuine and grounded in the material interests of his constituents. But strategically, Maori voices on immigration are extraordinarily powerful because they neutralise the primary weapon used to shut down discussion.

Te Pati Maori's own position on immigration is potentially interesting here. Their kaupapa is grounded in tino rangatiratanga (self-determination) and mana motuhake (autonomy). There is a intellectually coherent position -- which some Maori academics have articulated -- that the Treaty gives Maori a unique standing in decisions about who settles in Aotearoa. If Te Pati Maori were to engage seriously with immigration policy from a Treaty perspective, it would fundamentally reshape the debate.

Other neutralisation strategies:

- **Migrant voices.** Finding established migrants who support managed migration is powerful. "I came here 20 years ago, and I support having a population plan" is nearly impossible to dismiss as racist.
- **International comparisons.** Canada, Australia, and even progressive Scandinavian countries all have explicit immigration targets and regular reviews. Framing NZ's lack of a population policy as an outlier -- as less sophisticated than comparable countries -- appeals to NZ's desire to benchmark well internationally.
- **Data over anecdote.** Every claim should be backed by specific, verifiable data. This makes it harder to dismiss the argument as emotional or prejudiced.

**The narrative arc**

The overall narrative should follow this structure:

1. New Zealand is experiencing the fastest population growth in the developed world (relative to its infrastructure base)
2. This growth is almost entirely immigration-driven
3. No one voted for this level of growth -- there was no democratic mandate
4. The consequences are visible: housing costs, hospital waiting lists, infrastructure strain
5. Other comparable countries manage their population growth deliberately
6. New Zealand deserves a proper population policy, debated democratically, with targets set transparently
7. Every party should tell voters what population they're planning for

This narrative is positive (planning, democracy, transparency), concrete (specific consequences), and comparative (other countries do better). It avoids negativity toward immigrants and instead directs criticism at the system and the politicians who refuse to engage.

## 5. DATA AND INTELLIGENCE SYSTEMS

**The political intelligence stack**

A serious issue-forcing campaign should be supported by a purpose-built intelligence system. The good news is that New Zealand's small size and relatively open data environment make this feasible with modest resources. Much of this can be automated using code and Claude Code.

**Media monitoring system.** Track every mention of immigration, population, migration, visa, and related terms across NZ media. Sources to monitor: Stuff, NZ Herald, RNZ, Newsroom, The Post, interest.co.nz, Kiwiblog, The Platform, The Working Group, and political Twitter/X. This can be built with RSS feeds, web scraping, and API access where available. Claude Code can process scraped articles, classify them by frame (positive/negative/neutral toward immigration, policy-focused vs. culture-war-focused), and generate weekly reports on coverage volume, framing trends, and which journalists are covering the topic.

A practical implementation: a Python script that scrapes headlines and article text daily, stores them in a SQLite database, and uses Claude's API to classify each article's framing and extract key claims. Weekly automated reports show trends in coverage volume, dominant frames, and notable shifts.

**Parliamentary tracking system.** New Zealand's parliamentary data is publicly available through the Parliament website. Hansard transcripts, written question responses, select committee submissions, and order papers are all accessible. A system that monitors all parliamentary activity for immigration-related content would provide early warning of policy shifts and identify which MPs are engaging with the topic.

This can be automated: scrape the parliamentary questions database weekly, filter for immigration-related questions and answers, track which MPs are asking and which ministers are responding, and flag any new data points that emerge from ministerial responses.

**Party position database.** Build and maintain a comprehensive database of every public statement by every party and every MP on immigration. Include speeches, press releases, social media posts, select committee contributions, and media interviews. This database serves two purposes: it provides ammunition for holding parties accountable ("in March 2025, your spokesperson said X, but your policy says Y"), and it maps the internal diversity of positions within each party.

Claude Code can be used to build a structured database from unstructured text. Feed in press releases and speeches, extract positions on specific immigration sub-topics (total numbers, skill requirements, regional dispersal, refugee quota, family reunification, student visas, work visas), and output a structured party-by-party comparison.

**Polling and sentiment tracking.** While commissioning original polling is expensive, publicly available polling data can be systematically tracked. Roy Morgan, Curia/Taxpayers' Union, 1News/Verian, and Newshub/Reid Research all publish regular political polls that sometimes include issue salience data. Track all available data points on immigration sentiment over time.

Social media sentiment can supplement formal polling. Tracking immigration-related discussion on NZ Twitter/X, Reddit (r/newzealand), and Facebook groups provides a rough real-time sentiment indicator. This is noisy data but useful for detecting emerging narratives and local flashpoints.

**Electorate-level mapping.** Using Census data, Stats NZ population estimates, and electoral roll data, build electorate-level profiles of immigration exposure. Which electorates have seen the fastest population growth? Which have the highest proportion of recent migrants? Which have the longest hospital waiting lists or the highest house price-to-income ratios? Cross-reference this with electoral data to identify electorates where immigration is most likely to be electorally salient.

This is a data analysis project that can be built in Python using publicly available datasets from Stats NZ and the Electoral Commission. The output is a ranked list of electorates by "immigration salience potential" which tells you where electorate-level campaigns will have the most impact.

**Automation with Claude Code**

Claude Code is well-suited for several specific tasks in this infrastructure:

- Processing and classifying large volumes of text (media articles, parliamentary transcripts, party statements)
- Generating structured comparisons from unstructured sources (e.g., "compare Labour's and National's stated positions on skilled migration")
- Drafting OIA requests, parliamentary questions, and select committee submissions based on identified data gaps
- Building and maintaining the databases described above
- Generating regular briefing documents that synthesise the latest data across all tracked sources
- Analysing newly released government data (e.g., monthly migration statistics from Stats NZ) and automatically identifying newsworthy trends

The technical stack would be: Python for scraping and data processing, SQLite or PostgreSQL for storage, Claude API for text analysis and classification, and a simple web dashboard (or even a regularly generated PDF report) for output. This is a project a single competent developer could build and maintain.

## 6. THE PLAYBOOK: Ten Highest-Leverage Actions for the Next Six Months

What follows are ten actions ranked by their likely impact on forcing immigration policy onto every party's agenda before the 2026 election campaign begins in earnest.

**Action 1: Commission a credible fiscal impact study.**

Find an economist with academic credentials -- ideally at a New Zealand university -- to conduct a rigorous study of the net fiscal impact of recent immigration to New Zealand, broken down by visa category. Model it on the UK Migration Advisory Committee's work or the Australian Productivity Commission's reports. This study doesn't need to reach dramatic conclusions. It just needs to exist, to be credible, and to be publicly available. Budget: $50,000-$100,000 for a serious piece of work. Timeline: commission now, publish in 3-4 months. Once published, it becomes the reference point that every journalist, politician, and commentator cites. It forces engagement because ignoring a credible academic study is harder than ignoring a social media campaign.

**Action 2: Launch a sustained OIA and parliamentary question campaign.**

Draft 50-100 specific, data-focused OIA requests targeting Immigration NZ, MBIE, Treasury, the Ministry of Health, the Ministry of Education, and local councils in high-growth areas. In parallel, work with sympathetic MPs (NZ First, ACT, and any Labour MPs from high-growth electorates) to table a regular stream of written parliamentary questions on immigration data. The goal is to generate a steady drip of newsworthy data points -- one or two per week -- that keep immigration in the news cycle. Each data point should be packaged with a brief, quotable analysis and offered to journalists.

**Action 3: Build the political intelligence system.**

Build the media monitoring, parliamentary tracking, and party position database described in Section 5. This is the infrastructure that makes everything else more effective. Without systematic tracking, you're operating blind. With it, you can identify emerging opportunities, track the effectiveness of your campaign, and hold parties accountable with precision. A competent developer using Claude Code and Python could have a minimum viable version running within 4-6 weeks.

**Action 4: Establish the "population policy" frame.**

Every public communication -- every op-ed, every social media post, every select committee submission, every conversation with a journalist -- should use the phrase "population policy." The goal is to make "does New Zealand need a population policy?" the question that journalists ask politicians, rather than "are you anti-immigration?" This reframe is the single most important narrative task. It should be tested with focus groups if budget allows, but the international evidence strongly suggests it works. Commission 2-3 op-eds from credible voices (an urban planner, an economist, a former public servant) arguing for a population policy framework. Place them in Stuff, the Herald, and Newsroom over a 6-week period.

**Action 5: Create an electorate-level pressure campaign in 5-10 target electorates.**

Identify the 5-10 electorates where immigration-driven population growth is most acutely felt -- likely including several South Auckland electorates, Hamilton, Tauranga, and parts of Canterbury. In each, compile a localised briefing showing population growth, housing costs, school roll growth, and health service pressure. Deliver this briefing to the sitting MP (regardless of party), the likely candidates for 2026, and local media. Ask each candidate publicly: "What population is your party planning for in this electorate by 2030?" Their inability to answer is the story.

**Action 6: Push for a Productivity Commission inquiry.**

Formally request that the government task the relevant commission body with an inquiry into the economic and social effects of immigration and population growth in New Zealand. Build support for this request from business groups, local government, unions, and community organisations. If the government agrees, the inquiry itself forces every party and stakeholder to engage. If the government refuses, the refusal is newsworthy and becomes evidence of deliberate avoidance.

**Action 7: Recruit and amplify diverse voices.**

Actively recruit spokespeople who are Maori, Pasifika, and established migrants to publicly support a population policy. Shane Jones is already in this space but his voice alone isn't enough. Find a Pasifika community leader in South Auckland who can speak to housing pressure. Find a first-generation migrant who supports managed migration. Find a Maori academic who can articulate the Treaty dimension. Each additional voice makes the "racist" counter-frame harder to deploy. These voices should be given platforms -- op-eds, podcast appearances, select committee submissions -- and their contributions should be amplified through the media monitoring system.

**Action 8: Make coalition demands public.**

Work with NZ First to publicly articulate specific immigration policy demands for any post-2026 coalition agreement. These should be concrete and measurable: a net migration target range, a requirement for infrastructure impact assessments before visa category changes, a regular population policy statement to Parliament. Publishing these demands 12 months before the election forces every other party to respond. The media will ask National, Labour, ACT, and the Greens: "Will you agree to these terms?" Their answers -- or their refusal to answer -- shape the campaign.

**Action 9: Engage local government.**

Mayors and councils in high-growth areas are under direct pressure from immigration-driven population growth. They need to build infrastructure but have limited funding and no control over immigration settings. This creates natural allies. Approach mayors in Auckland, Hamilton, Tauranga, and Christchurch and encourage them to publicly call for central government consultation on population planning. When a mayor says "we can't build houses and schools fast enough for the population growth Wellington is imposing on us," that's a powerful, apolitical, credible voice demanding change. Local Government NZ (LGNZ) as an umbrella body could be engaged to adopt a position on population policy coordination.

**Action 10: Create a public-facing immigration policy tracker.**

Build a simple, well-designed website that tracks every party's immigration policy positions (or lack thereof) in real time. For each party, show: stated position on net migration targets (if any), stated position on visa categories, stated position on population planning, and recent statements by party leaders and spokespeople. Where a party has no position, show that explicitly -- a blank space is more powerful than any criticism. Update it weekly. Promote it to journalists as a reference resource. This tracker creates ongoing accountability pressure and gives journalists an easy angle: "Party X is the only major party with no stated immigration policy."

## Synthesis and Sequencing

The ten actions above are mutually reinforcing. The intelligence system (Action 3) supports everything else. The fiscal impact study (Action 1) provides ammunition for the OIA campaign (Action 2), the electorate pressure (Action 5), and the framing work (Action 4). The diverse voices (Action 7) make the framing credible. The policy tracker (Action 10) creates ongoing accountability. The coalition demands (Action 8) force responses that feed back into the tracker.

The sequencing should be:

**Month 1-2 (April-May 2026):** Build the intelligence system. Commission the fiscal study. Begin the OIA and parliamentary question campaign. Draft and place the first op-eds establishing the "population policy" frame.

**Month 2-3 (May-June 2026):** Launch the electorate-level briefings. Recruit diverse voices. Begin local government engagement. Submit the Productivity Commission inquiry request.

**Month 3-4 (June-July 2026):** Publish the fiscal impact study. Launch the policy tracker website. Amplify the study through all channels. NZ First begins signalling coalition demands.

**Month 4-6 (July-September 2026):** Sustain pressure across all channels. The OIA data drip continues. Electorate candidates face regular public questioning. The policy tracker shows which parties are engaging and which are hiding. Media coverage should be self-sustaining by this point, with journalists independently pursuing immigration angles because the infrastructure of data, sources, and frames has been built for them.

The goal by September 2026 is that every party has been forced to either release a detailed immigration and population policy, or visibly refuse to do so. Either outcome serves the broader goal: immigration becomes an unavoidable election issue, and voters can make informed choices about which party's approach they prefer.

## A Note on Risks

This strategy carries real risks that should be acknowledged honestly. Immigration debates can become ugly. They can be exploited by genuinely xenophobic actors. They can cause real harm to migrant communities who feel targeted. Any campaign that forces immigration onto the agenda has a moral obligation to maintain the distinction between policy criticism and people criticism, to amplify diverse voices, and to refuse to engage with or platform genuinely hateful actors.

The strongest safeguard is the framing itself. A campaign centred on "population policy" and "planning" is structurally different from a campaign centred on "too many immigrants." The former invites technocratic engagement. The latter invites scapegoating. Maintaining this distinction requires discipline and constant vigilance, but the international evidence shows it is possible. Denmark's immigration debate has been robust and sometimes harsh, but it has also produced genuine policy innovation and democratic engagement with a question that voters care deeply about. New Zealand deserves the same.
