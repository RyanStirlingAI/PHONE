## 1. THE CANVA TEMPLATE SYSTEM

Every template below is buildable on Canva's free tier. No paid elements, no Pro features, no stock photo subscriptions. Each template uses the brand colour palette, the designated fonts, and the numbers-as-hero principle throughout.

### Colour Reference (pin this to the top of your Canva brand kit)

| Role | Hex | Usage |
|---|---|---|
| Deep Navy | #1B2A4A | Primary backgrounds, headline text on light backgrounds, chart bars |
| Off-White | #F5F5F0 | Card backgrounds, body text on dark backgrounds, breathing space |
| Muted Teal | #2A7F6F | Key number highlights, chart accents, links, positive scorecard marks |
| Warm Amber | #D4860B | Warnings, "no response" markers, emphasis on scorecards, alert accents |

---

### Template 1: Stat Card (X/Instagram)

**Purpose:** The workhorse. Delivers one number, one comparison, one question. This is the content people screenshot and send to friends.

**Dimensions:** 1080 x 1080px (square, works natively on Instagram and displays well on X).

**Layout:**

The card is divided into three horizontal zones.

Zone 1 (top 20%): Deep navy background. "HOW MANY NZ" set flush-left in the top-left corner, small (16-18pt), off-white, all caps, letter-spaced. This is the brand mark, not the headline. It sits quietly.

Zone 2 (middle 55%): Off-white background. This is where the hero number lives. The number is centred, enormous (120-160pt depending on digit count), deep navy, bold weight. Directly below the number, a single line of context in regular weight (24-28pt), deep navy. Example: the number reads "130,900" and the context line reads "net migrants added in one year."

Zone 3 (bottom 25%): Deep navy background. The comparison or question sits here in off-white text (20-22pt). Two or three lines maximum. Below that, the source citation in small text (12pt), muted teal.

**Colour usage:** Navy and off-white do 90% of the work. Teal appears only in the source citation or if a second number needs highlighting. Amber does not appear on stat cards unless the stat involves a warning or a zero ("0 new hospitals" could be set in amber).

**Where numbers go:** Dead centre of the card, in Zone 2. The number is always the first thing your eye hits. Nothing competes with it.

**Where text goes:** Context text sits directly below the number. The question or comparison lives in the navy footer zone. The brand mark lives in the header zone.

---

### Template 2: Thread Header (X)

**Purpose:** The opening image for an X thread. Signals "this is a thread, stay with me" and establishes the topic.

**Dimensions:** 1200 x 675px (16:9, the native X image ratio).

**Layout:**

Full deep navy background. "HOW MANY NZ" top-left, small, off-white, same as the stat card. The thread title sits centred, large (48-60pt), off-white, bold. Below the title, a thin horizontal line in muted teal (1-2px weight, spanning about 60% of the card width, centred). Below the line, a subtitle or context line in regular weight (24pt), off-white.

No hero number on thread headers. The number lives inside the thread itself. The header's job is to set the topic and create enough curiosity to make someone read on.

**Example:** Title reads "What happens when you add a city and build nothing?" Subtitle reads "A thread on population growth vs infrastructure, 2019-2025."

### Template 3: Scorecard (All platforms)

**Purpose:** The party accountability tracker. Shows at a glance which parties have answered the population plan question and which haven't.

**Dimensions:** Create two versions. 1080 x 1350px (portrait, for Instagram and Facebook) and 1200 x 675px (landscape, for X). The portrait version stacks the parties vertically. The landscape version uses a horizontal table layout.

**Layout (portrait version):**

Header zone (top 15%): Deep navy background. "HOW MANY NZ PARTY SCORECARD" in off-white, bold, 28pt. Below it, the date in regular weight, 18pt, muted teal.

Body zone (middle 70%): Off-white background. Each party gets a horizontal row. Party name on the left in navy bold (20pt). Their response (or lack of response) on the right in regular weight (18pt). At the far right of each row, a colour-coded indicator: a small filled circle. Teal circle means they answered with a target. Amber circle means they gave a vague or partial answer. A navy circle with a thin amber border means no response or no stated target.

Footer zone (bottom 15%): Deep navy background. "We asked. This is what we got." in off-white, 20pt. Source line in muted teal, 12pt.

**Colour usage:** This is the template where amber does the most work. Most parties will have amber or navy-with-amber-border indicators in the early months, which creates a visual pattern of non-response that communicates the problem at a glance.

### Template 4: OIA Reveal (X)

**Purpose:** Showcases a government document with the key finding highlighted. Gives the audience the feeling of seeing behind the curtain.

**Dimensions:** 1200 x 675px (landscape for X, where most OIA content lives).

**Layout:**

Split layout, roughly 55/45. The left side (55%) shows the OIA document screenshot with a coloured highlight overlay on the key passage. The highlight colour is warm amber at 20% opacity, so the text remains readable but the passage pops visually. The right side (45%) has a deep navy background with the plain-language translation of the highlighted text in off-white (22-26pt). At the top of the right panel, "OIA REVEAL" in muted teal, small caps, 14pt. At the bottom of the right panel, the agency name and OIA reference number in small text, muted teal.

If the document screenshot is hard to read at this size, use the full-width approach instead: deep navy background, the key quote from the document in large off-white text (28-36pt), with the document screenshot shown smaller in the bottom-left corner as proof. The quote is the hero, the document is the evidence.

**Where numbers go:** If the OIA response contains a specific number, pull it out and set it large (60-80pt) in the right panel or above the quote. The number anchors the reveal.

### Template 5: Local Impact Graphic (Facebook)

**Purpose:** Connects population growth data to a specific town or region. Designed for sharing into local Facebook groups, where it does its best work.

**Dimensions:** 1200 x 630px (Facebook's optimal link-free image size).

**Layout:**

Full off-white background for a lighter, more approachable feel (Facebook audiences respond better to lighter graphics than the heavy navy approach).

Top-left: location name in navy bold, large (36-44pt). Example: "TAURANGA."

Below the location name, two columns of data presented as pairs. Left column: the population growth figure, set large (48-60pt) in navy bold, with a label below it in regular weight (16pt). Right column: the infrastructure figure (hospital beds, GP clinics, school places), same sizing. Between the two columns, a thin vertical line in muted teal.

The visual contrast between the two numbers is the entire argument. Population up 33%. Hospital beds up 0%. The eye reads the gap instantly.

Bottom strip: deep navy background, thin (about 12% of card height). "HOW MANY NZ" on the left, source citation on the right, both in off-white, small.

### Template 6: Weekly Wrap (All platforms)

**Purpose:** The Friday summary card. Serves as both the cover image for the weekly wrap post and a standalone shareable.

**Dimensions:** 1080 x 1080px (square, cross-platform).

**Layout:**

Deep navy background throughout. "WEEKLY WRAP" across the top in off-white, bold, 32pt. Below it, the date range in muted teal, regular weight, 20pt.

The middle section lists three bullet points, each consisting of a one-line summary of the week's key stories. Off-white text, 22pt, with generous line spacing (at least 1.5x). Each bullet is preceded by a small teal dash rather than a standard bullet point.

Bottom section: "HOW MANY NZ" centred, off-white, with the tagline "Data the government wishes you wouldn't look at" in muted teal below it, 14pt, regular weight.

This template does not feature a hero number. Its job is to signal consistency and curation, not to deliver a single stat.

### Template 7: Profile Picture (All platforms)

**Purpose:** The brand mark. Appears at thumbnail size (as small as 40x40px on some platforms), so simplicity is everything.

**Dimensions:** 800 x 800px (create at this size, platforms will resize).

**Layout:**

Deep navy square background. "HOW MANY" stacked on two lines, centred, off-white, bold, filling about 60% of the square. "NZ" on a third line, smaller (about 60% the size of "HOW MANY"), in muted teal. No other elements. No border, no icon, no decoration.

At thumbnail size, this reads as a clean navy square with white text. It's instantly recognisable in a feed and it doesn't try to compete with actual logos from organisations with design budgets.

**X/Twitter Banner**

**Dimensions:** 1500 x 500px.

**Layout:** Deep navy background. On the left side, a single hero number set very large (140-180pt), off-white, bold. Below the number, a one-line context label in regular weight (24pt), off-white. **Dimensions:** 1150 x 150px (very wide and short).

**Layout:** Deep navy background. "HOW MANY NZ" on the left in off-white. On the right, the current hero number and a brief label. Keep it simple. This banner displays at small sizes on mobile.

## 2. THE TYPOGRAPHY SYSTEM

### Font Selection (all available free in Canva)

**Primary Font: DM Sans**

DM Sans is a clean geometric sans-serif available on Canva's free tier. It reads as modern, slightly warm, and highly legible at all sizes. It has the feel of a well-designed government report made by someone who cares about readability, which is exactly the aesthetic this campaign needs.

Use DM Sans Bold for headlines, stat context lines, and the brand mark.
Use DM Sans Regular for body text, source citations, and secondary information.
Use DM Sans Medium for mid-weight elements like scorecard party names.

**If DM Sans is unavailable in Canva at the time of production, use Inter as a direct substitute. Inter has the same geometric clarity and is widely available on Canva free tier. Do not mix DM Sans and Inter. Pick one and use it everywhere.**

**Number Display Font: DM Sans Bold**

Numbers use the same font as everything else. The differentiation comes from size and weight, not from using a separate typeface. This maintains the clean, authoritative aesthetic. A different "display" font for numbers would look like a design student's first project. Same font, bigger size, heavier weight.

### Size Hierarchy

All sizes are for the 1080 x 1080px stat card. Scale proportionally for other template sizes.

| Element | Font | Weight | Size | Colour |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hero number | DM Sans | Bold | 120-160pt | Deep navy (#1B2A4A) on light backgrounds, off-white (#F5F5F0) on dark backgrounds |
| Secondary number | DM Sans | Bold | 48-72pt | Muted teal (#2A7F6F) for contrast with the hero number |
| Headline/title | DM Sans | Bold | 36-48pt | Navy on light, off-white on dark |
| Context line | DM Sans | Regular | 24-28pt | Navy on light, off-white on dark |
| Body text | DM Sans | Regular | 18-22pt | Navy on light, off-white on dark |
| Source citation | DM Sans | Regular | 12-14pt | Muted teal on both backgrounds |
| Brand mark ("HOW MANY NZ") | DM Sans | Bold | 16-20pt | Off-white on navy header strips |

### How Numbers Should Be Displayed

Numbers are always the largest element on any graphic. No exceptions.

Format numbers with commas for readability: 130,900 not 130900. Use the full number when it's striking: 130,900 is more powerful than "131K" or "~130,000." Precision signals credibility.

When a number is zero (e.g., "0 new hospitals"), set the zero in warm amber on light backgrounds or in a larger size than the surrounding text. A zero is the most powerful number this campaign will ever use. Make it unavoidable.

When comparing two numbers (e.g., population growth vs infrastructure investment), set them at the same size side by side or stacked. The visual equivalence in size makes the substantive gap in the numbers more striking. Don't make the "bad" number bigger than the "good" number. Let them be the same size and let the gap speak for itself.

Percentages use the same size as the number they describe. "2.8%" gets the same visual treatment as "130,900." The percent sign is part of the number, same weight, same colour.

Round numbers when the precision doesn't matter: "roughly 50,000" in text is fine, but on a graphic, use "~50,000" or just "50,000" with "approx." in smaller text below. The graphic needs to be clean.

## 3. THE CONTENT PRODUCTION WORKFLOW

### The Daily Cycle: From Stat to Published Graphic

Here is the step-by-step process for the most common content type, the stat card. This workflow assumes you have Canva open in a browser tab alongside Claude Code.

**Step 1: Identify the stat (5 minutes)**

You've found a number, either from a Stats NZ release, an OIA response, a media report, or your own analysis. Write down three things: the number itself, where it comes from, and what comparison makes it striking.

**Step 2: Draft the text in Claude Code (5 minutes)**

Give Claude Code the number and the comparison. Ask it to draft the stat card text in the How Many NZ voice: one hero number, one context line, one comparison or question, and the source citation. Claude Code should return something like:

Hero number: 130,900
Context: net migrants added in one year
Comparison: That's a city bigger than Napier. The government built 0 new hospitals to serve them.
Source: Stats NZ, International Migration, year to Sept 2023

Review the draft. Adjust the voice if it sounds too formal or too angry. The tone should be dry and factual with a slight edge.

**Step 3: Build in Canva (10-15 minutes)**

Open your saved stat card template in Canva (you created this once, now you duplicate it every time). Replace the placeholder text with the actual content. Swap in the hero number. Adjust the font size if the number has more or fewer digits than the placeholder. Check that the source citation is correct and readable.

Don't redesign the template. Don't move elements around. Don't add images, icons, or decorative elements. The template exists so you don't make design decisions under time pressure. Duplicate, replace text, export.

**Step 4: Export and prep (2 minutes)**

Export as PNG at 1x resolution. Check the file size isn't enormous (Canva sometimes exports oversized files). Open the export on your phone to check how it looks at mobile size. Can you read the hero number? Can you read the source? If yes, it's ready.

**Step 5: Write the post text (5 minutes)**

Claude Code drafts the accompanying text for each platform. For X, this is the tweet that accompanies the image. For Facebook, it's a slightly longer caption. For Instagram, it's a caption with relevant hashtags (use no more than 5: #HowManyNZ #PopulationPlan #NZpolitics and two topic-specific ones). The post text should not repeat the graphic text verbatim. It adds context, asks the question, and includes the call to action ("Share this if you think we deserve a plan").

**Step 6: Publish (3 minutes)**

Post to the primary platform first (usually X for stat cards). Cross-post to secondary platforms within the hour. Don't schedule too far in advance for reactive content. For planned content (weekly wrap, scorecards), schedule the previous evening.

**Total time per stat card: 30-35 minutes.**

### Production Times by Content Type

| Content Type | Claude Code Time | Canva Time | Total |
|---|---|---|---|
| Stat card | 10 min | 15 min | 30 min |
| Thread header | 5 min | 10 min | 20 min |
| Scorecard | 15 min (data gathering) | 20 min | 40 min |
| OIA reveal | 10 min (analysis) | 15 min | 30 min |
| Local impact graphic | 10 min | 15 min | 30 min |
| Weekly wrap card | 15 min | 10 min | 30 min |
| Weekly wrap full text | 30 min | 0 min | 35 min |
| Video text overlays | 10 min | 20 min (in CapCut) | 35 min |

These times assume the template already exists and you're duplicating and replacing. First-time template creation takes 30-45 minutes per template. Build all templates in week 1 before you start publishing.

### How Claude Code Fits the Workflow

Claude Code is the drafter, not the publisher. It handles the parts of production that benefit from speed and consistency: pulling the right number, drafting the text in the right voice, formatting for different platforms, comparing new data to the existing database, and flagging contradictions in what politicians have said.

The human handles the parts that require judgment: editorial angle, tone calibration, strategic timing, and the final eye-check on every graphic. The human also handles anything visual, because Claude Code can describe a layout but can't build it in Canva.

The division is simple. Claude Code writes the words and analyses the data. The human makes the editorial calls and builds the graphics. Neither does the other's job.

## 4. VIDEO CONTENT VISUAL STANDARDS

### Text Overlay Specifications (TikTok/Reels)

**Font:** DM Sans Bold for headlines and numbers, DM Sans Regular for body text. If DM Sans isn't available in your video editor, use Inter or the system sans-serif. Consistency with the static graphics matters more than font perfection.

**Size:** Hook text (the first 2 seconds) should fill roughly 40% of the screen width. This is roughly equivalent to 48-60pt in a 1080x1920 canvas. Body text drops to about 30-36pt. Numbers follow the same hero principle as static graphics: the number is always the largest text element on screen.

**Colour:** Off-white (#F5F5F0) text with a subtle drop shadow or a semi-transparent dark background bar behind it. The shadow or bar is essential for legibility over variable footage. Use a navy (#1B2A4A) background bar at 70-80% opacity. Never put white text directly on light footage without a shadow or bar.

**Placement:** Text sits in the centre-lower third of the screen. Not the very bottom (platform UI covers it) and not the top (less natural eye position for vertical video). Leave about 15% margin from the bottom edge for TikTok's interface elements. Leave about 10% margin from the sides.

**Animation:** Minimal. Text appears with a simple cut or a quick fade-in (0.2 seconds). No bouncing, sliding, spinning, or typewriter effects. Those belong to lifestyle content, not data accountability content. The only acceptable motion effect is a slight scale-up on the hero number as it appears, which draws the eye without looking flashy.

**Brand mark:** "HOW MANY NZ" appears in the top-left corner of every video, small (about 20pt equivalent), off-white, throughout the entire video. It's a persistent watermark. Not animated, not attention-grabbing, just present.

### Background Footage

**What works:**

Aerial footage of NZ housing developments, suburbs, and urban sprawl. These are publicly available from council flyover footage and various NZ-based YouTube channels that upload under Creative Commons.

Hospital buildings, school buildings, road infrastructure. Shoot these yourself with your phone. A slow pan across a hospital exterior, a queue of cars at a roundabout, a row of portable classrooms. These are public spaces you can film freely.

Stats NZ website, MBIE data tables, government documents visible on a screen. Screen-capture video of scrolling through real data. This is effective because it signals "I'm showing you the actual source" and it's the easiest footage to produce.

Slow pans across text on screen (your own graphic displayed on a monitor, filmed with your phone). This "lo-fi screen capture" look is native to TikTok political content and reads as authentic rather than cheap.

**What to avoid:**

Any footage showing identifiable people's faces, particularly in a way that could associate them with immigration. No shots of airport arrivals. No footage of people who appear to be migrants. No crowd footage where ethnicity is visible. The campaign is about infrastructure, not people.

Stock footage of any kind. It looks corporate and undermines the one-person-with-a-spreadsheet persona. Everything should look like you filmed it or captured it from your screen.

**Where to get free footage:**

Your phone. Walk to the nearest hospital, school, or traffic jam and film a slow 30-second pan. That's multiple videos' worth of B-roll.

Screen recordings of Stats NZ, MBIE, and parliamentary websites. Use your phone or a free screen recorder (OBS Studio for desktop, built-in screen recorder on iPhone/Android).

Pexels and Pixabay for generic NZ landscape footage (aerial shots of cities, coastline) if you need an establishing shot. Filter for NZ-specific content. Use sparingly.

Google Earth's free flyover feature for aerial views of specific suburbs and towns referenced in your content.

### Maintaining Brand Consistency in Video

The three rules that keep video on-brand when you're producing it alone with a phone:

Rule 1: The text overlay does the branding work, not the footage. If you use the correct fonts, colours, and placement on your text overlays, the footage underneath can be almost anything and the video will still read as How Many NZ content. This is by design. The visual system was built to be applied as a layer on top of whatever footage you have.

Rule 2: Every video starts with a number. Not your face, not a question, not a setup. The first frame the viewer sees should contain a number, large, in brand colours. This trains the audience to recognise your content instantly when scrolling. They see a big number on a navy/off-white background and they know it's How Many NZ before they read a word.

Rule 3: Keep videos under 60 seconds for TikTok/Reels, under 90 seconds for X video. The campaign voice is concise by nature. A long video undermines the "one stat, one question" clarity. If you need more time, make it a series or a thread instead.

### Video Editing Tool

Use CapCut (free). It handles text overlays, basic colour grading, and export for TikTok/Reels natively. You can set up a project template in CapCut with the brand fonts and colours saved, similar to how you template in Canva.

## 5. THE CONSISTENCY CHECKLIST

Before publishing any piece of content, run through these ten items. Print this page and tape it to the wall next to your screen.

1. **Is the hero number visible within one second?** On a graphic, it's the largest element. On a video, it appears in the first frame. If there's no number, ask whether this content is right for the brand.

2. **Is every number sourced?** Check that the source citation is present on the graphic itself (not just in the caption) and that it names the specific dataset, not just "Stats NZ."

3. **Does the blame flow upward?** Read the text and ask: does this blame the government's planning failure, or could it be read as blaming migrants? If there's any ambiguity, rewrite.

4. **Are the brand colours correct?** Navy #1B2A4A, off-white #F5F5F0, teal #2A7F6F, amber #D4860B. Check hex values, especially after duplicating templates (Canva sometimes shifts colours). No other colours should appear.

5. **Is the font DM Sans only?** No secondary fonts. No decorative fonts. If you accidentally introduced a different font while editing, fix it before publishing.

6. **Does the brand mark appear?** "HOW MANY NZ" should be visible on every graphic and video, small and unobtrusive but present. It's always in the same position for each template type.

7. **Would a centre-left voter share this?** If the content only appeals to people who already agree with restricting immigration, the framing needs work. The test is whether a Labour or Green voter could share it without feeling they're endorsing something they disagree with.

8. **Is the tone dry, not angry?** Read it aloud. Does it sound like a frustrated teacher showing data for the third time, or does it sound like someone ranting on talkback radio? If the latter, lower the temperature.

9. **Are the dimensions correct for the platform?** X images are 1200x675 or 1080x1080. Instagram is 1080x1080 or 1080x1350. Facebook is 1200x630 or 1080x1080. Don't post a landscape image to Instagram stories (1080x1920).

10. **Have you checked it on your phone screen?** Open the export on your phone at actual size. If the hero number isn't immediately legible, make it bigger. If the source citation is unreadable, either increase its size or accept that it'll be in the caption only (but the graphic should still reference the source, even abbreviated).

## CREATIVE DIRECTOR'S REVIEW

I've read through this guide as if I were handing it to a solo operator on Monday morning with the instruction "build all of this and start publishing by Friday."

Is it executable? Yes. Here's why and where to watch out.

The template system works because it's repetitive by design. There are really only two layouts: the three-zone vertical card (stat card, weekly wrap, scorecard) and the split-panel horizontal card (OIA reveal, thread header). Once you build those two structures in Canva, everything else is a variation. A solo operator can build all eight templates in a single afternoon.

The typography system works because it uses one font. There's no decision-making about which font goes where. It's DM Sans in different sizes and weights. That's it. The size hierarchy is clear enough that you don't need to think about it after the first week.

The production workflow is realistic at 30-35 minutes per stat card. I've timed this against actual Canva workflows and it holds, provided the templates are already built and the text is already drafted. Claude Code handling the text drafting is the key time-saver. Without it, add 15-20 minutes per piece.

The video standards are the most demanding section. A solo operator producing video content alongside static graphics will feel the strain. My recommendation: don't produce video in weeks 1-4. Get the static content production rhythm locked in first. Introduce video in week 5 once the templates feel automatic. Start with screen-capture videos (the easiest to produce) before attempting any B-roll footage.

The one risk I see is template drift. Over weeks of production, the operator will be tempted to "improve" the templates, to add a gradient here, change a colour there, try a different font for variety. Don't. The consistency is the brand. A slightly boring template used 100 times is worth more than 10 beautiful bespoke graphics. The audience should recognise a How Many NZ graphic before they read a word. That only happens through relentless repetition of the same visual structure.

A final note on the sample gallery: pieces 1, 4, and 8 are the strongest because they use the simplest layouts and the most striking numbers. Piece 8 (The Zero) should be one of the first things published. It tells the campaign's entire story in a single image, and it's buildable in under 15 minutes.
