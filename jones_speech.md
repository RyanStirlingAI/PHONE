# Shane Jones -- Immigration Speech, 2026

**NZ First Public Meeting -- Provincial New Zealand**

---

Tena koutou, tena koutou, tena koutou katoa.

E nga mana, e nga reo, e nga karangatanga maha -- greetings to you all, the good people of this town, the people who get up before the sun does, who work with their hands -- and who know damn well what it's like to live in a place Wellington can't find on a map.

I am Shane Jones. Half mud, half volcano. That's me. Son of Ngapuhi, born in the Far North, raised on a marae where the korero was straight and the kai was shared and nobody needed a consultant from Wellington to tell them what mattered. My whakapapa goes back a thousand years in this country. A thousand years. My tipuna sailed here on waka, read the stars, carved a civilisation out of the bush. So when I stand here tonight and talk about the future of Aotearoa New Zealand, understand that I am not some blow-in with a theory. I have skin in this game that goes deeper than anyone in the Beehive can fathom.

Now. I want to talk to you about something that has been done to this country. Nobody asked your permission. Nobody put it to a vote. Most of you didn't even know it was happening. Something that was done to you while you were out there working, paying the bills, doing the hard yards, coaching the kids' rugby, keeping your community alive -- and while your back was turned, a succession of governments, Labour and National alike, threw the doors of this country open so wide the hinges came off. And nobody stood up at an election rally and said, "We're going to change who lives in your country and what it looks and feels like, and here's why." They didn't say it because they knew -- they knew -- you would have said no.

In the past five years we have added nigh on half a million people to our population. Half a million. We are capped out at five million and they kept waving people through the gate like it was a Boxing Day sale at The Warehouse. The hospitals can't cope. Schools are bursting. Try finding a house in half the towns in this country. The roads are shot. The infrastructure was built for three and a half million and they've crammed five million into it and then they look at you with a straight face and say, "Why are you complaining?"

I'll tell you why we're complaining. Because nobody asked the people of this country whether they wanted it. Not once. That is not democracy. That is a mugging carried out with a spreadsheet and a smile.

Now look. There are people in Wellington -- the usual ivory-tower hand-wringers and their enablers in the media -- who will hear this speech tonight and they will say, "Oh, there goes Shane Jones again, banging the racist drum." They'll call me a bigot, a xenophobe, whatever else is in fashion this week. They'll furrow their brows on morning television and tut-tut about my language.

I don't care.

I don't care if I go on their programmes or anywhere in New Zealand and I get called out as a racist. I've been here before. Years ago I said what I thought about immigration and the Indian community and they called it a Bollywood overreaction -- and you know what, I'll use that phrase again tonight because it was accurate then and it's accurate now. Every time someone raises a legitimate question about the pace of demographic change in this country, the outrage industry fires up. The cameras come out. The petitions start. And the politicians who were supposed to be having this conversation on your behalf go running for cover. Well, I am not going to be cowed by people who feel characters like me should not have a view about the role of immigration and the size of our population. You're not going to shut me up. And I'll tell you something else -- a lot of Kiwis are glad somebody's finally saying what they've been thinking, because they've been shut out of this debate for twenty years. Twenty years of silence. Twenty years of being told to be polite about it. Well, politeness got us nowhere, brother. Politeness got us half a million extra people and a housing crisis.

And I want to say something here, because this matters to me. This is not about the people who come to this country. Most of them are decent, hard-working people chasing a better life and I don't blame them for that. I would do the same. This is about the governments -- the parade of ministers and officials -- who let them in without a plan, without a mandate, and without a thought for what it would mean for the communities who were already here. The mana of the tangata whenua, the wellbeing of ordinary Kiwis -- that didn't even enter the equation. Ka aroha.

But there is something else that needs saying, and I won't apologise for saying it. This country has a founding culture. It was forged by the Treaty. It was built by tangata whenua and by the settlers who came after, and over generations we created something -- a way of life, a set of values, a culture that is distinctly ours. We have to protect the foundation influences that created this nation. And when you change the character of your population as fast as we have, when you bring in hundreds of thousands of people in the space of a few years, there is an expectation -- a reasonable expectation -- that newcomers adapt and accept the host culture. Not abandon where they came from. Nobody's asking that. But accept that this place has a tikanga, a way of doing things, that was here long before they arrived. And if that observation offends somebody, well, I've got the hide of a rhinoceros, brother. It'll take more than that.

Ahakoa he iti, he pounamu. Though it is small, it is precious. That's what we say. And this country is precious. It is small and it is precious and it is not a bloody transit lounge for people on their way to Sydney. But that's what we've become. Thousands of people are getting fast-tracked to residency, and what happens? They either stay or -- more likely -- they use us as a stepping stone and hop across the Tasman. We train them, we house them, we stretch everything to breaking point -- and then they pack up and go. What are we, a halfway house?

I was in Kaikohe last month. Am I right, some of you know Kaikohe? Beautiful town. My town. And I talked to a young fella there, a rangatahi, nineteen years old, smart as a whip, wants to work. And he tells me he can't get a start because the orchard down the road would rather bring in workers on a visa. Cheaper, they reckon. Easier. And I looked at that young man and I thought, what have we done? What have we done to our own people?

Because some employer -- and I'm looking at the orchardists, the builders, the hospitality crowd -- decided it was easier to bring someone in from overseas than train up a young Kiwi. The government has been pumping up the tyres of employers and leaving our own people flat. Why are we bringing in more low-skilled workers on visas when we have young people in Kaikohe, in Kawerau, in Opotiki, in Wairoa, in every small town in this country, who want to work but can't get a look in?

That is a failure of policy and a failure of will. And frankly it is a betrayal.

Now, I know -- I know -- some of you are looking at me thinking, there he goes, the most dangerous man in the Cabinet, the Northland wrecking ball. And you're right. I am dangerous. I am dangerous to anyone who thinks they can change this country without asking the people who live here.

And as tangata whenua -- as Maori -- I have a particular anxiety that I make no apology for. Our population has hit five million. You know what that means? It means our percentage as Maori is shrinking. Every wave of mass immigration makes us a smaller fraction of our own country. Unless we get a grip on this, the next generation of Maori are going to be strangers in their own land. Strangers in their own land. A people whose whakapapa stretches back to Kupe, marginalised by arithmetic. I will not accept that. NZ First will not accept that.

And I have a word for the universities too, while I'm on a roll. I watched Auckland University -- a taonga, supposedly -- turn from a house of learning into a fee-farming operation. A degree factory where the business model depends on international fee-paying students and the domestic students -- your kids, my kids -- are an afterthought. That's not education. That's a business wearing a gown.

The India free trade agreement. The Green Party zealots and the free-market fantasists will try to sell you a fairy tale about that one. We are not opposed to trade. NZ First believes in trade. But we are not going to be a dumping ground for people trying to come to New Zealand under the guise of a trade deal. That agreement has got holes in it you could drive a truck through, and we will not sign away our sovereignty at the border so some diplomat can have a photo op.

You know, Cicero said that the welfare of the people is the supreme law. That was two thousand years ago and we still haven't learned it. The welfare of the people. Not the welfare of the employers. Not the welfare of the universities. Not the welfare of the trade negotiators. The people.

So what are we going to do about it? I'll tell you. Because unlike the dreamers on the other side, NZ First has actual answers.

We want a population policy. A real one. How many people can this country actually hold? Can the hospitals handle it? Can the schools? Have we got enough houses? You answer those questions first. Then you talk about immigration numbers. You don't just open the tap and hope the bath doesn't overflow.

We want employers to train locals first. If you can't fill a job with a New Zealander, fine, we'll talk about a visa. But you prove it. You invest in training our people. You give the rangatahi a fair go before you go looking overseas.

We want an end to the fast-track residency pipeline that turns New Zealand into a waiting room for Australia. And we want an immigration system that works for this country, not one that works this country over.

And above all -- and this is the kaupapa that matters most -- we want this decision, who comes here and how many, to be made democratically. If you want to change what this country looks like, who lives here, then go and campaign on it. Put it to the people. Win the argument. Nobody's done it. Nobody's had the guts. And that silence, my friends, has been deafening. Twenty years of cowardice dressed up as politeness.

I'm not giving you a slogan. I'm giving you a warning. Unfettered immigration is going to remake this country into something your grandchildren won't recognise. Everyone else in Parliament runs a mile from this conversation. They'd rather talk about cycleways and pronouns. Well, I'm not everyone else.

We are at eleven point nine percent in the polls and climbing. You know why? Because the provinces are awake. Because the people who got ignored for twenty years are finding their voice. Because when you tell people to shut up for twenty years, eventually they stop being polite about it. And that's what's happening.

There's no point being poor in paradise. And there's no point having a paradise if you give it away without asking the people who live here.

I am the Prince of the Provinces. Ngapuhi to my bones. NZ First till they carry me out. And I am not going anywhere.

Kia ora tatou.
