# Wave 1 Brand Identity: Name, Voice, and Visual Principles

---

## 1. Name Options: 10 Candidates with Analysis

### Option 1: Population Plan NZ

**Cognitive associations:** Planning, governance competence, the gap between what exists (nothing) and what should exist (a plan). Signals technocratic concern, not ideology. "Population" is neutral. "Plan" implies the absence of one.

**Headline test:** "According to Population Plan NZ, no major party has a stated target for net migration." Works well. Sounds like an organisation, not a person.

**Handle:** @PopulationPlanNZ (20 chars, slightly long but clean). Could shorten to @PopPlanNZ.

**Vulnerabilities:** Slightly dry. Could be mistaken for a government body, which is actually useful early on but could create confusion. "Population plan" has faint associations with China's one-child policy for some people, though this is a stretch in NZ context.

**Te reo resonance:** No direct resonance, but doesn't clash either. "Population" has no te reo equivalent that's commonly used in public discourse. Neutral ground.

---

### Option 2: The Population Gap

**Cognitive associations:** A gap between infrastructure and people. Implies something missing, broken, unfinished. "Gap" is concrete and visual. It frames the problem as a deficit in planning rather than an excess of people.

**Headline test:** "The Population Gap says Wellington Hospital was built for 180,000 people but now serves 220,000." Strong. The name itself makes the argument.

**Handle:** @ThePopulationGap (16 chars, clean).

**Vulnerabilities:** Could be read as academic or think-tanky, which might trigger the "self-appointed expert" version of tall poppy syndrome. The word "gap" is slightly abstract.

**Te reo resonance:** Neutral. No clash, no resonance.

---

### Option 3: Plan the Growth

**Cognitive associations:** An imperative, a demand. Not anti-growth but pro-planning. Shifts the frame from "stop immigration" to "manage what you're doing." Implies the government is being reckless, not that immigration is bad.

**Headline test:** "Plan the Growth, a campaign tracking New Zealand's infrastructure deficit, released new OIA data today." Functional but slightly clunky as a source attribution.

**Handle:** @PlanTheGrowth (13 chars, clean and available-length).

**Vulnerabilities:** Could sound like a business consultancy or urban planning group. Doesn't immediately signal immigration. Requires explanation.

**Te reo resonance:** Neutral.

---

### Option 4: How Many NZ

**Cognitive associations:** A question. How many people? How many houses? How many hospital beds? The question format signals curiosity rather than ideology. It positions the campaign as asking what nobody else will ask. "NZ" grounds it locally.

**Headline test:** "According to How Many NZ, the government has no answer to how many people it plans to add next year." Very strong. The name IS the argument.

**Handle:** @HowManyNZ (9 chars, short, clean, memorable).

**Vulnerabilities:** Could be co-opted or misread as anti-immigrant ("how many more?") by bad-faith critics. The ambiguity cuts both ways: it's powerful as a question, but opponents could fill in the blank negatively.

**Te reo resonance:** No direct resonance, but "how many" is such a basic phrase it's culturally neutral.

---

### Option 5: Infrastructure Gap NZ

**Cognitive associations:** Infrastructure first, not immigration first. Signals that the problem is building, not people. Very defensible framing. Anyone who attacks this is arguing against infrastructure investment.

**Headline test:** "Infrastructure Gap NZ has released data showing population growth is outpacing hospital capacity in five regions." Solid. Sounds credible.

**Handle:** @InfraGapNZ (10 chars, clean).

**Vulnerabilities:** Too narrow. Immigration is the cause, infrastructure is the symptom. If you name yourself after the symptom, you may struggle to pivot to the cause. Also sounds like it could be an engineering lobby group.

**Te reo resonance:** Neutral.

---

### Option 6: NZ Numbers

**Cognitive associations:** Data, transparency, facts. "Numbers" signals that you deal in evidence, not opinion. Short. Punchy. The name implies "we show you the numbers the government won't."

**Headline test:** "NZ Numbers analysis shows net migration added a city the size of Hamilton last year." Clean and credible.

**Handle:** @NZNumbers (9 chars, excellent).

**Vulnerabilities:** Generic. Could be about anything: economics, sports stats, census data. Lacks the specificity that makes people immediately understand what you're about. Would need strong bio and content to compensate.

**Te reo resonance:** Neutral.

---

### Option 7: Who Asked Us

**Cognitive associations:** Democratic deficit. Nobody asked New Zealanders whether they wanted this level of population growth. The name carries the "reluctant Kiwi" energy perfectly: understated, slightly aggrieved, democratic rather than ideological. It's the pub question made into a brand.

**Headline test:** "Who Asked Us, a population accountability campaign, says the government is building a bigger country without a mandate." Very strong. The name does rhetorical work.

**Handle:** @WhoAskedUsNZ (12 chars, clean).

**Vulnerabilities:** Could sound conspiratorial or populist in the wrong context. "Who asked us" can shade toward grievance politics if the tone slips. Also, opponents could reply "democracy asked you, via elections" which is a valid counter.

**Te reo resonance:** Interesting potential. The concept of consultation, of asking, runs deep in tikanga Maori. The idea that major decisions affecting the whenua (land) should involve proper consultation resonates with hui process and Treaty principles. Not explicit, but the underlying value aligns.

---

### Option 8: Demand the Plan

**Cognitive associations:** An imperative. Civic demand. Implies the plan should exist but doesn't. Assertive without being aggressive. Frames the audience as citizens with the right to demand competence from their government.

**Headline test:** "Demand the Plan released its party scorecard today, finding that only NZ First has a stated net migration target." Works. Slightly activist-sounding.

**Handle:** @DemandThePlan (13 chars).

**Vulnerabilities:** The imperative voice could trigger tall poppy syndrome. It's telling people what to do. New Zealanders resist being told what to do, even when they agree. Also sounds like it could be about any policy area.

**Te reo resonance:** Neutral.

---

### Option 9: Growing Pains NZ

**Cognitive associations:** Familiar phrase, domestic, relatable. Implies NZ is growing too fast and it hurts. The "pains" are felt by ordinary people. Slightly self-deprecating, which fits the NZ persona. Not ideological.

**Headline test:** "Growing Pains NZ has tracked the gap between population growth and hospital capacity across 12 regions." Works well. Approachable.

**Handle:** @GrowingPainsNZ (14 chars, clean).

**Vulnerabilities:** Could sound like a parenting blog or a coming-of-age reference. Slightly too cute for serious policy moments. May not command enough authority when quoted in parliamentary context or select committee submissions.

**Te reo resonance:** Neutral.

---

### Option 10: The Missing Plan

**Cognitive associations:** Something that should exist but doesn't. Frames the entire campaign premise in two words. NZ has no population plan. That's the missing plan. Simple. Memorable. Points the finger at government negligence without pointing at migrants.

**Headline test:** "The Missing Plan has OIA'd every relevant government agency and found no department is responsible for matching infrastructure to population growth." Excellent. The name makes the finding feel inevitable.

**Handle:** @TheMissingPlan (14 chars) or @MissingPlanNZ (13 chars).

**Vulnerabilities:** Slightly passive. It describes an absence rather than demanding action. Could feel like it's only about one thing (the plan) and struggle to expand scope. Also, once a party eventually does release a plan, the name could feel outdated, though you could pivot to "is the plan good enough?"

**Te reo resonance:** Neutral.

## 2. Top Recommendation: How Many NZ

**The name: How Many NZ. Handle: @HowManyNZ.**

Here is why it beats the alternatives.

**It is the question, not the answer.** The entire strategy from the expert analysis rests on one principle: the campaign must ask questions, not make accusations. "How Many NZ" is literally a question. Every piece of content can start with "how many": How many people did we add last year? How many houses did we build? How many hospital beds? How many GPs? How many new schools? The name generates the content format. It IS the editorial voice.

**It passes the reluctant Kiwi test.** "How many" doesn't sound like a crusader or an ideologue. It sounds like someone who looked at the numbers and started asking obvious questions. It's the friend at the barbecue who pulls up Stats NZ on their phone and says "hang on, have you seen this?" That is exactly the persona the NZ cultural analysis says will work: understated, curious, data-driven, slightly incredulous rather than angry.

**It is impossible to attack without looking evasive.** If a politician or commentator criticises "How Many NZ," they are criticising someone for asking how many. The name frames any attack as an attack on transparency. "Why are you against counting?" is the implicit counter. This is strong defensive positioning.

**It works across all content types.** Data post: "How many new houses did Auckland consent last year vs how many people it added? Here's the ratio." Reactive post: "The PM says immigration is under control. How many is 'under control'? What's the number?" Question to politician: "How many people does your party think NZ should add next year?" Accountability post: "We asked all six parties how many. Three answered. Three didn't. Here's the scorecard." The name structures the content naturally.

**The handle is short, clean, and available-format.** @HowManyNZ is 9 characters. It works on X, TikTok, Facebook, and as a website (howmany.nz is a plausible domain using New Zealand's .nz TLD). It's memorable after one encounter. You can say it in conversation: "Have you seen the How Many NZ page?"

**It scales without losing meaning.** If the campaign grows, "How Many" becomes a frame that others can use. People start asking their own MPs "how many?" at electorate meetings. The question spreads beyond the account. You've created a meme in the original Dawkins sense: a unit of cultural transmission. "How many?" becomes the two-word summary of the population planning argument.

**It avoids every trap the experts identified.** It doesn't sound anti-immigrant (it's a question about planning, not about people). It doesn't sound like NZ First (NZ First makes statements; this asks questions). It doesn't trigger tall poppy syndrome (asking a question is humble, not grandiose). It doesn't invoke race, culture, identity, or the Treaty in any direction. It's clean.

**The headline test is strong across contexts:**
- News: "How Many NZ releases data showing infrastructure spending hasn't matched population growth"
- Opinion: "How Many NZ is asking the question politicians won't answer"
- Attack (from opponents): "How Many NZ is a front for..." -- even the attack has to use the name, which reinforces the question
- Social share: "Have you seen @HowManyNZ? They're tracking the numbers no one talks about"

**Why it beats the close alternatives:**

It beats "Population Plan NZ" because it's warmer, more conversational, and less institutional. Population Plan NZ sounds like a government agency. How Many NZ sounds like a person who cares.

It beats "The Missing Plan" because it's active rather than passive. The Missing Plan describes a problem. How Many NZ demands an answer.

It beats "Who Asked Us" because it's less prone to being read as grievance politics. "Who asked us" can shade into resentment. "How many" stays in the domain of curiosity and accountability.

It beats "NZ Numbers" because it's specific enough to signal what you're about while being broad enough to cover housing, health, infrastructure, and migration all under one umbrella.

**One vulnerability to address:** Bad-faith actors could try to turn "how many" into "how many foreigners" or similar. The defence is in the content, not the name. Every piece of content answers "how many what?" with infrastructure, houses, hospital beds, school places, GP clinics. The pattern establishes that "how many" is always about what's missing in services, not about who's arriving. Build this pattern early and consistently, and the bad-faith reading doesn't stick.

## 3. Visual Identity Principles

These are designed for a solo operator making graphics in Canva, not for a design agency. The goal is consistency and credibility with zero budget.

**Primary colour: Deep navy (#1B2A4A) or very dark blue-grey.** This is the colour of authority, data, and seriousness in the NZ context. It reads as trustworthy without being corporate. It's the colour of government reports and broadsheet journalism. It says "I have the receipts."

**Accent colour: A clear, slightly warm white (#F5F5F0) for backgrounds and text.** Not pure white (too stark on screen) but an off-white that feels considered. This keeps everything legible and clean.

**Highlight colour: A muted teal or greenstone-inspired green (#2A7F6F).** Used sparingly for emphasis, key numbers, call-to-action elements, and the occasional chart highlight. Teal reads as modern, slightly environmental, and vaguely NZ without being on the nose about it. It's not pounamu green exactly, but it's in that family. It signals care for the country without waving a flag.

**Alert/contrast colour: A warm amber (#D4860B) for scorecards, warnings, and "parties that didn't answer" markers.** Amber reads as caution, not danger. It says "pay attention to this" without screaming.

**Typography feel:** Use a clean sans-serif for everything. In Canva, this means Inter, DM Sans, or similar. No serif fonts (too academic), no display fonts (too flashy), no handwritten fonts (too casual). The feel should be: a well-made government report that was designed by someone who actually cares about readability. Headings in bold, body text in regular weight. Generous spacing. Numbers should always be large and prominent, set in the same typeface at heavier weight.

**Numbers are the hero.** Every graphic should make a number the largest element. Not words, not images, not the logo. The number. 130,000. 14,200. 40,000 houses. 0 new hospitals. The number IS the content. Everything else is context.

**Imagery principles:** Use real NZ photography sparingly: aerial shots of housing developments, hospital buildings, traffic, infrastructure. Never use stock photos of people, especially not ethnically identifiable people. The campaign is about infrastructure and planning, not about who lives here. If you use images of people, they should be distant, anonymous, or in silhouette: a queue at an emergency department, cars in traffic, a "for sale" sign. The imagery should evoke the consequence (strain, crowding, shortage) without putting a face on it.

**No logo initially.** Use the text "How Many NZ" set in your primary typeface, bold, navy, as your profile image and brand mark. A text-only mark signals data and transparency. It avoids the risk of a bad logo (which a solo operator will almost certainly produce) undermining credibility. If the campaign grows and budget appears, commission a simple wordmark from a designer. Until then, typographic consistency IS the brand.

**Chart and data visualisation style:** Simple bar charts and line charts. Navy bars, teal highlights, amber for emphasis. Always label axes clearly. Always cite the source in small text below the chart. Never use 3D effects, gradients, or decorative elements on charts. The aesthetic is "clean government data that someone actually made readable." Canva's built-in chart tools are sufficient for this.

**Social media profile consistency:** Same profile image across all platforms (the text mark). Same bio structure: "Tracking New Zealand's population growth vs infrastructure. Data from Stats NZ, MBIE, OIA responses." Same navy background on the profile banner with one large number (update this periodically with the latest net migration figure or the most striking stat of the month).

## 4. Voice and Tone Guide

**The voice is:** A sharp, data-literate New Zealander who is genuinely frustrated but never unhinged. They sound like a senior public servant who quit and started saying what they actually think, or an economics teacher who's fed up. The tone is dry, direct, slightly sardonic, and always grounded in a specific number. They never raise their voice but they never look away from the data either.

**Guiding principles for voice:**

Always lead with a number. Every post should contain at least one specific, sourced data point. The number comes first, the opinion comes second. This order is non-negotiable. It's what separates this voice from a rant.

Ask questions more than make statements. The name is a question and the voice should reflect that. "Did you know?" and "Why won't they say?" are the two workhorse constructions.

Blame systems, not people. The government, the policy settings, the lack of planning. Never individual migrants. Never ethnic groups. Never "immigrants" as a collective noun used negatively.

Use NZ English naturally. "Heaps" not "a lot." "Sorted" not "resolved." "Sweet as" if it fits. The voice should sound like it's from here without performing Kiwi-ness.

Be willing to praise. When a party does release a population target or a plan, acknowledge it. "Credit where it's due: [Party] is the first to put a number on it. Now let's see if the others follow." This signals fairness and makes criticism more credible.

**10 sample posts across content types:**

**1. Data post (X/Twitter)**

Net migration in the year to Sept 2023: 130,900 people.

New hospital beds added nationally that year: 0 net increase.

New GP clinics opened in Auckland: 3.

Someone explain the maths to me.

Source: Stats NZ, Ministry of Health annual report.

**2. Reactive post (responding to a housing announcement)**

The government just announced 5,000 new social houses over 3 years.

Net migration over the same period will add roughly 150,000 people at current settings.

5,000 houses. 150,000 people.

This isn't a housing plan. It's a rounding error.

**3. Question to a politician (X/Twitter, tagging the PM)**

Genuine question for @ChrisPMNZ: How many people does your government think is the right number to add to New Zealand next year?

Not a trick question. Just a number.

Every serious country has one. We don't. Why?

**4. OIA reveal post (X/Twitter)**

We OIA'd MBIE asking whether any government department is responsible for matching infrastructure investment to population growth.

The answer: no single agency has this responsibility.

Nobody is in charge of planning for the people we're adding.

Read the full response here: [link]

**5. Scorecard post (all platforms)**

HOW MANY NZ PARTY SCORECARD -- March 2026

Does your party have a net migration target?

NZ First: Yes (15,000-25,000) -- GREEN
Labour: No stated target -- RED
National: No stated target -- RED
Greens: No stated target -- RED
ACT: "Market should decide" -- AMBER
TPM: No stated position -- RED

We asked. This is what we got.

**6. Local impact post (Facebook, targeting a specific region)**

Tauranga's population grew by 12,000 in the last two years.

In that time, the Western Bay of Plenty DHB added zero new acute care beds.

Tauranga Hospital's emergency department was designed for a city of 110,000. The city is now 156,000.

If you've waited 8 hours in that ED, this is why.

**7. International comparison post (X/Twitter)**

Australia just announced a net migration cap of 185,000 and a plan to build 1.2 million homes in 5 years to match it.

Canada cut its immigration target by 20% and linked it to housing starts.

NZ's plan for matching infrastructure to population growth: [crickets]

**8. Self-deprecating/human post (TikTok caption or X)**

Started this account because I looked up how many people NZ added last year vs how many houses we built.

Thought I'd made a spreadsheet error.

I hadn't.

Now I can't stop asking "how many?" about everything. Turns out the answer is almost always "nobody knows" or "nobody's counting."

Anyway, welcome. The data is free and the government really wishes you wouldn't look at it.

**9. Engagement/question post (all platforms)**

Honest question: when the government adds 100,000 people to the country, whose job is it to make sure there are enough GPs, schools, and houses for them?

Because we OIA'd five agencies and none of them said it was their job.

If you know the answer, we'd love to hear it. Genuinely.

**10. Election-focused post (closer to election day)**

There are roughly 120 days until the election.

We've asked every party one question: how many people should New Zealand add each year, and what's your plan to house and serve them?

Three parties have answered. Three haven't.

If your party hasn't answered, ask them. At the electorate meeting. At the town hall. In the DMs.

The question is simple. The silence tells you everything.

## 5. What the Brand Is Not

This section defines the hard boundaries. These are not guidelines. They are rules that protect the campaign from self-destruction in the NZ context.

**How Many NZ never targets ethnic groups.** Never. Not by name, not by implication, not by image, not by dog whistle. No "certain communities." No mention of specific nationalities in a negative context. No images that single out any ethnic group. The campaign is about numbers, infrastructure, and planning. The moment it becomes about who is arriving rather than how many and what's the plan, it's over. This is the single most important rule.

**How Many NZ never uses the word "remigration."** Not to discuss it, not to distance from it, not to define it. The word does not exist in this brand's vocabulary. If asked about it, the answer is: "That's not what we do. We ask how many and we ask what's the plan."

**How Many NZ is not affiliated with any political party.** Not NZ First, not ACT, not anyone. The brand praises and criticises all parties based on whether they answer the question. The moment it becomes identified with one party, it loses the ability to pressure the others and it loses cross-partisan credibility. If NZ First supporters become the dominant audience, actively create content that appeals to Labour and Green voters (housing affordability, environmental impact of growth, worker exploitation in migration schemes).

**How Many NZ never sounds angry or outraged.** Frustrated, yes. Exasperated, yes. Sardonic, yes. But never shouting, never furious, never apocalyptic. The tone is the teacher who's shown you the data three times and can't believe you still haven't read it. Not the protester with a megaphone. NZ's cultural immune system attacks anger. It respects persistence.

**How Many NZ never uses conspiracy framing.** No "great replacement." No "they're doing this deliberately." No "the elites want..." No "agenda." The framing is always incompetence, not malice. The government doesn't have a sinister plan. It doesn't have a plan at all. That's the problem. Incompetence is a more credible and more devastating accusation in NZ than conspiracy, because New Zealanders already believe their government is somewhat incompetent. You're confirming a suspicion, not introducing a theory.

**How Many NZ never punches down.** Never at migrants, refugees, international students, or workers who came here under legal settings. These people are not the problem. The policy settings are the problem. If the campaign discusses migrant worker exploitation (which it should), the framing is: "the government's own immigration system is exploiting vulnerable people" not "migrants are taking jobs."

**How Many NZ never looks slick, expensive, or institutional.** No glossy graphics. No professional video production. No language that sounds like it was written by a comms team. The visual identity is clean but modest. The voice is human and slightly rough around the edges. This is a person with a spreadsheet and some questions, not a lobbying operation with funding. The moment it looks funded or professional, NZ's tall poppy instinct kicks in: "Who's paying for this? What's their agenda?"

**How Many NZ never makes claims it can't source.** Every number has a citation. Every stat has a link. If the data is uncertain, say so. "We think the number is roughly X based on [source], but the government won't release exact figures" is fine. "The number is X" without a source is not. Credibility is the only asset. One unsourced claim that gets debunked and the brand is finished.

**How Many NZ never engages in personal attacks on politicians.** Criticise their policies, their silence, their refusal to answer. Never their character, their appearance, their family, their personal life. "The Minister has refused to state a target" is fine. "The Minister is an idiot" is not. NZ is too small for personal attacks. The politician you insult today is the one you might need to work with in six months.

**How Many NZ never comments on the Treaty of Waitangi as a primary topic.** The brand can note that population policy affects Treaty obligations and that tangata whenua have not been consulted on the rate of growth. But it does not take a position on the Treaty itself, on Treaty settlements, on co-governance, or on any of the current Treaty debates. That is a different battlefield and stepping onto it alienates half the potential audience no matter which side you take. Stay in your lane: how many, what's the plan.

## 6. Stress Test

**Attack vector: "This is just anti-immigration racism with a data veneer."**

Defence: Every post leads with government data. The campaign explicitly advocates for migrant welfare. The question is not "should people come" but "how many and what's the plan to house and serve them." The name itself is a question, not a statement. This attack will be attempted but it struggles to land when the content is consistently about hospitals, houses, and infrastructure.

**Attack vector: "Who funds this?"**

Defence: Nobody. The answer is always the same: one person, zero budget, public data, free tools. The modest visual identity supports this. If pressed, the campaign can publish its total costs (a domain name and a Canva free account). Radical transparency on funding is a feature, not a vulnerability.

**Attack vector: "NZ First front group."**

Defence: The scorecard treats all parties equally. Content should actively engage with Labour and Green voters on housing and environmental grounds. Periodically ask NZ First hard questions too: "NZ First says they want lower migration. How many, exactly? What's the target? Show us the plan." Equal-opportunity accountability is the proof.

**Attack vector: "This person has no qualifications."**

Defence: The data speaks for itself. Every claim is sourced to Stats NZ, MBIE, or OIA responses. "I'm not an expert. I'm a person who can read government data. You can check my sources. I wish the experts were saying this louder." The reluctant Kiwi persona absorbs this attack naturally.

**Attack vector: "They're ignoring the economic benefits of immigration."**

Defence: "Great point. How many immigrants does the government think we need for economic growth, and what's their plan to build the infrastructure those people need? We'd love to see the cost-benefit analysis. We OIA'd it. It doesn't exist." Turn the attack into another "how many" question.

**Attack vector in te ao Maori: "Pakeha telling tangata whenua who can come to Aotearoa."**

Defence: The campaign does not tell anyone who can come. It asks how many people the country is planning for and whether the infrastructure exists. If Maori voices raise concerns about population growth affecting their communities and resources, the campaign amplifies those voices rather than speaking for them. The campaign explicitly notes that tangata whenua have never been formally consulted on the rate of population growth. This positions the campaign as an ally of Treaty-based consultation, not a competitor to it.

**Stress test against NZ media norms:** The name "How Many NZ" reads as neutral-to-curious in a headline. It does not trigger the "here we go, another extremist" reflex that names like "Migration Watch" or "Population Defence" would. A journalist can quote "How Many NZ" without feeling like they're platforming something dangerous. That frictionless quotability is essential in NZ's small, cautious media environment.

**Stress test against tall poppy syndrome:** The name is a question, not a claim of expertise. The voice is frustrated citizen, not self-appointed authority. The visual identity is modest. The brand says "I'm asking what you're already thinking" rather than "I have the answers." This is the posture that survives tall poppy syndrome in NZ.

**Stress test against longevity:** "How many" never goes out of date. It works whether net migration is 130,000 or 14,000. It works whether the issue is hospitals, houses, schools, roads, or water infrastructure. It works in a boom and a recession. The question is always relevant because the answer is always either "we don't know" or "nobody planned for it." The brand name has a long shelf life.
