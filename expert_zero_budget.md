# Zero-Budget Political Influence: Forcing Immigration onto New Zealand's 2026 Election Agenda

## 1. The Brutal Truth About Zero-Budget Influence

The mechanics of going from nobody to politically relevant with no money are well understood. They are also unforgiving. Here is how it actually works.

**Attention is the only currency you have.** Every other resource (money, staff, institutional credibility) is just a way of buying attention. If you can generate attention directly, you can skip the intermediaries. Social media made this possible. The algorithm does not care about your budget. It cares about engagement.

**The compounding mechanism works like this.** A piece of content gets shared. Some percentage of those who see it follow you. Your next piece of content reaches a larger base. A small percentage of that larger base shares it. The cycle repeats. The math is exponential but the base rate is tiny. Most content gets zero traction. You need volume and consistency to hit the occasional breakout, and you need to convert each breakout into retained followers.

In practice, the compounding curve looks like nothing happening for weeks, then a spike, then back to nothing, then a bigger spike, then sustained low-level growth punctuated by spikes. Most people quit during the "nothing happening" phase. The ones who break through are the ones who kept posting through it.

**The minimum viable audience for political pressure in NZ is smaller than you think.** New Zealand's political media ecosystem is tiny. There are maybe 20 political journalists who matter, a handful of political editors, and a few hundred thousand people who actively follow politics on social media. A Twitter/X account with 5,000 engaged followers in NZ political circles is a notable voice. An account with 15,000 is a force. A piece of content that gets 50,000 views in the NZ political space will be seen by most journalists and a meaningful share of politically engaged citizens.

The threshold for "parties must respond" is roughly: when journalists start asking party spokespeople about your claims or framing, you have won. Journalists ask when they think something is newsworthy, and they think something is newsworthy when it is being widely discussed by politically engaged people. That is a circle of maybe 10,000-30,000 people in NZ.

**The real mechanism of pressure is triangulation.** You do not pressure a party directly. You create a situation where journalists pressure the party on your behalf. Journalists do this when (a) the topic is generating public discussion, (b) they have a specific question to ask (a data point, a claim, a contradiction), and (c) they believe their audience cares. Your job is to manufacture all three conditions.

**What actually does not work with zero budget:** press releases nobody reads, petitions with small numbers, formal letters to MPs, policy papers nobody downloads, websites nobody visits, Facebook groups that become echo chambers. These feel productive. They produce nothing.

**What actually works with zero budget:** content that is inherently shareable because it triggers an emotional response (anger, surprise, validation), content that gives people something specific to argue about, content that makes someone in power look bad or hypocritical, content that packages a complex issue into a simple and memorable frame, and relentless consistency that builds an audience over time.

## 2. Case Studies

### Case 1: Marcus Sheridan and the "They Ask, You Answer" Model (adapted political example: Shaneel Lal, NZ)

Shaneel Lal was a student activist in New Zealand with no budget, no organisation, and no institutional backing who became a central figure in the debate around conversion therapy. They used social media (primarily Twitter and Instagram) to share personal testimony, call out specific politicians by name, and create shareable content that framed the issue in emotional terms. The path was: personal story content on social media, building a following among sympathetic audiences, escalating to direct challenges of politicians, which journalists then picked up because the confrontations were newsworthy. Timeline: roughly 18 months from obscure student to nationally recognised voice. Budget: effectively zero.

### Case 2: The Grenfell Tower Accountability Campaign (UK, 2017)

Before the Grenfell Tower fire, a small blog run by residents of the tower (the Grenfell Action Group, fewer than 5 active members) had been documenting safety concerns for years. They had no budget. Their blog posts were ignored by the council and media. After the fire, their prescient warnings became the story. This is a grim example, but the lesson matters: documented, timestamped, public warnings about a specific problem create a powerful accountability record. When the thing you warned about happens, you become the authority. The blog had been running for about 4 years before the fire. The activists had spent nothing beyond their time.

### Case 3: Greta Thunberg (Sweden, 2018)

One teenager with a handmade sign sitting outside parliament. No budget, no organisation, no connections. What made it work: a single, visually striking, repeatable action (sitting outside parliament every Friday), a clear and simple message, and the inherent news value of a child doing something adults were not. A local journalist wrote about her after a few weeks. That story got shared. International media picked it up. Within 6 months she was speaking at the UN. The mechanism was: a visually compelling weekly action created a recurring story hook that journalists could return to, and each wave of coverage brought more followers, which made the next wave of coverage bigger. Total budget for the first year: essentially zero.

### Case 4: The "We Are the 99%" Tumblr / Occupy Wall Street (US, 2011)

Before the physical occupation of Zuccotti Park, the framing battle was won online. A Tumblr blog invited people to photograph themselves holding handwritten signs describing their financial situation with the tagline "We are the 99%." This was started by a handful of people, cost nothing, and went viral because it gave ordinary people a way to participate in the narrative. The phrase "the 99%" reframed the entire economic debate in the US and forced every politician to respond to the frame of inequality. The Tumblr launched in August 2011; by October it had reshaped political discourse. The core team was under 5 people. The budget was zero.

### Case 5: Matt Bruenig and the People's Policy Project (US, 2017)

Matt Bruenig was a blogger with no institutional affiliation who built a following by writing sharp, data-driven takedowns of centrist policy positions. He combined rigorous policy analysis with an aggressive social media voice. He was frequently combative, which generated engagement. His blog posts were free, his distribution was Twitter, and his credibility came from the quality of his analysis rather than from any institutional backing. He eventually launched the People's Policy Project as a small think tank, funded by small donations that came entirely from his social media following. He went from random blogger to someone whose policy frames were being discussed by US Senators within about 2 years. Starting budget: zero.

**The common pattern across all five:** a clear frame that simplifies a complex issue, content that is inherently shareable because of emotional resonance or conflict, consistency over months (not days), and a moment where mainstream media picks up the frame because it has already gained traction organically.

## 3. The "Why Would They Answer Us" Problem, Solved

The activist is correct: parties ignore random organisations. Here are 7 concrete mechanisms to become un-ignorable, each costing nothing.

### Tactic 1: Public Scorecarding

Create a public, regularly updated scorecard that rates every party's immigration policy on specific criteria. Post it on social media. Tag every party. Tag journalists. The power of a scorecard is that it forces a binary: either a party has a position or it does not. "No stated position" is itself a damaging score. Parties hate being publicly graded because it creates a comparison frame they do not control. When journalists see the scorecard being shared, they have a ready-made story: "Party X has no immigration policy according to [scorecard]." Parties will eventually respond because not responding makes them look evasive.

### Tactic 2: The Direct Public Question

Film yourself (or write a post) asking a single, specific, unanswerable question directed at a specific politician. Not "what is your immigration policy" but something like "Minister, Stats NZ data shows net migration hit [X] last year. What number do you think is right for New Zealand, and why won't you say it?" Post it publicly. Tag the politician. Tag journalists. Repeat with a new question every week. The specificity matters. Vague questions are easy to ignore. Specific questions with data attached are hard to ignore because not answering looks like hiding.

### Tactic 3: The FOIA/OIA Machine

New Zealand's Official Information Act is free to use. File OIA requests to every relevant government department asking for specific immigration data, internal policy advice, ministerial briefings on immigration levels, and projections. When the responses come back (they are legally required to respond within 20 working days), the information itself becomes content. "I OIA'd Immigration NZ and here's what they told the Minister..." is inherently newsworthy. If they refuse or redact, that is also a story. This is one of the most powerful free tools available to any NZ citizen and most activists underuse it massively.

### Tactic 4: Candidate Confrontation at Public Events

Election campaigns involve public meetings, town halls, and candidate events. These are free to attend. Show up. Ask your specific question on camera. Film the response (or the non-response). Post it. A politician visibly dodging a question on camera is shareable content. You do not need media credentials. You need a phone and the willingness to stand up and ask. If you do this consistently at multiple events across multiple parties, you become a known quantity. Journalists will start watching for you because your confrontations generate content they can use.

### Tactic 5: Data Packaging for Journalists

Most journalists covering immigration do not have time to dig through Stats NZ data, MBIE reports, and OIA responses. If you become the person who packages this data into clear, accurate, ready-to-use formats with proper sourcing, journalists will use your work because it saves them time. You are not asking them to cover you. You are giving them free research assistance. The implicit deal is: they use your data, your framing comes with it. Create a regular data release (weekly or fortnightly) with a single striking finding. Make it easy to screenshot and share. Always cite the primary source so your credibility is verifiable.

### Tactic 6: Exploit the Election Cycle Calendar

Parties release policies on a schedule. Every policy release is an opportunity to publicly ask "but what about immigration?" When Labour releases a housing policy, your immediate response is "Labour's housing plan does not mention immigration. Net migration added [X] people last year. Where will they live?" When National releases an infrastructure plan, same move. When the Greens release a climate plan, "what is the emissions impact of population growth driven by migration?" Every policy release by every party becomes an opportunity to insert immigration into the conversation. This only works if you are fast (responding within hours) and specific (citing real numbers).

### Tactic 7: Coalition Pressure Mapping

NZ's MMP system means coalition dynamics matter enormously. Publicly map out the coalition scenarios and immigration positions. "If NZ First holds the balance of power, their coalition partner will have to negotiate on immigration. National has no stated position. What will they concede?" This kind of analysis forces parties to pre-commit or look unprepared. Post these analyses regularly as polling shifts. Journalists love coalition speculation because it drives engagement. If your coalition analysis consistently includes immigration as a coalition sticking point, it becomes part of the conventional wisdom.

**Why these work when formal approaches fail:** each tactic creates a public, visible pressure point that journalists can observe. Journalists amplify the pressure by asking parties about it. The party then has to respond to the journalist, even if they would never respond to you directly. You are not trying to get the party to answer you. You are trying to create a situation where the party has to answer someone about the issue you raised.

## 4. Path A vs Path B: The Zero-Budget Lens

The activist has correctly identified the core tension. Path B (respectable policy advocacy) requires either institutional credibility or paid distribution to get attention. A random person publishing a policy paper gets zero downloads. Path A (provocative viral content) gets free distribution because the algorithm rewards controversy. On the surface, this seems to settle the question in favour of Path A.

But it is more nuanced than that.

**The problem with pure Path A** is burnout and credibility collapse. Provocation gets initial attention, but it also generates organised opposition. In NZ's small political ecosystem, you can be labelled a crank very quickly, and once that label sticks, journalists stop taking you seriously. Reform UK had Nigel Farage, who had decades of political experience and media training. A solo activist in NZ does not have that armour. Pure provocation also attracts the wrong kind of follower (people who enjoy outrage rather than people who will take political action) and alienates potential allies.

**The problem with pure Path B** is that it does not generate enough attention to matter. A well-researched policy paper that nobody reads has zero political impact regardless of its quality. The activist is right to worry about this.

**The actual answer is a hybrid, weighted toward Path A for distribution and Path B for credibility.** The content should be emotionally engaging, shareable, and occasionally provocative, but it should be built on real data and defensible claims. The tone should be confident and confrontational but not unhinged. Think of it as: the headline and framing are Path A (designed for shareability and emotional response), but when someone clicks through or reads the detail, the substance is Path B (real data, real sources, real analysis).

In practice this looks like: a social media post that says "Stats NZ just confirmed net migration hit [X]. That's [vivid comparison]. And not a single major party has a plan. Here's the data." The post is provocative enough to share. The data is solid enough to survive scrutiny. The call to action is political enough to create pressure.

The ratio should be roughly 70% provocative framing, 30% policy substance. The provocation gets the distribution. The substance prevents credibility collapse.

One more thing: the question is not really Path A vs Path B. It is about the sequence. You start with attention (Path A dynamics) because without attention nothing else matters. Once you have attention and a following, you can introduce more substantive policy content because you now have an audience for it. Trying to build the policy platform first is putting the cart before the horse when you have zero budget.

## 6. The 90-Day Plan

### Week 1: Foundation

Set up social media accounts on Twitter/X, TikTok, and Instagram under a clear, memorable brand name. Something simple like "NZ Immigration Watch" or "Immigration Accountability NZ." Do not use your personal name initially; a branded account looks more institutional and is easier to scale later if others join.

Use Claude Code to build a basic OIA request template and tracking system. File your first 10 OIA requests targeting: MBIE (immigration volumes, visa processing, employer accreditation data), Stats NZ (population projections, net migration breakdowns), Treasury (fiscal impact of migration), Ministry of Housing (housing demand projections linked to population growth), and the Ministry of Education (school roll projections). Each request should ask for something specific enough to yield usable data.

Use Claude Code to build v1 of the data dashboard using publicly available Stats NZ data. Host it on GitHub Pages (free). It does not need to be beautiful. It needs to be accurate and clear.

Create your first 5 pieces of content. Each should present one striking data point about immigration in NZ with proper sourcing, a vivid comparison or frame, and a question directed at a specific party or politician. Post one per day for the first week.

Theory of pressure: you are building the infrastructure (OIA pipeline, data dashboard, content system) that will compound over time, while simultaneously starting to build an audience with daily content.

### Week 2: Escalation and Rhythm

Post daily. Establish a consistent posting rhythm: one data post per day, one "question for [Party]" post per day. Each question should be specific and data-backed. Tag the party, tag journalists who cover politics (there are maybe 15-20 who matter in NZ: the political editors at NZ Herald, Stuff, RNZ, Newsroom, The Post, plus the press gallery journalists on Twitter).

Start building the politician statement tracker using Claude Code. Go back through the last 12 months of Hansard and media to log every statement by a party leader or immigration spokesperson about immigration. Identify contradictions and gaps.

Begin engaging with other accounts in the NZ political Twitter/X space. Reply to journalists' posts about housing, infrastructure, and public services with immigration data. Do not be aggressive. Be the person who always shows up with a relevant number and a source. This builds visibility in the right networks.

File 10 more OIA requests based on gaps you have identified in week 1.

Theory of pressure: you are establishing a daily presence, beginning to insert immigration data into adjacent political conversations, and building a database that will generate content for months.

### Week 3: First Major Content Push

By now you should have enough data to produce a "state of immigration" thread or post. A comprehensive, data-heavy, well-sourced overview of NZ immigration that is designed to be the definitive layperson's summary. This is your breakout attempt. Put significant effort into making it visually clear (simple charts, bold numbers) and shareable.

Post it on a Tuesday or Wednesday morning (peak NZ political Twitter engagement). Tag every relevant journalist. Tag every party. Frame it as: "I compiled every public data source on NZ immigration into one place. Here's what the numbers actually show. No party has a plan for this."

Simultaneously, use the dashboard as the "source" that people can visit for more detail.

If this post gets traction, engage actively with every reply. If it does not get traction, do not be discouraged. Iterate and try again the following week with a different angle.

Theory of pressure: a comprehensive data resource is inherently useful to journalists and commentators. Even if the initial post does not go viral, the dashboard will start accumulating inbound links and references.

### Week 4: OIA Responses Start Arriving

Your first OIA responses should start arriving (20 working day deadline from week 1). Each response is content. For each response, use Claude Code to: extract the key finding, compare it to public statements by politicians, draft a social media post, and draft a longer analysis for the dashboard.

Post OIA findings with screenshots of the actual government documents. Screenshots of official documents are highly shareable because they look authoritative and leaked, even though they are legally obtained public information.

If any OIA request is refused or heavily redacted, that is also a story. "I asked [Agency] for [specific data]. They refused. What are they hiding?" Refusals and redactions are often more shareable than the data itself.

Start the party scorecard. Grade each party on immigration across 5-6 specific criteria (stated migration target, housing plan linked to population, infrastructure plan, wage impact analysis, etc). Publish it. Update it monthly.

Theory of pressure: OIA responses provide exclusive content that nobody else has. Exclusive content is what builds a following because people cannot get it elsewhere. The scorecard creates a recurring accountability frame.

### Month 2: Amplification

By this point you should have a small but growing following (target: 500-1,500 followers across platforms). The content rhythm should be established and partially automated with Claude Code assistance.

Key actions for month 2:

Increase OIA volume. File 20 more requests, increasingly targeted based on what you have learned from the first batch of responses. Follow up on anything interesting with more specific requests.

Start producing short video content for TikTok and Instagram Reels. Use your phone to record 30-60 second explainers of immigration data points. Simple format: you talking to camera with a striking number on screen. TikTok's algorithm is more favourable to new accounts than Twitter's, and political TikTok in NZ is relatively uncrowded.

Begin the "candidate question" series. As election candidates are announced, create a standard set of 5 immigration questions. Publicly challenge each candidate to answer them. Track who responds and who does not on the scorecard.

Identify and engage with sympathetic voices. There will be journalists, commentators, academics, and other activists who share your concerns. Do not ask them to promote you. Share their work, cite their research, and be a useful node in the network. Over time, they will reciprocate.

If any content has broken through to journalists, follow up directly (DM or email) with additional data and offer yourself as a source. Journalists always need sources. If your data is good and your framing is clear, they will use you.

Theory of pressure: month 2 is about audience growth and establishing yourself as a credible, consistent source in the NZ immigration discussion. The candidate question series primes the ground for election coverage.

### Month 3: Political Pressure

By month 3 you should have a meaningful following (target: 2,000-5,000 across platforms), a substantial body of published content and data, a track record of OIA-based revelations, and emerging relationships with journalists.

Key actions for month 3:

Publish a comprehensive "Immigration Election Scorecard" grading every party. Make it visually polished. Promote it hard. Send it to every political journalist. This is designed to generate coverage like "Activist group grades parties on immigration, most get failing marks."

Attend public political events (candidate forums, party rallies, public meetings). Ask your immigration questions on camera. Post the responses (or non-responses). Physical presence adds a dimension that purely online activism cannot.

If you have not yet been covered by mainstream media, directly pitch your best OIA finding to a journalist as an exclusive. "I have government documents showing [X]. Nobody has reported this yet. Do you want an exclusive?" Journalists are more likely to cover a story if they feel they are getting it first.

Use the coalition pressure mapping tactic. As election scenarios crystallise, publish analysis of what immigration policy will look like under each coalition scenario. This forces parties to address the issue in the context of coalition negotiations.

Begin engaging with select committee processes. Parliament's select committees accept public submissions on relevant legislation. These submissions are public record. A well-crafted submission backed by your OIA data and analysis enters the parliamentary record and can be cited by sympathetic MPs.

Theory of pressure: month 3 is about converting online presence into political and media pressure. The scorecard forces parties to respond or be seen as evasive. Physical presence at events creates confrontation content. Select committee submissions create a parliamentary record.

### Measuring Success

Do not measure success by follower count alone. The metrics that matter are:

Has a journalist cited your data or asked a party about your claims? This is the key threshold. Once this happens, you are in the feedback loop where media coverage drives more followers, which drives more media coverage.

Has any party responded to your scorecard or questions, even negatively? A negative response is still a response. It means you are on their radar.

Are other people using your framing when they discuss immigration? If you start seeing your specific data points and comparisons repeated by others, your frame is spreading.

Is immigration being discussed more frequently in political media than it was 90 days ago? This is the ultimate measure.

### Risks and Mitigations

**Risk: being labelled racist or xenophobic.** Immigration is a sensitive topic. Lead with economic data (housing, wages, infrastructure) rather than cultural arguments. Always frame the issue as "what is the right number and what is the plan" rather than "immigrants are bad." Cite the impacts on recent immigrants as well as existing residents (overcrowded housing affects new arrivals too). This framing is harder to dismiss as xenophobia.

**Risk: legal threats.** OIA requests are legally protected. Published data from public sources is legally safe. Opinions clearly stated as opinions are legally safe. Do not make factual claims you cannot source. Do not publish personal information about private individuals. Stick to public figures, public data, and public statements.

**Risk: burnout.** A solo activist producing daily content will burn out within months without AI assistance. Use Claude Code to handle as much of the mechanical work (data processing, draft generation, OIA tracking) as possible. Reserve your human energy for strategic decisions, public appearances, and relationship building.

**Risk: co-option.** If you succeed, political parties or interest groups will try to co-opt your work. Maintain independence. Do not endorse parties. Do not accept funding with strings. The moment you become aligned with one party, you lose the ability to pressure all parties.

## Summary

The core theory of this plan is straightforward. A single person with no money but with AI tooling and relentless consistency can generate enough attention on a specific political issue to force media coverage, which forces political party responses. The key ingredients are: data that journalists can use, framing that is shareable on social media, consistency that builds an audience over time, and the AI-enabled capacity to operate at the output level of a small organisation.

The honest assessment of probability: there is maybe a 25-35% chance this works within 90 days. Political agenda-setting is hard and uncertain. But the downside risk is near zero (you spend time, not money), and if the first 90 days do not break through, the infrastructure and audience you have built compound into the next 90 days. The activists who succeed are the ones who treat it as a long game and do not quit when the first month feels like shouting into the void.
