# Wave 3: Network Building Playbook

## How "How Many NZ" goes from one person to a movement without budget or formal structure

---

## 1. THE NETWORK MAP

Political influence in New Zealand runs through roughly 200 people. Not 200,000. Two hundred. The entire political-media-policy ecosystem fits in a large room. This is the campaign's greatest structural advantage. You don't need reach. You need relationships with the right people in the right order.

### Tier 1: Must Have (first 8 weeks)

**Who they are:** The 10-15 people whose engagement gives the campaign instant credibility and a distribution channel you could never build yourself. These are people who already care about the population-infrastructure question and have audiences that trust them.

**The specific people:**

Bernard Hickey (The Kaka). He has been making this argument for years. He has a paying subscriber base of the exact demographic you need: economically literate, politically engaged, centre-to-centre-left New Zealanders. His endorsement of your data is the single most valuable thing that can happen in months one through three.

Michael Reddell (Croaking Cassandra). Former Reserve Bank economist. Has been writing about immigration and productivity for a decade. His intellectual credibility is deep. If Reddell engages with your data on his blog, that's a signal to every economist and policy person in the country that your work is serious.

Jenee Tibshraeny (Interest.co.nz). Covers the housing-economy intersection daily. Already writes about population dynamics. Would use good data immediately if it landed in her inbox.

Shamubeel Eaqub (economist, commentator). Carries weight with the centre-left. If he's citing your data, it can't be dismissed as right-wing grievance.

Bryce Edwards (Democracy Project). Aggregates political commentary daily. If he includes your analysis in his roundup, it reaches the entire political class over their morning coffee.

One sympathetic NZ First MP (most likely Shane Jones's office). Not for partisan alignment but for the practical power of getting your data cited on the parliamentary record. A written question using your analysis converts you from "anonymous account" to "source cited in Parliament."

One or two local body politicians in high-growth areas (Auckland, Tauranga, Hamilton, Christchurch). Mayors or councillors who deal with infrastructure-population pressure daily and would welcome having someone else producing the data that proves what they already know.

**How to reach them:** You don't pitch these people. You produce something so good they can't ignore it. Send Hickey your first major OIA finding with a two-line note: "Thought you might find this useful for your work. Happy for you to use it however you like." Send Reddell a data analysis and say: "I'd value your view on whether I've got this right." Send Tibshraeny your dashboard with a note: "This pulls together some numbers you've been writing about. Use it if it's helpful." The tone is always: here is something useful for you. Never: please help me.

**What you're asking of them:** Nothing explicitly. You're giving them material they can use in their own work. The implicit ask is: use this, cite it, engage with it. When they do, your credibility cascades. Hickey cites you in The Kaka. His subscribers look you up. Reddell blogs about your analysis. Policy people read his blog. Edwards includes your work in his roundup. Gallery journalists see it there. This is how visibility builds in NZ's small ecosystem without self-promotion.

### Tier 2: Important (weeks 4-12)

**Who they are:** The 20-30 people who amplify the campaign into the mainstream and create the sense that this isn't one voice but a conversation happening across sectors.

**Academics who study housing, urban planning, infrastructure, or demographics.** People at the University of Auckland's public policy programme, Victoria University's School of Government, or anyone who has published on New Zealand's infrastructure deficit. Academics are cautious by nature, but many are privately frustrated that the population-infrastructure link isn't discussed. They don't need to endorse you. They just need to engage with your data publicly, even critically. An academic saying "the How Many NZ analysis is broadly right but understates the problem" is better than silence.

**How to reach them:** Send a concise, well-sourced analysis to the relevant academic and ask for their view. Academics respond to people who show intellectual seriousness. "I've pulled together Stats NZ and OIA data on X. I'd welcome any corrections or context I might be missing." Many will ignore you. A few won't. A few is all you need.

**Housing and infrastructure advocates.** Infrastructure NZ, the Property Council, Community Housing Aotearoa, housing affordability campaigners. These organisations already know the numbers don't add up. They would welcome a voice making the population-infrastructure connection louder, because it supports their existing advocacy for more infrastructure spending.

**How to reach them:** Attend their public events. NZ's policy sector runs on breakfast seminars and lunchtime panels. Show up. Ask a smart question. Follow up with an email. "Good event this morning. I've been pulling together some data on population growth vs infrastructure capacity that connects to what your speaker was saying. Happy to share." In a small city like Wellington, showing up in person three or four times makes you a known face.

**Healthcare workers, teachers, and frontline public servants.** People who experience the infrastructure deficit daily. The ED nurse who knows the hospital was built for a smaller city. The principal who's running out of classrooms. The GP whose practice is closed to new patients. These people are credible witnesses to the problem. Their voices carry weight that no amount of data can match.

**How to reach them:** This is where social media does its job. Your data posts on health, education, and housing will find these people organically. When a nurse replies to your hospital capacity post saying "this is exactly what we're seeing in our ED," that's the opening. Engage, ask if they'd be willing to share more (anonymously if needed), and build the relationship. Over time, you develop a small network of frontline voices you can call on.

**Sympathetic journalists beyond Tier 1.** Thomas Coughlan, Andrea Vance, Henry Cooke, and the other gallery journalists identified in the ops document. These people are Tier 2 because you approach them later, after you have credibility from Tier 1 engagement.

**What you're asking of Tier 2:** Academics: engage with the data publicly. Housing advocates: cite the population-infrastructure link in their own work. Frontline workers: share their experience (with consent) as evidence of the lived reality behind the numbers. Journalists: cover the story. The ask for each group is different, but the underlying principle is the same: you're giving them something useful and asking them to use it in their own way.

### Tier 3: Helpful (months 3-6)

**Who they are:** The broader base of engaged citizens who give the campaign mass and make MPs feel constituent pressure.

**Small business owners in high-growth areas** who see the infrastructure strain daily. The cafe owner in Mt Roskill whose staff can't afford to live nearby. The tradesperson in Tauranga who's quoting six-month waits because there aren't enough workers, roads, or materials. These people talk to their communities. Their word-of-mouth is more trusted than any media outlet.

**How to reach them:** Your local-impact content (the Tauranga hospital post, the Auckland housing ratio) finds these people. Local Facebook groups are the distribution channel. When someone shares your content in a community group and a local business owner engages, that's your signal. Follow up. Ask them to share their experience.

**Engaged citizens in swing electorates.** The specific electorates where MPs' margins are thin and constituent pressure actually changes behaviour. Auckland seats, Hamilton, Tauranga, provincial seats where population growth is felt but not planned for.

**How to reach them:** The local-impact content strategy does double duty here. Posts about specific regions attract people from those regions. When they engage, you give them a specific, local action: attend your electorate clinic, ask your MP this question, submit on this bill.

**Young professionals and renters.** The generation locked out of housing. They feel the population-infrastructure gap more intensely than anyone. Many of them are politically disengaged because they feel powerless. The campaign offers them a frame that explains their lived experience and a channel for frustration that isn't just venting.

**How to reach them:** TikTok and Instagram Reels for initial reach. Short-form content built around the housing ratio (net migration vs houses consented) and the generational fairness angle. These platforms skew young and the content format suits the campaign's data-first approach.

**What you're asking of Tier 3:** Small actions that compound. Share this post. Email your MP this question. Show up at this electorate meeting. Fill in this select committee submission (with a template you've provided). The ask is always specific, always small, and always comes with everything they need to complete it.

## 2. THE ALLY RECRUITMENT PLAN

### The Core Principle

You don't recruit allies by asking for help. You recruit them by being useful. Every relationship starts with you giving something: data they can use, analysis that supports their existing position, a frame that makes their own argument stronger. The ask comes later, and it comes naturally because you've already established mutual value.

### Approach by Sector

**Academics.** Academics care about rigour and hate being associated with anything that could damage their reputation. Approach: send them your data with a genuine request for feedback. "I've been pulling together OIA responses on infrastructure spending vs population growth. I'd appreciate your expert eye on whether my methodology holds up." If they engage, even to correct you, you have a relationship. A correction is actually valuable because it lets you say publicly "we updated our analysis based on feedback from [Professor X at the University of Auckland]." That's credibility building, not a setback.

What you offer them: a public audience for their expertise that academic publishing doesn't provide. Most academics working on housing, infrastructure, or demographics are frustrated that their research doesn't reach the public. You're a bridge between their work and a politically engaged audience.

What you ask of them: cite your data in their own public commentary. Appear on your platform (if you develop one) to explain their research. Provide expert validation when journalists ask "is this data credible?"

**Local body politicians.** Councillors and mayors in high-growth areas are fighting the infrastructure-population battle every day in resource consent hearings, long-term plan consultations, and budget meetings. They have data you don't have (local infrastructure assessments, development contributions, three-waters capacity models). You have data they don't have (national migration numbers, OIA responses, comparative analysis across regions).

What you offer them: national-level data and media attention for what they already know locally. A councillor in Tauranga saying "our wastewater system can't handle current growth" is a local story. That same councillor citing How Many NZ data showing this is happening in every high-growth area in the country is a national story.

What you ask of them: share your analysis through their own channels. Cite the population-infrastructure link in council meetings. Introduce you to journalists who cover their region.

**Housing advocates.** Community Housing Aotearoa, housing affordability campaigners, tenant advocacy groups. These organisations want more housing built. You want population growth planned for. The arguments are complementary: if you're adding 100,000 people and building 30,000 houses, both groups have a case to make.

What you offer them: a complementary frame that strengthens their demand for more housing by showing why housing supply can never catch up under current population settings. You're not competing with them. You're providing the demand-side data that makes their supply-side argument more urgent.

What you ask of them: co-amplification. When you post housing-population data, they share it. When they post housing supply analysis, you share it. Parallel voices reinforcing the same conclusion from different angles.

**Healthcare workers and teachers.** These are your most powerful allies because they speak from lived experience, not ideology. A nurse saying "we don't have enough beds" is unchallengeable. A teacher saying "we're running out of classrooms" is unchallengeable. Their authority comes from doing the job, and no politician or commentator can dismiss that without looking callous.

What you offer them: an anonymous (if needed) channel to share what they're seeing. Many frontline workers feel gagged by professional obligations or fear of employer reprisals. You can share their testimony without identifying them, or you can share aggregate data that proves what they're experiencing individually.

What you ask of them: permission to share their stories (anonymised as needed). Willingness to appear (even anonymously) when a journalist asks "is this real?" Sharing your content through their own professional networks.

**Small business owners.** Particularly in construction, hospitality, retail, and healthcare -- sectors where population growth is both an opportunity and a strain. The builder who can't hire enough workers. The restaurant owner whose staff can't afford rent nearby. The GP practice that's turned away patients.

What you offer them: a frame that connects their daily frustrations to a systemic cause. Most small business owners feel the infrastructure strain but don't connect it to immigration policy because the conversation is taboo. You give them permission and a vocabulary.

What you ask of them: share the content in their business and community networks. Raise the question with their local MP. Participate in local media if asked.

**Concerned citizens.** The largest group numerically and the hardest to identify in advance. These are the people who discover your content organically, feel recognition, and want to do something. The suburban parent in Auckland. The retiree in Tauranga. The young renter in Wellington.

What you offer them: an identity (the Responsible Kiwi) and a channel for their concern that doesn't make them feel like a racist or an extremist. You give them the data, the language, and the actions.

What you ask of them: start with sharing. Then emailing their MP. Then attending an electorate clinic. Then submitting on a select committee bill. Each action is one step bigger than the last, and each comes only after they've completed the previous one.

## 3. THE JOURNALIST CULTIVATION PLAYBOOK

### Building from Cold

In NZ's media ecosystem, there are about 15-20 journalists who matter. You can realistically build a working relationship with all of them within six months. Here's how.

**Step 1: Be useful before you make contact.** Before you ever email a journalist, have at least three pieces of published, data-sourced content live. This is your portfolio. When a journalist googles you (and they will), they should find rigorous, well-sourced analysis that looks like it was produced by someone serious. First impressions in NZ's small media world are permanent. If they look you up and find low-quality content or ideological ranting, you're done before you start.

**Step 2: The first contact.** Email, not DM. Journalists in NZ's gallery receive DMs from every crank with a conspiracy theory. Email signals seriousness.

The email should be three paragraphs maximum. Paragraph one: who you are in one sentence ("I run How Many NZ, tracking population growth versus infrastructure capacity"). Paragraph two: what you're sending and why it's relevant to their beat, with the actual data attached or linked ("I've just received an OIA response from MBIE showing [specific finding]. Given your recent coverage of [specific piece they wrote], I thought you might find this useful"). Paragraph three: no ask. Just availability ("Happy to discuss if you'd like more context, but no pressure at all. I'll keep sending things through when I think they're relevant to your work").

Do not ask them to write a story. Do not ask them to follow you. Do not ask for anything. You are providing a service. You are a source, not a supplicant.

**Step 3: The follow-up cadence.** After the first email, wait for a natural reason to follow up. That might be two weeks or two months. The trigger should always be: you have something new and relevant. A fresh OIA response. A new data analysis. A Stats NZ release that you've contextualised. Never follow up to ask "did you see my email?" That marks you as needy.

The cadence is roughly: one email every 2-4 weeks, always with new material, always relevant to their specific beat. Thomas Coughlan gets the policy-political analysis. Andrea Vance gets the investigative OIA findings. Jenee Tibshraeny gets the housing-economy data. You're not blasting the same thing to everyone. You're curating for each journalist.

**Step 4: The pivot from source to trusted source.** This happens when a journalist contacts you first. It might be: "Hey, Stats NZ just released the quarterly migration numbers. Do you have any quick analysis?" That's the moment. You respond within the hour with a clean, quotable analysis and your phone number. Speed and reliability are how you become a trusted source in NZ's gallery.

From this point, the relationship becomes reciprocal. They call you when they need context. You call them (sparingly) when you have something significant. The key is to never waste their time. Every contact should be genuinely useful to them. If you call a journalist with something that isn't a story, you've damaged the relationship. NZ's press gallery is small enough that word gets around.

**Step 5: Becoming part of the furniture.** After 3-4 months of consistent, reliable data provision, you are a known source. Gallery journalists talk to each other. If Coughlan has used your data and found it reliable, he'll mention it to Vance. If Tibshraeny cites you and nobody finds an error, the others notice. In NZ's small media world, a reputation for accuracy and reliability compounds fast.

At this point, you can start pitching stories (gently). Not "you should write about X" but "I've got something that might be worth a look." The pitch is always framed as a favour to them, not a request. "This OIA response shows something nobody's reported. Happy to give you first look if you're interested, otherwise I'll put it out on our channels."

### The Exclusive Economy

Exclusives are currency in NZ journalism. Offering a journalist first look at an OIA response or a data finding before anyone else sees it is the most powerful relationship-building tool you have. But use it carefully.

Give exclusives to one journalist at a time. Rotate them. If Coughlan got the last exclusive, Vance gets the next one. This prevents any single journalist from feeling they "own" you (which would signal partisan alignment) and ensures multiple journalists have a reason to maintain the relationship.

Never give an exclusive and then publish the same material yourself before their story runs. That's a relationship-ending betrayal in the gallery. If you give someone an exclusive, wait until their story is published before putting anything out on your own channels.

### When to Pitch a Story vs Just Provide Data

Provide data when you have interesting findings that are useful context but not necessarily a standalone story. "Here's our latest analysis of housing consents vs population growth by region." This is background. It builds the relationship and the journalist's understanding.

Pitch a story when you have a specific, newsworthy finding with a clear angle. "An OIA response shows no government agency is responsible for matching infrastructure to population growth." That's a story. "The Budget allocated $X for hospital beds while projecting population growth requiring $Y worth of beds." That's a story.

The dividing line: if a news editor would run it as a headline, pitch it. If it's supporting evidence for a broader argument, provide it as context.

## 4. THE COALITION WITHOUT AN ORGANISATION

### Why No Formal Organisation

In NZ, formal organisations attract three responses that are all bad for this campaign. First, "who's funding it?" triggers the astroturfing suspicion. Second, "who appointed them?" triggers tall poppy syndrome. Third, "they're just a lobby group" provides politicians with an easy dismissal. A formal organisation with a name, a structure, and a spokesperson is a sitting target.

What works instead is a loose network of voices saying similar things independently. This looks organic because it is organic. No one is coordinating. No one is being directed. Multiple people have simply looked at the same data and reached similar conclusions. That's not a campaign. That's a consensus emerging.

### How to Create This Effect

**Step 1: Seed the frame, not the message.** Don't give people talking points. Give them data and a frame. The frame is: how many people are we adding, and what's the plan to house and serve them? Once people have internalised this frame, they express it in their own words, through their own channels, to their own audiences. A councillor in Tauranga doesn't need to say what How Many NZ says. They say what a Tauranga councillor would say, using the same underlying data. That's more credible than coordination and more powerful than any single voice.

**Step 2: Create shareable assets, not branded content.** The most useful thing you can produce is a chart, a ratio, or a data point that other people cite without necessarily crediting you. If the "net migration per new house consented" ratio starts appearing in opinion pieces, council reports, and MP speeches without your name attached, you've won. You've created a meme (in the Dawkins sense) that propagates independently. The brand gets credit eventually, but the measure of success is when the frame travels without you.

**Step 3: Cross-pollinate without coordinating.** When Hickey writes about housing and population, share it. When Reddell blogs about immigration and productivity, share it. When a councillor mentions infrastructure strain, amplify them. When a nurse tweets about ED overcrowding, connect it to the numbers. You're the node that links these separate conversations into a visible pattern. You never need to say "we're all on the same side." The audience sees the pattern themselves.

**Step 4: Use different voices for different audiences.** The academic speaks to the policy class. The nurse speaks to suburban parents. The small business owner speaks to National voters. The environmental scientist speaks to Green voters. The Maori voice (if one emerges organically, which it must, because you cannot recruit or create this) speaks to the Treaty dimension. You, as How Many NZ, connect them all through data. But each voice reaches an audience the others can't.

### The NZ-Specific Approach to Looking Like a Movement

In NZ, a movement that looks like a movement is immediately suspected of being manufactured. The cultural immune system asks: "Who's organising this? What's the agenda? Why is this so polished?"

The antidote is visible imperfection and genuine independence. Allied voices disagree with each other on specifics. The academic thinks the campaign should focus on productivity. The housing advocate thinks it should focus on supply. The nurse doesn't care about economics and just wants more hospital beds. These disagreements are features, not bugs. They prove nobody is reading from a script.

When multiple independent voices converge on the same basic question ("how many, and what's the plan?"), it looks like a genuine consensus emerging from different perspectives. That's much harder to dismiss than a coordinated campaign.

The practical coordination happens through lightweight, invisible channels. A Signal group with 15-20 key allies. No formal name. No minutes. No agenda. Just a place to share "heads up, Stats NZ numbers out tomorrow, here's the context" or "Coughlan is writing on immigration this week, might be worth engaging." This is how every effective loose coalition in NZ actually works. The Marriage Equality campaign, the End of Life Choice campaign, and the anti-TPPA movement all operated through informal networks of this kind.

## 5. THE MP ENGAGEMENT PATH

### The Ladder from Nobody to Someone They Listen To

In NZ's Parliament, there are 120 MPs. Most of them are accessible in ways that would astonish people from larger countries. The path from "random person" to "someone this MP pays attention to" has five rungs.

**Rung 1: Written contact.** Email the electorate office, not the parliamentary office. Electorate offices are staffed by people whose job is constituent relations. Parliamentary offices are staffed by people whose job is to manage the MP's Wellington schedule. Your email should be short (under 200 words), reference the specific electorate ("As a resident of [electorate] / As someone who works in [electorate]"), include one specific data point, and end with one specific question. "I'd like to know what [MP name]'s position is on whether New Zealand should have a population plan that links migration to infrastructure capacity."

The email will get a form reply or a brief response. That's fine. You're now on the record.

**Rung 2: The electorate clinic.** Most NZ electorate MPs hold regular clinics or constituent meetings. These are listed on their websites or available by calling the electorate office. Showing up in person changes the dynamic completely. You're no longer an email. You're a face. Bring a one-page summary of your data. Be respectful, be concise, and leave the document with them. The conversation should last five minutes, not fifteen. You're planting a seed, not delivering a lecture.

What to say: "I've been looking at population growth versus infrastructure in [local area] and the numbers are concerning. I've put together some data and I'd like to leave it with you. I'm not asking you to agree with me. I'm asking whether your party has a position on population planning."

**Rung 3: Select committee submissions.** When a relevant bill comes before a select committee, submit on it and request to appear in person. Oral submissions put you in a room with MPs who are required to listen to you. This is an astonishing level of access for a solo operator. You get 10-15 minutes to present, and then MPs ask you questions. Those questions tell you what's on their mind.

Prepare meticulously. Know the bill. Know which MPs are on the committee. Anticipate their questions. Bring printed copies of your data for each committee member. Be factual, be concise, and be respectful. After you appear, you are on the parliamentary record. That changes your status permanently.

**Rung 4: The direct relationship.** After an electorate visit and a select committee appearance, you have grounds to email the MP directly. "Thanks for your questions at the select committee last month. I have some new OIA data that's relevant to what we discussed. Happy to send it through." You're now a known source providing useful material. Some MPs will never engage. Others will. You only need a few.

For backbench MPs who are looking for a profile issue, you're offering them something valuable: a data-rich, defensible topic that distinguishes them from their colleagues. A National backbencher who starts asking pointed questions about infrastructure and population growth using your data gets media attention for being "serious about policy." That's an incentive for them to maintain the relationship.

**Rung 5: The informal advisor.** This is rare and takes months or years, but it happens in NZ because the country is small. An MP who has found your data useful multiple times, who has cited you in Parliament, and who has seen that you're reliable and non-partisan may start calling you before a major debate or policy announcement. "What does the data say about X?" At this point, you have influence. Not formal, not public, not acknowledged. Just the quiet influence of being the person who provides the numbers.

### Who to Target

Don't try to engage all 120 MPs. Focus on:

Opposition MPs who need ammunition. Labour MPs who want to hold the government accountable on housing. NZ First MPs who want immigration data for their existing positioning. ACT MPs who want fiscal cost analysis.

Backbenchers looking for a profile issue. Every Parliament has backbench MPs who are looking for an issue to own. Population planning is perfect: it's data-rich, it's cross-partisan, and it's undercovered.

MPs on the Finance and Expenditure Committee and the Education and Workforce Committee. These committees handle the legislation and budgets most relevant to population and immigration.

MPs in high-growth electorates. MPs whose constituents are feeling infrastructure strain have a personal political incentive to engage.

### The Parliamentary Question Pipeline

One of the most powerful tools available to a solo operator is drafting written parliamentary questions for sympathetic MPs to lodge. The MP gets a useful question. You get an official answer from the Minister on the parliamentary record. The answer (or the non-answer) becomes content.

Draft the questions in the exact format Parliament requires. Include a one-paragraph briefing note explaining what you expect the answer to reveal and why it matters. Make it so easy that the MP's staff just have to forward it. If you consistently provide good questions that generate newsworthy answers, MPs will keep lodging them.

## 6. THE ACTIVATION PLAYBOOK

### When you need the network to do something specific, here's how.

### The Pre-Conditions for Activation

You can only activate a network that exists. Activation without relationship is spam. The first three to four months are about building, not activating. You're depositing into the relationship account. When you make a withdrawal (asking people to do something), the balance needs to be positive.

### The Activation Hierarchy

**Level 1: Share (lowest friction).** You need people to amplify a specific piece of content. This is the easiest ask and should be used most frequently.

Channel: Post the content with an explicit but casual ask. "If this is useful, share it with someone who should see it." Not "please retweet" (which sounds needy in NZ) but a soft invitation. For your inner circle (the Signal group), a direct message: "This one's important. Would appreciate it getting around."

Lead time: None needed. The content should be strong enough to share on its own merits.

Messaging: The content is the message. If you have to explain why people should share it, the content isn't good enough.

**Level 2: Email your MP (medium friction).** You need people to send a specific question or message to their electorate MP.

Channel: Post a template with a link to the "Find your MP" page on the Parliament website. Make it copy-paste simple. Provide the question, provide the link, tell them it takes 90 seconds. Follow up 24 hours later with a count: "147 people sent this question yesterday."

Lead time: Publish the template at least 48 hours before any deadline. People need time to decide, overcome inertia, and act.

Messaging: "Your MP works for you. This is one question. It takes 90 seconds. Here it is." Frame the action as small, normal, and civic. Not as protest. Not as activism. As what citizens do.

**Level 3: Submit on a select committee bill (higher friction).** You need people to make a submission on a piece of legislation.

Channel: Produce a submission guide. Not a template (select committees can tell when they receive 200 identical submissions and give them less weight), but a guide that gives people the key arguments, the relevant data points, and the format requirements. Tell them the three things to cover and let them write it in their own words.

Lead time: Minimum two weeks before the submission deadline. People need time to read the guide, draft their submission, and feel it's their own.

Messaging: "Select committees read every submission. This is how you get your voice into Parliament. It takes 30 minutes. Here's everything you need." Include a note that they can request to appear in person, and explain how.

**Level 4: Show up (highest friction).** You need people to attend a select committee hearing, an electorate clinic, or a public meeting.

Channel: Direct communication to people you've already identified as willing to take action. Not a broadcast ask. This is where the Signal group and individual relationships matter. "The Education and Workforce Committee is hearing submissions on [bill] next Thursday at 10am in Wellington. If you're in Wellington and can attend, your presence matters. Even just sitting in the public gallery shows the committee this issue has public attention."

Lead time: Minimum one week, ideally two. People need to arrange their schedules.

Messaging: Be honest about why it matters. "Six people in the gallery looks like nobody cares. Thirty people looks like an issue the committee needs to take seriously. Every person visible matters." Give logistics: where, when, how to get in, what to expect.

### The Activation Cadence

Do not activate frequently. Every activation spends social capital. If you ask your network to do something every week, they'll stop responding by month two. Reserve activation for moments that actually matter.

The rough cadence should be: one major activation per month maximum during the build phase (months 1-4), increasing to two or three per month as the election approaches (months 5-7). Each activation should feel earned and significant, not routine.

### The Feedback Loop

This is where most campaigns fail. They activate, people act, and then silence. The person who emailed their MP never hears whether it mattered. The person who submitted to the select committee never learns what happened. They feel like they shouted into a void, and they never act again.

Close the loop every time. "234 people sent the question to their MPs. Here's what we've heard back so far. Three MPs have responded with substantive answers. Seven sent form replies. The rest haven't responded. We'll follow up in two weeks." Then actually follow up in two weeks.

When an action produces a visible result, amplify it. "The Finance and Expenditure Committee received 89 submissions on [bill]. The chair acknowledged the volume of public interest. This was you." When an MP changes their position or raises the issue in Parliament after constituent pressure, make the connection explicit: "Last month, 300 people in [electorate] emailed [MP name] about population planning. This week, [MP name] raised population planning in a parliamentary question. That's how this works."

### What Channels to Use

For Tier 1 allies (Hickey, Reddell, journalists, MPs): direct email or Signal. Never public calls to action.

For Tier 2 allies (academics, advocates, frontline workers): a mix of direct messages and semi-public calls. The Signal group for coordination. Email for specific asks.

For Tier 3 (general supporters): social media posts, email newsletter if you build one. The activation must be entirely self-contained in the post: the ask, the template, the link, the instructions. Assume people will not click through to another page.

Platform specifics: X/Twitter reaches journalists and the politically engaged. Facebook reaches suburban parents and older voters. TikTok and Instagram reach younger voters. LinkedIn reaches the policy-professional class. You need to be on all four, but the activation ask should be the same across platforms, just formatted appropriately.

## Organiser's Review

Reading this back through the lens of three advocacy campaigns in small democracies, here is what I'd flag.

The sequencing is right. Tier 1 first, always. The temptation is to go broad immediately, especially on social media where follower counts feel like progress. Resist that. In Ireland, the marriage equality campaign's early months were spent in living rooms, not on billboards. The same principle applies here. Your first three months should feel private.

The journalist approach is realistic for NZ. I'd add one thing: when you get your first media mention, don't celebrate publicly. Don't post "we were in the Herald today!" That triggers tall poppy syndrome and makes journalists feel used. Let others notice it. Let it circulate on its own. The most you should say is "good piece by [journalist] today on population planning" with a link. Let the audience connect the dots.

The MP engagement path accurately reflects how NZ Parliament works. The one addition I'd make: select committee clerks are the unsung allies of the process. They're helpful, knowledgeable, and genuinely committed to public participation. A quick phone call to the clerk's office before submitting ("I'm planning to submit on [bill]. Is there anything I should know about what the committee is particularly interested in?") can dramatically improve your submission's relevance.

The coalition-without-an-organisation model is exactly how the End of Life Choice referendum campaign worked. Multiple independent voices. No central organisation directing them. A shared frame ("compassion and choice") that everyone used in their own way. The opponents couldn't attack the movement because there was no single target. There was just a growing number of people saying the same thing in their own words. That's what you're building here.

One caution about activation: NZ's small population means your supporter numbers will always look small in absolute terms. Don't quote numbers if they're unimpressive. "47 people submitted" sounds pathetic on social media. "Dozens of New Zealanders submitted, including healthcare workers, teachers, and local body politicians" sounds like a movement. Frame for quality and breadth of support, not for headcount.

Final point. The entire network strategy rests on one thing: the quality of your data. In a small country where everyone is two degrees of separation from everyone else, reputation travels fast in both directions. One bad number and you're finished. One unsourced claim and nobody trusts you again. Your network is only as strong as the data that holds it together. Get the numbers right. Check everything twice. Source everything. If you're not sure, say you're not sure. That honesty is what makes people trust you, and trust is the only currency you have.
