# Wave 1: Strategic Framing Architecture

## A Cognitive Linguistics Analysis for the How Many NZ Campaign

*Prepared March 2026. Designed for a zero-budget, solo-operated campaign targeting the 7 November 2026 general election.*

---

## 1. THE MASTER FRAME: "The Unprepared Country"

### The Cognitive Architecture

The master frame for this campaign is **negligent stewardship**. Not "too many immigrants." Not "protect our culture." The frame is: *New Zealand's government is growing the population without preparing the country to handle it.* The moral failure is negligence, not openness.

In Lakoff's terms, this frame draws on a conceptual metaphor that operates beneath conscious awareness: **THE COUNTRY IS A HOUSEHOLD.** Every New Zealander intuitively understands that if you invite more people to live in your house, you need more bedrooms, more food, a bigger water heater. The government has been inviting people without building the bedrooms. That is not generosity. That is irresponsibility.

This metaphor system is powerful because it does not require anyone to feel hostile toward the people who arrived. The problem is not the guests. The problem is the host who didn't prepare. The anger flows upward toward government, not sideways toward migrants.

### The Underlying Moral Logic

Lakoff's framework identifies two competing moral systems in politics: the Strict Father (conservative) and the Nurturant Parent (progressive). The genius of the negligent stewardship frame is that it activates values from *both* moral systems simultaneously, which is why it can reach across the political spectrum.

From the Strict Father frame, it activates: **responsibility, planning, consequences for failure, accountability.** A responsible head of household plans before expanding the family. The government failed to plan. That is a moral failing the centre-right intuitively grasps.

From the Nurturant Parent frame, it activates: **care, protection, ensuring wellbeing for everyone including newcomers.** Bringing people into a country without adequate housing, healthcare, or infrastructure is not compassionate. It is neglect -- of existing residents *and* new arrivals. The centre-left can grasp this without feeling they are betraying their values.

### What the Frame Makes Salient

The negligent stewardship frame foregrounds:

- The gap between population growth and infrastructure capacity
- Government failure to plan and invest
- The lived experience of housing costs, hospital waits, congested roads
- The absence of a stated population strategy (no target, no plan, no consultation)
- The question "who decided this?" -- the democratic deficit of population growth without mandate

### What the Frame Backgrounds

The frame deliberately pushes to the margins:

- The ethnic or cultural composition of migration (irrelevant to the frame)
- Individual migrants and their stories (they are not the problem)
- Whether migration is good or bad in the abstract (the frame sidesteps this entirely)
- Historical migration patterns (the frame is about the present failure)

### The Metaphor System in Detail

The master frame deploys three interlocking metaphors.

**Metaphor 1: THE COUNTRY IS A HOUSEHOLD.** You don't add family members without adding bedrooms. You don't throw a party for 130,000 without stocking the fridge. This is the intuitive backbone of the entire frame. It makes complex policy visceral and domestic.

**Metaphor 2: POPULATION GROWTH IS A JOURNEY.** The government is "driving blind" -- accelerating without knowing the destination, without a map, without checking whether the road can handle the traffic. This metaphor activates fear of loss of control and the need for direction. "Where are we going?" and "Who's steering?" are questions that flow naturally from it.

**Metaphor 3: INFRASTRUCTURE IS A FOUNDATION.** You cannot build a house on sand. You cannot grow a population on crumbling hospitals and unbuilt roads. This metaphor makes the sequence clear: foundation first, then growth. The government reversed the sequence. That is structural negligence.

### The Frame in One Sentence

*The government is building a bigger country without building the foundations to support it -- and without asking you.*

This sentence contains all three metaphors (household/building, journey/direction, foundation/structure) and adds the democratic accountability dimension ("without asking you"). It works as a TikTok caption, as an op-ed thesis, and as a response to any journalist's question.

## 2. SUB-FRAMES FOR DIFFERENT AUDIENCES

The master frame is the trunk. These sub-frames are the branches. Each one takes the core logic of negligent stewardship and connects it to the specific concerns of a target audience.

### 2a. Centre-Right Voters: "The Planning Failure"

**Core concern:** Economic mismanagement, housing affordability, government incompetence.

**Sub-frame:** This is a planning failure by government. No business would double its workforce without expanding its office space, its IT systems, its supply chain. The government has doubled population growth without expanding the infrastructure to match. This is the kind of incompetence that would get a CEO fired.

**Values activated:** Responsibility, fiscal discipline, competence, consequences for failure. The Strict Father morality is strong here: the government broke the rules of responsible management and should be held accountable.

**Language:** "planning failure," "no business would operate like this," "accountability," "what's the plan?", "population Ponzi scheme" (use sparingly -- it's provocative but highly shareable).

**Content examples:** Compare net migration numbers with housing consents. Show the gap as a chart. "Last year NZ added X people and consented Y houses. Do the maths." Compare NZ's population planning with Australia's, Canada's, Singapore's. "Every serious country has a population strategy. We don't. Why?"

### 2b. Centre-Left Voters: "The Broken Promise"

**Core concern:** Wages, public services, worker exploitation, inequality.

**Sub-frame:** High immigration without planning hurts the most vulnerable. It suppresses wages in sectors where working-class Kiwis compete for jobs. It overloads hospitals and schools that low-income families depend on. And it hurts migrants themselves, who arrive to find overcrowded housing, exploitative employers, and a healthcare system that can't serve them. This isn't compassion. It's managed neglect.

**Values activated:** Fairness, protection of the vulnerable, empathy for workers, genuine (not performative) care for migrants. The Nurturant Parent morality is central: a caring government would ensure everyone is looked after before growing the numbers.

**Language:** "managed neglect," "the people who pay the price," "who benefits?", "wage suppression," "exploited workers -- migrant and local," "this isn't compassion, it's abandonment."

**Content examples:** Data on wage growth (or stagnation) in migrant-heavy sectors. Stories of migrant exploitation. Hospital wait times in low-income areas. The gap between what the government promises migrants and what they actually experience. "We tell skilled migrants they're coming to a land of opportunity. They arrive to a rental crisis and a three-month wait to see a GP."

### 2c. Maori / Tangata Whenua: "The Unconsulted"

**Core concern:** Treaty obligations, self-determination, Maori resource allocation, political dilution.

**Sub-frame:** As tangata whenua, Maori have never been consulted about the rate of population growth on these islands. The Treaty of Waitangi guarantees Maori rangatiratanga (self-determination) and a partnership in governance. Population policy -- which determines the future composition and resource demands of the country -- is a Treaty matter. Yet there has been no consultation, no discussion, no recognition that Maori have a unique standing in this conversation.

**Values activated:** Tino rangatiratanga (sovereignty/self-determination), kaitiakitanga (guardianship), manaakitanga (the obligation of proper hosting -- which requires preparation), whakapapa (the deep connection to place that gives Maori unique standing).

**Language:** "tangata whenua were never asked," "this is a Treaty issue," "kaitiakitanga means planning for the future," "population policy without Maori consultation is a Treaty breach," "manaakitanga requires preparation."

**Content examples:** OIA requests about whether any immigration policy briefings included Treaty analysis. Data on Maori housing outcomes relative to population growth. Interviews or quotes from Maori voices who have raised population concerns. The question: "Has the Crown consulted iwi on population growth targets? If not, why not?"

**Critical note:** This sub-frame should only be deployed if the campaign has genuine Maori engagement or support. A Pakeha solo operator using Maori framing without Maori backing will be seen as appropriation and will backfire. If the operator is Pakeha, the move is to raise the question ("has the Crown consulted Maori on this?") rather than to speak for Maori.

### 2d. Young People / Locked-Out Generation: "The Betrayal"

**Core concern:** Housing affordability, inability to buy a first home, feeling that the system is rigged against them.

**Sub-frame:** Your parents bought a house on a single income. You can't buy one on two incomes. That's not because you're lazy or because avocado toast is expensive. It's because the government added hundreds of thousands of people to the country without building the houses, and that drove prices beyond your reach. They grew the demand and didn't grow the supply. You're paying the price.

**Values activated:** Fairness (generational equity), anger at a rigged system, betrayal by those who were supposed to look out for you. This is Nurturant Parent morality with a sharp edge: the parent failed the child.

**Language:** "locked out," "your future, their failure," "they grew the demand without building the supply," "generational betrayal," "who decided your house should cost a million dollars?"

**Content examples:** House price to income ratios over time, overlaid with net migration. The number of houses needed per 100,000 net migrants versus the number actually built. Comparisons with what housing cost when the viewer's parents were their age, adjusted for migration-driven demand. Short, punchy TikTok content: "In 1990, the median house cost 3x the median income. Now it costs 9x. What changed? We added a million people and didn't build the houses."

### 2e. Regional / Provincial New Zealand: "The Forgotten"

**Core concern:** Feeling ignored by Wellington, declining local services, the sense that cities get everything and the regions get nothing.

**Sub-frame:** When the government grows the population, the new arrivals go to Auckland, Wellington, and Christchurch. But the resources to serve them get pulled from everywhere else. Your rural hospital loses a doctor to Auckland. Your local school can't fill a teaching position because teachers go where the population is. Provincial NZ is subsidising the growth of the cities without getting anything in return.

**Values activated:** Fairness (regional equity), the dignity of provincial life, resentment of Wellington/Auckland elitism, practical self-interest.

**Language:** "Wellington decided, the regions pay," "your hospital, their population growth," "provincial NZ is being hollowed out," "where did your GP go?"

**Content examples:** Doctor and nurse vacancy rates in provincial areas versus cities. School roll changes in regional versus metropolitan areas. Infrastructure spending per capita, regional versus metro. The argument that population growth concentrates resources in cities at the expense of everywhere else.

## 3. COUNTER-FRAME DEFENCE

Every frame generates predictable attacks. The strength of a frame is not just what it asserts but how it handles the counter-frames. Here are the three most dangerous attacks and the cognitive moves that neutralise each.

### 3a. "You're racist"

**Why this attack works:** It works because it reframes the entire conversation from policy to identity. Once someone is successfully labelled racist, nothing they say about data or infrastructure matters. The label becomes the frame, and everything is interpreted through it. In NZ's cultural context, where anti-racism norms are strong and the Christchurch attack is recent, this accusation carries more weight than almost anywhere else in the English-speaking world.

**The wrong defence:** Saying "I'm not racist." This fails because it accepts the opponent's frame. As Lakoff's first rule states: when you negate a frame, you activate it. "I'm not racist" forces the listener to think about racism, which is exactly what the attacker wants.

**The correct cognitive move: REFRAME TO NEGLIGENCE.** The response is not a denial. It is a redirect that makes the attacker's frame look like a deflection.

The line: "This isn't about who comes. It's about what's built when they get here. The government added 130,000 people and didn't build the hospitals, houses, or schools to support them. Calling that observation racist is how politicians avoid accountability."

The cognitive move here is threefold. First, it reasserts the master frame (negligent stewardship). Second, it reframes the racism accusation itself as a *tactic of evasion* by those in power. Third, it positions the accuser as complicit in the negligence: they're protecting the government from scrutiny by policing language instead of demanding better planning.

**Reinforcement lines:**
- "I'm talking about infrastructure. If you want to talk about race, that's your frame, not mine."
- "Calling out a planning failure isn't racist. Ignoring the consequences of that failure hurts everyone, including the migrants themselves."
- "Every migrant who arrives to find no affordable housing and a six-month GP wait is also a victim of this negligence. This isn't us versus them. It's everyone versus a government that didn't plan."

### 3b. "You're just NZ First"

**Why this attack works:** It works because it reduces you to a partisan actor. If you're "just NZ First," then people who dislike Winston Peters have permission to ignore you. It also implies you're not independent, which undermines credibility.

**The wrong defence:** Distancing from NZ First by attacking them. This alienates NZ First's base (who are potential allies) and makes you look like you're competing rather than advocating.

**The correct cognitive move: ELEVATE ABOVE PARTISANSHIP.** Position the campaign as holding ALL parties accountable, including NZ First.

The line: "I'm not aligned with any party. I'm asking every party the same question: what is your population plan? NZ First talks about immigration more than other parties, but they haven't published a specific target either. No party has. That's the problem."

The cognitive move: by demanding a specific answer from NZ First too, you demonstrate independence. You're not carrying water for Peters. You're holding everyone to the same standard. This is credible because it's true -- NZ First has rhetoric but not a published population target with infrastructure projections.

**Reinforcement lines:**
- "I'll praise any party that produces a real population plan and criticise any party that doesn't. That includes NZ First."
- "This isn't a partisan issue. Housing costs hurt National voters, Labour voters, Green voters, NZ First voters. The question is the same for all of them: what's the plan?"
- "If NZ First had a detailed population strategy with infrastructure commitments, I'd be the first to say so. They don't. Nobody does."

### 3c. "Immigrants contribute to the economy"

**Why this attack works:** It shifts the frame from infrastructure to economics, where the case for immigration is easier to make in aggregate terms (GDP growth, tax revenue, skills gaps). It also subtly implies that questioning immigration is questioning the value of immigrants as people.

**The wrong defence:** Arguing that immigrants don't contribute. This is factually wrong and morally ugly. It also accepts the opponent's frame and debates within it.

**The correct cognitive move: ACCEPT AND REDIRECT.** Don't contest the economic contribution. Absorb it and pivot to the gap between contribution and investment.

The line: "Of course migrants contribute. That's not the question. The question is: did the government invest the economic gains from population growth into the infrastructure those people need? They didn't. The economy grew and the hospitals, roads, and houses didn't. Where did the money go?"

The cognitive move: you accept the premise (immigrants contribute), which makes you look reasonable and not anti-immigrant. Then you redirect to the *real* failure: the government captured the economic upside of immigration (GDP growth, tax revenue) but didn't reinvest it in the infrastructure those additional people need. This is actually a stronger argument than "immigrants don't contribute," because it indicts government management rather than migrants themselves.

**Reinforcement lines:**
- "A growing population should mean a growing tax base. So why are hospital wait times getting longer, not shorter?"
- "GDP went up. House prices went up. But did your GP get more appointment slots? Did your commute get shorter? The growth went somewhere. It didn't go to you."
- "If migrants generate economic value -- and they do -- then the government has even less excuse for not building infrastructure. They had the revenue. They just didn't spend it."

## 4. KEY PHRASES AND LANGUAGE RULES

### Words and Phrases to Always Use

**"Population plan"** -- This is the core demand of the campaign. It implies that the absence of a plan is the problem, which is harder to argue against than any specific immigration number. It sounds technocratic and responsible. It activates the planning/responsibility values from the Strict Father frame while being inoffensive to progressives. Always "population plan," never "immigration target" (which sounds restrictionist).

**"Infrastructure gap"** -- This makes the problem concrete and measurable. A "gap" implies something that should be filled, which creates an implicit demand for action. It avoids abstract or emotional territory and stays on the solid ground of physical reality.

**"Planning failure"** -- This assigns blame to government without targeting any group of people. A "failure" implies there was a responsibility that was not met. It activates accountability frames.

**"Negligence"** -- Stronger than "failure." Negligence implies culpability, not just incompetence. It is a legal concept as well as a moral one, which gives it weight.

**"Who decided?"** -- This two-word question activates the democratic deficit frame. It implies that a decision was made (population growth happened) without consent. It turns every immigration statistic into a question about democracy.

**"Every serious country"** -- This phrase, used in comparisons ("every serious country has a population strategy"), implies that NZ's absence of a plan is not normal but aberrant. It activates national pride and the desire to be taken seriously.

**"Including new arrivals"** -- Always include migrants as also harmed by the planning failure. "This hurts everyone, including new arrivals who were promised a better life and arrived to a housing crisis." This neutralises the racism frame and demonstrates genuine concern for all residents.

**"Net migration"** -- Always use "net migration," not "immigration." Net migration is a statistical term. It sounds technical and data-driven. "Immigration" carries cultural and emotional freight that triggers defensive reactions.

**"Consent"** -- "Did New Zealanders consent to this level of population growth?" Consent is a powerful value word that activates both progressive (bodily autonomy, informed consent) and conservative (democratic mandate, sovereignty) moral frames.

### Words and Phrases to Never Use

**"Remigration"** -- Toxic in NZ. Immediately associated with white nationalism and European identitarian movements. In a country where Maori are tangata whenua, the concept collapses under its own contradictions. Using this word ends your credibility instantly.

**"Replacement"** -- As in "great replacement." Conspiracy theory framing that is actively dangerous in a post-Christchurch context. Using it would be both morally wrong and strategically suicidal.

**"Flood" / "swamped" / "invasion" / "wave"** -- Dehumanising water metaphors that frame people as a natural disaster. NZ's cultural norms reject this language. It also triggers the racism frame immediately and irreversibly.

**"Kiwis first" / "New Zealanders first"** -- Sounds like NZ First branding. Triggers the partisan frame. Also carries uncomfortable echoes of "America First" nationalism.

**"Foreigners"** -- Othering language. Use "new arrivals," "migrants," or simply "people." The frame positions the government as the problem, not the people who moved here.

**"Too many"** -- As in "too many immigrants." This phrase frames people as excess, which is dehumanising. Instead: "more people than we have infrastructure for." The problem is the infrastructure gap, not the people.

**"Our country" / "our culture"** -- Possessive framing that implies exclusion. In a Treaty context, "our country" immediately raises the question "whose country?" which leads to a Maori sovereignty argument you cannot win. Frame it as "the country" or "New Zealand" -- collective rather than possessive.

**"Illegal"** -- NZ doesn't have a significant illegal immigration issue comparable to the US or UK. Using this language imports a foreign frame that doesn't match NZ reality. It also sounds like you're borrowing American talking points, which triggers tall poppy syndrome and cultural cringe.

**"Crisis"** -- Use sparingly and only about infrastructure ("housing crisis," "infrastructure crisis"). Never "immigration crisis." Calling immigration itself a crisis frames the people as the emergency. Calling infrastructure a crisis frames the government's failure as the emergency.

### Why Each Matters Cognitively

Language is not a label placed on a pre-existing thought. Language activates the frame through which the thought is processed. When you say "flood of immigrants," the listener's brain activates the natural disaster frame: uncontrollable, destructive, something to be feared and defended against. The target of fear becomes the people. When you say "infrastructure gap from population growth," the listener's brain activates the engineering frame: a problem to be diagnosed, measured, and solved. The target of frustration becomes the government that failed to build.

This is not spin. It is how cognition works. The research from Lakoff and others in cognitive linguistics demonstrates that frames are not rhetorical decoration. They are the cognitive structures through which all reasoning occurs. You cannot reason outside a frame. You can only choose which frame to activate. Every word choice in this campaign either activates the frame you want (negligent stewardship, government accountability) or the frame your opponents want (racism, xenophobia, fear of the other). There is no neutral language.

## 5. THE EMOTIONAL ARCHITECTURE

### The Emotional Sequence

Political mobilisation follows a specific emotional pathway. Content should move people through these stages in order. Skipping stages or getting the sequence wrong produces either paralysis (too much fear without direction) or rejection (too much anger without validation).

**Stage 1: Recognition ("I'm not the only one thinking this")**

The first emotional need is validation. Most New Zealanders who are concerned about population growth and its effects have been self-censoring. They've internalised the idea that expressing concern about immigration makes them racist. The tall poppy syndrome compounds this: speaking up about a controversial topic feels dangerous.

The first wave of content must give people permission to feel what they already feel. The emotion here is *relief*. "Someone is saying what I've been thinking."

Content at this stage is data-driven and calm. Present the statistics without editorialising. Let the numbers do the work. "NZ added 130,000 people in one year. Housing consents in that period: X. Hospital beds added: Y." No commentary needed. The gap between the numbers is the argument.

The tone is: a thoughtful person who looked at the data and was surprised by what they found. Understated. Slightly puzzled. "Huh, that's a big gap."

**Stage 2: Frustration ("Why isn't anyone doing anything about this?")**

Once people feel validated in their concern, the next emotion is frustration at inaction. This is where you introduce the democratic deficit and accountability frames.

Content at this stage asks questions rather than making accusations. "I looked for any party's population plan. I couldn't find one. Did I miss it?" This is the "reluctant Kiwi" persona at work. You're not attacking. You're asking. But the questions are pointed.

The emotion is *rising frustration*. Not rage. Frustration. The difference matters. Rage alienates in NZ culture. Frustration is relatable.

Content: the scorecard (which party has a plan?), OIA responses showing the government has no strategy, comparisons with countries that do plan.

**Stage 3: Indignation ("They should have done better")**

This is where frustration transforms into moral judgment. The frame shifts from "nobody has a plan" to "they had a responsibility to have a plan and they failed." This is where "negligence" enters the vocabulary.

Content at this stage is more assertive. "The government knew the numbers. Treasury projected the infrastructure costs. They chose not to invest. That's not an accident. That's a choice."

The emotion is *moral clarity*. The audience now has a clear sense of who is responsible (the government) and what the failure was (choosing not to plan or invest). This is the stage where passive concern becomes active judgment.

**Stage 4: Agency ("What can we do?")**

Indignation without a channel becomes cynicism. The campaign must provide a specific, achievable action at the point where people are emotionally ready to act. This is where the "ask your MP" and candidate confrontation tactics deploy.

Content at this stage is action-oriented. "Here are five questions for your electorate MP. Ask them. Film the answer. Share it." The scorecard becomes a tool people can use, not just information they consume.

The emotion is *empowerment*. The shift from "this is terrible" to "I can do something about this" is the most important emotional transition in the entire campaign. Without it, all the earlier stages produce nothing but informed despair.

**Stage 5: Identity ("I am someone who cares about this")**

The final stage is when concern becomes part of how people see themselves. They are not just worried about housing or infrastructure. They are the kind of person who holds their government accountable on population planning. This is where a movement begins to form.

Content at this stage is community-building. Sharing other people's content, amplifying voices, creating the sense that this is a growing conversation rather than one person's campaign. "More and more New Zealanders are asking the same question: where's the population plan?"

The emotion is *belonging*. The audience is now part of something. They share the content not just because they agree with it but because sharing it is an expression of who they are.

### Emotional Guardrails

**Never trigger fear of the other.** Fear of migrants, fear of cultural change, fear of ethnic displacement -- these emotions are available and powerful, but they are strategically and morally off-limits. They trigger NZ's cultural immune response (racism accusation), they alienate the centre-left audience, and they are incompatible with the master frame (which blames government, not people). The moment content triggers fear of migrants rather than frustration with government, the campaign has failed.

**Never trigger hopelessness.** If the audience concludes "nothing will change," they disengage. Every piece of content about the problem should be paired with an implicit or explicit path to accountability. The problem is real but it is fixable. The fix is a population plan. The path to the fix is political pressure through the election.

**Anger should be cold, not hot.** Hot anger (shouting, insults, personal attacks) triggers NZ's tall poppy response and looks unhinged. Cold anger (precise, factual, controlled, slightly sardonic) is far more effective in NZ culture. Think of a parent who is not yelling but is clearly, quietly furious. That controlled intensity commands respect in Kiwi culture in a way that ranting never does.

**Humour is essential.** New Zealanders process difficult topics through dry, self-deprecating humour. Content that is relentlessly grim will burn out the audience. Occasional sardonic wit -- the kind that makes people laugh and then feel uncomfortable about laughing -- is powerful. "We added a city the size of Hamilton last year. Nobody told Hamilton." That kind of line.

## 6. INTEGRATION: HOW THE FRAME OPERATES IN PRACTICE

### The One-Paragraph Version

Everything in this document serves a single strategic purpose: making it impossible for the 2026 election to pass without every party being forced to answer the question "what is your population plan?" The master frame (negligent stewardship) provides the moral logic. The sub-frames connect that logic to every major voter group. The counter-frame defences protect against the predictable attacks. The language rules ensure every piece of content reinforces the right frame. The emotional architecture moves people from passive concern to active demand. The campaign succeeds when a journalist asks a party leader "what is your population plan?" on live television and the leader has to answer. Everything works backward from that moment.

### The Content Test

Before publishing any piece of content, apply this four-question test:

1. **Does it blame the government or does it blame migrants?** If the latter, kill it.
2. **Does it advance the "population plan" demand?** If not, does it at least reinforce the infrastructure gap frame?
3. **Would a centre-left voter share it without feeling uncomfortable?** If not, the tone or framing needs adjustment. Cross-partisan reach is the entire game.
4. **Does it contain data?** Every assertion should be traceable to a public source. Credibility is the only asset a zero-budget campaign has.

### The Persona Test

The campaign persona is a "reluctant Kiwi" -- someone who didn't want to get into this but felt compelled by what the data showed. Before publishing, ask: does this sound like a concerned citizen with a spreadsheet, or does it sound like an activist with an agenda? The former has permission to speak in NZ culture. The latter triggers the immune system.

## APPENDIX: SELF-CRITIQUE AND REVISIONS

*What follows is a critical review of the above document, conducted as if grading a doctoral thesis in political communication, with revisions applied.*

### Weakness 1: The Treaty Sub-Frame Risks Instrumentalisation

**Original problem:** The Maori sub-frame (section 2c) risked treating Maori perspectives as a tactical resource for a campaign rather than as a genuine political concern with its own integrity. A Pakeha operator deploying Treaty language to bolster an immigration argument would likely be read as cynical appropriation.

**Revision applied:** Section 2c now includes an explicit warning that this sub-frame should only be deployed with genuine Maori engagement, and that a Pakeha operator should raise the consultation question rather than speak for Maori. The frame is available but gated behind a legitimacy requirement.

### Weakness 2: Insufficient Attention to the NZ First Overlap Problem

**Original problem:** The document didn't adequately address the risk that the campaign would be perceived as, or actually function as, a NZ First front operation regardless of the operator's intent. In NZ's small political ecosystem, any immigration-focused campaign will be mapped onto the NZ First axis by default.

**Revision applied:** The counter-frame defence in section 3b was strengthened with the specific tactic of publicly grading NZ First alongside other parties, demanding a specific population target from them as well. The campaign must be visibly willing to criticise NZ First for lacking specificity. This is both strategically sound (it demonstrates independence) and factually warranted (NZ First does not have a published population target with infrastructure projections).

### Weakness 3: The Emotional Architecture Assumed Linear Progression

**Original problem:** The five-stage emotional sequence assumed audiences would encounter the campaign's content in order. In reality, social media means people will encounter content from any stage at any time. Someone might see a Stage 4 action post before they've seen any Stage 1 validation content.

**Response:** This is a genuine limitation of any emotional sequence model applied to social media content. The mitigation is to ensure that every piece of content, regardless of its primary emotional stage, contains at least a trace of Stage 1 (data that validates the concern). The pinned post or bio link should always lead to the foundational data. Every action post should include the core statistic. This way, any entry point provides enough Stage 1 grounding to make the rest comprehensible.

### Weakness 4: Under-Specified Measurement of Frame Adoption

**Original problem:** The document described what success looks like ("a journalist asks about the population plan on live television") but didn't specify intermediate indicators of frame adoption.

**Addition:** Intermediate indicators that the frame is working include: (a) journalists using the phrase "population plan" or "infrastructure gap" in their own coverage without attributing it to the campaign; (b) politicians adopting the frame's language in parliamentary debate or media appearances; (c) other commentators and accounts using the campaign's specific data points and comparisons; (d) the question "what's your population plan?" appearing in audience questions at political events without the campaign's direct involvement. Each of these represents the frame propagating beyond the campaign itself, which is the definition of successful framing.

### Weakness 5: The Document Could Be More Honest About Failure Modes

**Addition:** The most likely failure mode is not that the frame is wrong but that the campaign never achieves sufficient reach for the frame to propagate. Framing only matters if people encounter it. A perfectly designed frame that reaches 500 people has no political impact. The bottleneck is distribution, not architecture. This document provides the cognitive infrastructure, but without the execution described in the companion expert documents (consistent content production, OIA pipeline, journalist engagement, Claude Code automation), the frame remains theoretical. The frame is necessary but not sufficient. Execution is the other half.

---

*This framing architecture is designed to be read alongside the campaign's operational plans. The frame without execution is academic. Execution without the frame is noise. Together they form a campaign that can punch far above its weight in a small country where a handful of journalists, a few thousand engaged citizens, and one persistent voice can shift what an entire election is about.*
