# NZ Operational Playbook: Forcing Immigration onto the 2026 Election Agenda

## How Things Actually Work in New Zealand

This is the operational reality check. The other experts have given strategic advice. This document is about the specific people, processes, dates, and cultural dynamics that determine whether anything actually happens in NZ politics.

## 1. THE NZ JOURNALIST MAP

These are the people who determine whether immigration becomes an election issue. In NZ's concentrated media market, there are roughly 15-20 journalists and commentators whose coverage sets the agenda. If they're not writing about it, it's not on the agenda. Full stop.

### Tier 1: The Agenda Setters

These are the journalists whose coverage directly shapes what politicians talk about. Getting your data into their hands is the single highest-value activity in this campaign.

**Thomas Coughlan** -- Political Editor, NZ Herald. @ThomasCoughworx on X. Coughlan is the most important political journalist in the country right now. He's young, sharp, data-literate, and covers the intersection of economics and politics better than anyone in the gallery. He's written on housing, infrastructure, and fiscal policy extensively. He would be receptive to a well-sourced population-infrastructure framing because it's exactly the kind of policy-meets-numbers story he likes. Approach: send him OIA findings with clear data visualisations. Don't pitch him a story. Give him a fact he can't ignore and let him write the story himself.

**Claire Trevett** -- Senior Political Reporter, NZ Herald. @CTrevettNZH. Trevett is the dean of the Press Gallery in terms of institutional knowledge. She understands parliamentary process better than most MPs. She's not naturally drawn to immigration as a beat, but she covers party politics and coalition dynamics. Useful for the "NZ First coalition leverage" angle. Approach: frame your material around party positioning and coalition negotiations, not raw immigration data.

**Andrea Vance** -- Senior Political Reporter, Stuff. @andraborovik. Vance is an investigative instinct wrapped in a political reporter. She loves OIA-sourced stories and government accountability angles. She's done strong work on housing and has shown interest in the gap between government promises and delivery. Receptive to a "government has no plan" framing. Approach: exclusive OIA findings. She responds to the investigative angle more than the policy angle.

**Henry Cooke** -- Political Reporter, Stuff. @henrycooke. Cooke is analytical and policy-focused. He would engage seriously with a population policy argument if it was well-sourced. He's covered immigration policy before and understands the numbers. Approach: give him the comparative international data. He appreciates the "here's how Australia/Canada handle this" frame.

**Jason Walls** -- Political Editor, Newstalk ZB. @Jaborwallky. Walls covers politics through a centre-right lens and is comfortable with stories that challenge the government on delivery. He's accessible and responsive on X. Approach: quick, punchy stats that work for talk radio. Think "sound bite with a source."

### Tier 2: The Amplifiers

These people don't set the agenda alone but they amplify stories that are already gaining traction. Getting them engaged multiplies your reach significantly.

**Bernard Hickey** -- The Kaka (Substack/newsletter). @bernardhickey. Hickey is the single most important person outside the gallery for this campaign. He has been making the housing-population-infrastructure argument for years. He has a large, engaged, paying subscriber base of economically literate readers. He is already sympathetic to the population policy framing. He's not someone you need to convince. He's someone you need to feed data to so he amplifies it. Approach: share your OIA findings and data dashboard with him directly. He will use it if it's good. His endorsement of your data gives you instant credibility with the policy class.

**Mike Hosking** -- Newstalk ZB, NZ Herald. @mikaborhosking. Hosking is the most-listened-to broadcaster in the country. He's centre-right, contrarian, and willing to push on immigration if it's framed as government incompetence rather than anti-migrant sentiment. He reaches the suburban National voters who are the key swing demographic. Approach: you don't pitch Hosking directly. You get your material into the Herald ecosystem and his producers find it. Alternatively, if your content goes viral on social media, he'll pick it up. He follows what's trending.

**Heather du Plessis-Allan** -- Newstalk ZB Drive host. @HesatherDPA. Similar audience to Hosking but slightly more willing to be provocative. She has pushed on immigration numbers before. Approach: same as Hosking. Get into the ecosystem. Her producers scan X and the Herald.

**Jack Tame** -- TVNZ Q+A host. @JackTame. Tame is the most important interviewer on NZ television. His Sunday morning show is where political leaders get grilled. If immigration becomes an election issue, it'll be because Tame asks the question on Q+A. Approach: you don't approach Tame directly. You create the conditions where immigration is so obviously a live issue that his producers put it on the run sheet. That means the story needs to be in the Herald and on Stuff first.

**Guyon Espiner** -- RNZ senior journalist. @GuyonEspiner. Espiner does long-form investigative and analytical work. He'd be interested in the systemic failure angle: government knew the numbers, had no plan, documents prove it. Approach: the deep OIA material. He wants the story behind the story.

### Tier 3: The Specialists and Commentators

**Jenee Tibshraeny** -- Interest.co.nz editor. @JeneeTibshraeny. Covers the housing-economy intersection. Already writes about population and housing dynamics. Would engage with good data immediately. Approach: send her your dashboard and data analysis directly.

**Michael Reddell** -- Croaking Cassandra blog. @MReddellNZ. Former Reserve Bank economist. Has been making the immigration-productivity argument for a decade. He is your intellectual ally and your credibility anchor. Approach: engage with his work publicly. Cite him. He will reciprocate. Getting Reddell to engage with your data on his blog is worth more than 10,000 social media followers.

**Bryce Edwards** -- Political commentator, Democracy Project. @BryceEdwards. Edwards aggregates and analyses political commentary. If he includes your work in his daily political roundup, it reaches the entire political class. Approach: produce consistently good analysis and he'll find it. He reads everything.

**Sean Plunket** -- The Platform. @SeanPlunket. Right-leaning broadcaster willing to cover immigration more aggressively. Smaller audience than ZB but a useful amplification channel. Approach: he's accessible. Pitch directly. But understand that association with Plunket positions you on the right, which limits cross-partisan appeal.

**Liam Hehir** -- Manawatu Standard columnist, conservative commentator. @liamhehir. Smart centre-right voice with a provincial NZ perspective. Would engage with population pressure arguments, especially as they affect regional NZ.

**Shamubeel Eaqub** -- Economist and commentator. @Shamubeel. Has written extensively on housing and demographics. Would engage with the data if it's rigorous. His endorsement carries weight with the centre-left.

### How to Work This Map

The strategy is sequential, not simultaneous. You do not blast all 20 people at once. That looks like a PR campaign and gets ignored.

Phase 1 (weeks 1-4): Engage with Hickey, Reddell, Tibshraeny, and Edwards. These are the people who already care about the topic and will engage with good data. Build credibility with them.

Phase 2 (weeks 4-8): When you have OIA responses coming back, approach Vance (for investigative angles) and Coughlan (for policy-political angles) with exclusive material. One at a time. Give them something nobody else has.

Phase 3 (months 3-6): By now, if your data and framing are being cited by the Tier 2 and Tier 3 people, the Tier 1 gallery journalists will come to you. That's the goal. You want to be pulled into the coverage, not pushing your way in.

## 2. THE PARLIAMENTARY PATHWAY

NZ's parliamentary system offers specific tools for a solo operator. Most activists waste these because they don't understand how Parliament actually works day to day. Here's the operational detail.

### Select Committees

Select committees are where the real work of Parliament happens and where a solo operator has the most direct access to MPs.

**The committees that matter for this campaign:**

The **Finance and Expenditure Committee** (FEC) examines the Budget and all financial legislation. Every year, the Budget Policy Statement and the Budget itself get referred to FEC. You can submit on both. A submission linking immigration numbers to fiscal costs (infrastructure, health, education spending) is within scope and gets read by the MPs on the committee. Current chair changes with each Parliament, but FEC always includes senior MPs from both government and opposition who take the fiscal argument seriously.

The **Education and Workforce Committee** handles immigration legislation. When any immigration bill comes before the House, this is where it goes. Submitting on immigration bills is the most direct pathway to being on the parliamentary record on immigration specifically. Request to appear in person. Oral submissions carry more weight than written ones because you're in the room with MPs and they ask you questions. That interaction is on the record and can be quoted.

The **Social Services and Community Committee** and the **Environment Committee** are secondary targets for submissions on housing and infrastructure bills that have population dimensions.

**How submissions actually work in practice:** Select committees call for submissions when a bill is referred to them. The deadline is usually 4-6 weeks after referral. You can also submit on petitions and on the Estimates (the annual budget review). The clerk's office is helpful and will tell you what's coming up. You can sign up for email alerts on the Parliament website. The key is to be ready: have your data and your argument prepared so you can submit quickly when a relevant bill appears.

**The trick most people don't know:** you can request to appear before a select committee even on petitions and Estimates reviews, not just bills. If there's a petition before a committee that touches on immigration or population, you can submit on it and request oral submission. This gives you face time with MPs in a formal setting.

### Written Parliamentary Questions

Written questions are one of the most underused tools in NZ politics. Any MP can lodge written questions to Ministers, and the Minister must respond within six working days. The questions and answers are published in Hansard and online.

**How this works for the campaign:** You draft the questions. You find an MP willing to lodge them. The MP doesn't need to care deeply about the issue -- they just need to be willing to put their name on a question. Opposition MPs lodge hundreds of questions every sitting week as a matter of course.

**Who would ask them:**

NZ First MPs are the most obvious candidates. Shane Jones has been vocal on immigration. Any NZ First MP would lodge immigration-related questions because it serves their party positioning. Draft the questions for them. Make them specific and data-focused: "What is the Minister's estimate of additional hospital capacity required to serve the net migration intake of the last three years?" "How many additional school places has the Ministry of Education planned for as a result of population growth from net migration since 2021?"

ACT MPs might lodge questions framed around fiscal cost and government efficiency. Labour MPs might lodge questions framed around worker exploitation or migrant welfare. The framing changes depending on the party, but the underlying data demand is the same.

**The operational process:** Email the question to the MP's office (electorate offices are often more responsive than parliamentary offices). Include a one-paragraph explanation of why the question matters and what you expect the answer to reveal. Make it easy for their staff. Draft the question in the exact format Parliament requires. If you've built any relationship with the MP or their staff, a quick phone call before emailing makes it more likely to get lodged.

### The OIA Process in Practice

The Official Information Act is theoretically straightforward. In practice, it's a game with specific rules that most people don't understand.

**How to use OIAs effectively:**

Be specific. "All documents relating to immigration" will be refused as too broad. "Briefings provided to the Minister of Immigration between January and June 2026 regarding net migration targets" will get a response.

Request specific document types: briefings to Ministers (BIMs), Cabinet papers, aide-memoires, internal analysis. These are the documents where the real thinking happens.

Target the right agencies. MBIE holds most immigration operational data. Treasury holds fiscal impact analysis. The Ministry of Housing and Urban Development holds the housing-population nexus data. Stats NZ holds demographic projections. The Department of Prime Minister and Cabinet holds cross-government strategy documents (if any exist, which is itself a revealing question).

**The timeline game:** Agencies have 20 working days to respond. They routinely extend. When they extend, you can complain to the Ombudsman. The Ombudsman's investigation itself generates pressure on the agency. A pattern of extensions and refusals on immigration-related OIAs is itself a story: "Government agencies are stonewalling requests for basic immigration data."

**The OIA-to-media pipeline:** When you get a response, the document is yours to publish. Screenshot the key pages. Write a one-paragraph summary of what it reveals. Send it to the relevant journalist with the actual documents attached. Journalists will almost always cover OIA-sourced material because it's exclusive, it's official, and it's defensible. They didn't make it up. The government wrote it.

File in batches of 10-15 across multiple agencies. Stagger them so responses come back over weeks, giving you a steady pipeline of content.

### Petitions

Any NZ citizen or resident can present a petition to Parliament. A petition with even a modest number of signatures (a few hundred) gets referred to a select committee, which must consider it and report back to the House. The petition itself is a media hook: "Petition calling for a national population plan presented to Parliament." The select committee consideration is a second media hook. The committee's report is a third.

Draft a petition with a simple, clear ask: "That the House recommend the Government develop and publish a comprehensive national population strategy, including an annual net migration target linked to infrastructure and housing capacity." Get a sympathetic MP to present it. The Green Party and NZ First both have MPs who might present such a petition depending on framing.

## 3. THE PARTY-BY-PARTY ANALYSIS

### National

**Their vulnerability:** National's base includes property owners who benefit from population-driven demand, and businesses that want cheap migrant labour. But their voter base also includes the suburban families who can't get a GP appointment, whose kids can't afford a house, and who sit in Auckland traffic for two hours a day. National's core promise is competent economic management, but they have no coherent population strategy. They approved high migration numbers during their current term and the infrastructure hasn't kept pace. The gap between their "good managers" brand and the visible infrastructure strain is the pressure point.

**Internal tension:** The business wing wants high migration for labour supply. The suburban voter wing wants houses they can afford and services that work. These are directly in tension at current migration levels. National manages this tension by simply not talking about it, which is exactly what the campaign should force them to do.

**Sympathetic MPs:** Look to MPs in high-growth Auckland electorates where constituents are feeling infrastructure pressure. Backbenchers are more likely to engage than frontbenchers. MPs who came from local government backgrounds understand the infrastructure-population nexus because they dealt with it as councillors. Simeon Brown has urban development in his portfolio and has to answer for the infrastructure gap. Chris Bishop as Housing Minister is directly accountable for the housing-population mismatch.

**Pressure point:** Force them to state a number. "What net migration level does National consider sustainable?" They don't want to answer because any number either alienates business (too low) or voters (too high). Making the refusal to state a number visible is the play.

### Labour

**Their vulnerability:** Labour presided over the record migration surge of 2022-2023 through policy settings they put in place. Their working-class and lower-income base is the most directly affected by wage competition and housing pressure from high migration. But Labour's activist base is culturally progressive and views immigration scepticism with suspicion. Labour's internal polling will show their working-class voters care about this. Their activist class doesn't want to hear it.

**Internal tension:** This is the deepest internal tension of any NZ party on immigration. Labour's union-aligned wing (historically sceptical of migration that undercuts wages) versus their progressive-cosmopolitan wing (which views immigration restriction as xenophobic). The CTU has made careful noises about migrant worker exploitation which is a proxy for concerns about labour market impacts. Labour simply cannot talk about this issue without one wing getting angry.

**Sympathetic MPs:** Look for MPs in South Auckland and provincial electorates where housing pressure and wage stagnation are real. Kieran McAnulty types -- provincial, working-class-oriented Labour MPs who hear from constituents about cost of living and housing. They know immigration is part of the story even if they can't say it openly.

**Pressure point:** The housing angle. "Labour's housing policy assumes what level of net migration?" If they don't have an answer, their housing policy is a fiction. If they do have an answer, they've implicitly conceded that migration levels matter for housing, which opens the whole conversation.

### NZ First

**Their vulnerability:** NZ First talks tough on immigration but has been in government twice and delivered very little actual restriction. Winston Peters has been promising to cut immigration for 30 years. He's currently in government. The numbers are what they are. The credibility gap between NZ First's rhetoric and their delivery in government is significant.

**Internal tension:** NZ First is Winston Peters. The party's internal dynamics are whatever Winston decides they are. The tension, such as it is, is between NZ First as a protest vehicle (which needs to keep the immigration issue alive) and NZ First as a governing party (which needs to show it can actually deliver). Shane Jones represents the delivery-oriented wing. Peters himself is more comfortable with grievance than governance on this issue.

**Sympathetic MPs:** Shane Jones is the most engaged on immigration specifically. He's said publicly that immigration will be central to NZ First's 2026 pitch. He would use your data in Parliament if it's good. The NZ First parliamentary office is small and accessible. An email to Jones's office with well-packaged data has a reasonable chance of getting a response.

**Pressure point:** The accountability angle. "NZ First has been in government for X months. Net migration is Y. What have they actually delivered?" This isn't attacking NZ First. It's holding them to their own promises. This is useful because it forces NZ First to either deliver (which advances the cause) or look ineffective (which keeps the issue live as unresolved).

### ACT

**Their vulnerability:** ACT is ideologically pro-market, which includes relatively open labour markets. David Seymour has historically been moderate on immigration, favouring a skills-based system rather than numerical restriction. But ACT's voter base overlaps significantly with people who are frustrated about housing costs and the infrastructure deficit. ACT's libertarian economics sits awkwardly with the reality that mass migration requires massive government infrastructure spending.

**Internal tension:** ACT's free-market ideology says labour should move freely. ACT's fiscal conservatism says government shouldn't spend billions on infrastructure for people it actively chose to bring in. These two commitments are in tension and ACT hasn't resolved it.

**Sympathetic MPs:** ACT MPs who focus on fiscal responsibility and government efficiency would engage with a "cost per migrant to the taxpayer" framing. The question "what is the fiscal cost to the Crown of each net migrant in terms of required infrastructure, health, and education spending?" is an ACT-friendly question.

**Pressure point:** The fiscal angle. "What is the net fiscal impact of current migration settings? Has Treasury modelled this? If not, why not?" ACT can't resist a government-waste story.

### Greens

**Their vulnerability:** The Greens have a deep historical connection to population and environmental sustainability that they've largely abandoned in favour of social justice positioning. The original Green movement globally was explicitly concerned about population growth and resource limits. NZ's Greens under recent leadership have moved away from this, but the environmental logic hasn't changed. More people means more emissions, more habitat pressure, more resource consumption. The Greens can't answer the question "what is the carbon impact of adding 100,000 people per year?" without either admitting migration has environmental costs or contradicting their own climate commitments.

**Internal tension:** The environmental wing versus the social justice wing. The social justice wing dominates current party positioning and views immigration restriction as xenophobic. The environmental wing quietly knows the numbers don't add up but can't say so within the party. Some of the Greens' older members and supporters come from a population-sustainability tradition.

**Sympathetic MPs:** This is the hardest party to find allies in on this issue, given current positioning. Look for Green MPs or candidates with backgrounds in environmental science or planning rather than social activism. The framing has to be purely environmental: carrying capacity, infrastructure carbon costs, per-capita emissions.

**Pressure point:** The environmental contradiction. "The Greens' climate plan assumes what level of population growth? If net migration adds X people, what is the additional emissions impact? How does this square with your 2030 targets?" This is a question the Greens absolutely do not want to answer because any honest answer creates internal conflict.

### Te Pati Maori

**Their vulnerability:** Te Pati Maori's current positioning is broadly pro-migrant, framing migrants as fellow victims of colonialism and standing in solidarity. But their base -- Maori communities -- disproportionately faces housing pressure, healthcare access issues, and competition for limited social resources. The gap between TPM's elite solidarity positioning and the lived experience of their constituents on housing and services is a tension they haven't resolved.

**Internal tension:** The ideological commitment to anti-colonial solidarity with migrants versus the material reality that their communities bear significant costs of rapid population growth. Maori are over-represented in housing stress statistics. Maori communities in South Auckland, Rotorua, and the Eastern Bay of Plenty are the ones most directly affected by the infrastructure deficit.

**Sympathetic MPs:** This requires the most careful approach. Do not frame this as anti-immigration to TPM. Frame it as rangatiratanga: "Shouldn't tangata whenua have a say in the rate of population growth on their whenua?" Frame it as Treaty-based: "Has the Crown consulted with Maori on immigration settings as required under the Treaty?" These are questions TPM can engage with without contradicting their broader politics.

**Pressure point:** The Treaty consultation angle. "When was the last time the Crown consulted iwi on immigration settings? Article Two of the Treaty guarantees tino rangatiratanga. Does that extend to having a say in how many people are added to the population annually?" This puts the government on the back foot without requiring TPM to be anti-migrant.

## 4. THE NZ CALENDAR

The 2026 election will likely be held in October or November 2026. Working backwards from that, here are the windows that matter.

### March-April 2026 (Now)

Parliament is sitting. This is the time to file OIA requests so responses arrive during the budget period. Submit on any bills currently before select committees that have population or infrastructure dimensions. Begin engaging the journalist network.

The NZ First annual convention is typically held around this period. Watch for immigration policy signals. Any NZ First announcement on immigration is an opportunity to respond with data and push for more specificity.

### May 2026: The Budget

Budget day (usually mid-to-late May) is the single most important date in the political calendar before the election. The Budget determines the fiscal frame for the next 18 months.

**What to do:** Have a pre-prepared "population audit" of the Budget ready to publish within hours of the Budget's release. "The Budget allocates $X for health infrastructure. Based on population growth of Y, this funds Z additional hospital beds. That's a shortfall of W." Do this for health, education, housing, and transport. Make it the population lens on the Budget.

Pre-brief your analysis framework to journalists: "On Budget day, I'll be publishing a population audit showing whether spending keeps pace with population growth. Happy to provide analysis on deadline." Journalists working on Budget day are under intense time pressure. If your analysis is ready and credible, they'll use it.

### June-July 2026: Post-Budget and Party Conferences

The post-Budget period is when parties start positioning for the election. Party conferences typically happen between June and August. National's conference and Labour's conference are the big ones.

**What to do:** Publish the party scorecard before conference season. Parties draft their conference policy platforms in the weeks before. If your scorecard is generating media attention, party organisers will feel pressure to include population/immigration in their platform discussions. Even if they don't, journalists covering the conferences will ask "why isn't immigration on your agenda?"

### August-September 2026: Pre-Campaign Season

This is when the election campaign effectively begins even if it hasn't been officially called. Parties release their major policies. Candidate selections are finalised. The media shifts into election mode.

**What to do:** Launch the coalition pressure mapping analysis. "Here are the four most likely coalition scenarios. Here's what each means for immigration policy." This is catnip for political journalists because MMP coalition speculation is the story of every NZ election. If immigration is embedded in the coalition analysis, it becomes embedded in the campaign coverage.

Attend candidate meetings in key electorates. Have your immigration questions ready. Film the responses. Post them.

### September-October 2026: The Campaign

The official campaign period is roughly the last five weeks before election day. The writ period (when Parliament is dissolved) is about three weeks.

**What to do:** By this point, either immigration is on the agenda or it isn't. If it is, your job is to keep feeding data to journalists and holding all parties accountable. If it isn't, your remaining play is confrontation content at public events and viral social media moments.

The leaders' debates are the peak moments. If you can get immigration questions into the leaders' debates, you've won. You can't directly control this, but you can influence it by ensuring immigration is a live issue in the media coverage that debate moderators use to select their questions.

### Waitangi Day 2026 (February 6) -- Already Passed

For future reference, Waitangi Day is an annual opportunity to raise the Treaty-immigration nexus. The day is covered extensively by media and political leaders are on the marae making promises. The question "has the Crown consulted Maori on immigration settings?" is perfectly timed for Waitangi Day coverage.

### Other Key Dates

Statistics NZ releases quarterly migration data. Every data release is a content opportunity. Know the release calendar and have your analysis framework pre-built so you can respond within hours.

Parliamentary sitting weeks create more media coverage than recess weeks. Focus your major content pushes for sitting weeks when gallery journalists are active and looking for stories.

Local government elections (if any by-elections occur) are opportunities to localise the population-infrastructure argument.

## 5. THE CULTURAL LANDMINES

These are the things that will destroy this campaign in NZ. Not "might damage." Will destroy.

### The Absolute No-Go List

**Never use the word "remigration."** In any context, for any reason. Not even to discuss it. Not even to say you're against it. The word is toxic in NZ in a way that goes beyond the UK context. The Treaty makes it a joke: if anyone's getting remigrated, it's Pakeha. The Christchurch mosque attack is recent enough that any language associated with European identitarian movements triggers an immediate and overwhelming backlash. The word alone will define you forever. You will never recover.

**Never target specific ethnic groups.** Not Indian students. Not Chinese investors. Not Pacific overstayers. Not anyone. The moment you name a group, you're a racist in NZ public discourse. It doesn't matter if the numbers support the point. The cultural norm is absolute on this. Frame everything as "numbers" and "planning," never as "who."

**Never use Great Replacement language or framing.** "Replacement," "demographic change," "becoming a minority in our own country" -- all of this is radioactive. It was already toxic before Christchurch. After Christchurch, it's the language of the terrorist's manifesto. Any association, even indirect, with that framing will end you.

**Never criticise immigrants as people.** Never question their work ethic, their culture, their values, or their right to be here if they're here legally. The only acceptable target is government policy and government failure to plan. The line is: "The government is failing both existing residents and new migrants by not planning for population growth." Migrants are victims of the same policy failure, not the cause of it.

**Never position yourself against the Treaty of Waitangi.** Immigration scepticism combined with Treaty scepticism is the specific combination that gets you labelled as a white nationalist in NZ. You can be concerned about immigration. You can support the Treaty. Doing both simultaneously is not only safe, it's strategically powerful. But if you're seen as anti-Treaty AND anti-immigration, you're finished.

**Never appear to be importing overseas culture war content.** New Zealanders have an almost allergic reaction to American and British political imports. If you look like you're copying Reform UK, people will dismiss you as a foreign-influenced provocateur. The campaign must feel unmistakably Kiwi. Use NZ data, NZ examples, NZ humour, and NZ framing.

### The Subtler Landmines

**Don't be too slick.** Professional-looking campaign materials, polished videos, and branded content trigger the "who's funding this?" question in NZ. People assume astroturfing. A slightly rough, authentic, obviously-one-person operation is more credible than something that looks like a campaign.

**Don't be too angry.** The NZ register for political advocacy is concerned, not furious. Exasperated, not enraged. The "angry white man shouting about immigration" is a character that NZ media will instantly typecast and dismiss. Controlled frustration works. Performative rage does not.

**Don't claim to speak for New Zealanders.** "New Zealanders are sick of..." or "Kiwis want..." invites the response "who appointed you?" Let the data speak. Let people draw their own conclusions. The reluctant-citizen framing ("I just looked at the numbers and they're concerning") is safe. The populist-leader framing ("the people demand...") triggers tall poppy syndrome.

**Don't attack individual migrants' stories.** Media will run human interest stories about migrants. If you respond negatively to a story about a family's immigration journey, you look heartless. Ignore the individual stories. Stay on the systemic argument. "That family's story is wonderful. The question is whether the government has built the infrastructure to support all the families arriving."

**Don't be seen at protests or rallies associated with the far right.** NZ has a small far-right fringe. Some of them showed up at the Parliament occupation in 2022. Any association, even accidental, with those networks contaminates you permanently. Maintain distance. Do not share their content. Do not attend their events. Do not engage with their accounts, even to agree on narrow points.

**Don't use the phrase "population ponzi" unless you're prepared to defend it.** It's a powerful phrase and some economists (Bernard Hickey among them) have used it. But it's also a phrase that opponents will seize on to portray you as extreme. If Hickey says it, he has the economic credentials to back it up. If you say it without that credibility, it sounds like a conspiracy slogan. Let others say it. You say "population growth without infrastructure investment."

## 6. THE REALISTIC ASSESSMENT

### Probability of Success

A solo operator forcing immigration onto the 2026 election agenda: I'd put it at 20-30% probability of meaningful impact, defined as immigration being a regular topic in election coverage and at least two major parties being forced to state a position they wouldn't otherwise have stated.

Here's why it's not higher:

The timeline is very tight. It is March 2026. The election is in roughly seven months. Building a credible media presence from zero in seven months is possible but demands near-full-time commitment and a lot of things going right.

NZ First already occupies the space. Winston Peters will make immigration an issue regardless of what this campaign does. The risk is that all immigration coverage gets filtered through the NZ First lens, and the solo operator's contribution is invisible or redundant.

The economy may not cooperate. If the economy is performing reasonably and unemployment is low heading into the election, the immigration-infrastructure argument is harder to make politically salient. People tolerate a lot of strain when they feel economically secure.

The media may not cooperate. If a major news event (earthquake, international crisis, political scandal) dominates the cycle, there's simply no room for immigration to break through. You can't compete with a natural disaster for media attention.

Here's why it's not lower:

The underlying public concern is real and polling confirms it. The argument is latent in the electorate. It needs a catalyst, not a conversion effort.

NZ's media market is small enough that one determined person can actually reach the key players. You don't need to influence 500 journalists. You need to influence 15.

OIA-sourced material is irresistible to NZ political journalists. If your OIAs yield anything newsworthy, it will get covered.

The housing crisis provides a permanent on-ramp for the conversation. Every housing story is an immigration story if you make the connection.

### Most Likely Failure Mode

The most likely failure mode is not destruction. It's irrelevance. You post consistently, you file OIAs, you build a small following, but you never break through to the mainstream media cycle. You end up as another voice on political X that a few hundred people follow and nobody else notices. The OIA responses are interesting but not explosive enough to generate standalone stories. Journalists are polite but don't use your material. Parties don't respond to your scorecard because they don't have to.

This is the default outcome for 80-90% of political advocacy campaigns and the honest truth is that most fail through this mode rather than through controversy. You don't blow up. You just never take off.

The second most likely failure mode is getting labelled before you can establish credibility. If your first major piece of content is interpreted as anti-migrant rather than pro-planning, the label sticks and recovery is almost impossible in NZ's small media ecosystem. First impressions matter disproportionately because there are so few people making the judgement.

### The One Thing That Would Most Increase the Odds

### The Honest Bottom Line

This campaign is running against the clock, against an entrenched media ecosystem that defaults to ignoring new voices, and against NZ's cultural tendency to punish people who stick their heads up. The strategic advice from the other experts is largely sound, but the operational reality is that NZ is a small, relationship-driven political culture where institutional credibility matters enormously and outsiders face a steep climb.

The path that works is narrow: produce genuinely excellent data analysis, feed it to the right journalists, file OIAs that yield newsworthy material, and build credibility through relentless accuracy and usefulness rather than through provocation or self-promotion. It's not glamorous. It's grinding. But it's the only path that has a realistic chance of working in NZ's specific political culture within the time available.

If I were a Press Gallery journalist watching this campaign launch, I'd be asking three questions. First, is the data actually good? Because NZ is small enough that errors get caught fast and your credibility is gone with one bad number. Second, does this person understand how NZ works, or are they importing a foreign playbook? Because Kiwi journalists can smell British or American political tactics from a mile away and it immediately triggers scepticism. Third, is this person going to be here in six months? Because the gallery has seen a hundred people show up with passion and disappear after three weeks. Persistence is the ultimate credibility test.

Pass those three tests and you have a chance. Fail any one of them and you're another name in the long list of people who tried to change NZ politics and discovered that this small country is harder to move than it looks.
