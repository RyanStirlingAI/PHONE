## 3. THE WEBSITE QUESTION

### Should there be a howmany.nz website?

Yes, but not on day 1. The website serves a different audience than social media. Social media reaches the public and journalists. The website serves as a credibility anchor, a reference library for your data, and eventually the place where the data dashboard lives.

### What platform?

**Substack as the initial "website."** Here is why.

For the first 4-6 weeks, howmany.nz should redirect to your Substack (howmanynz.substack.com). Substack gives you a publishing platform, email list, and enough customisation to look credible, for free. It has built-in SEO, built-in email delivery, and you don't have to build or maintain anything. Connect the howmany.nz custom domain to Substack (Substack supports this on free plans).

This is the minimum viable website. It has:
- An About page (your origin story and mission)
- A Subscribe button (email list from day 1)
- Posts that serve as the reference library (the Weekly Wrap, OIA deep-dives, the party scorecard)
- A professional enough appearance for a journalist to link to

### What goes on it at launch?

1. **About/origin story** -- the "who is this and why should I care" page
2. **The first Stat Drop post** -- your opening data analysis
3. **The party scorecard** -- even if it's mostly "no stated position" at launch, having it live from day 1 establishes the accountability framework
4. **An OIA tracker page** -- a running log of what you've requested, from whom, and what came back. This is both a transparency mechanism and a content generator.

### What gets added later?

**Month 2-3: The data dashboard.** This is where the website earns its keep. A simple, visual dashboard showing:
- Net migration over time (line chart)
- Housing consents vs population growth (bar chart comparison)
- Hospital capacity vs population by region
- The "infrastructure gap" metric -- population growth rate vs infrastructure spending growth rate

For the dashboard, use one of these approaches:
- **Google Sheets embedded charts** (free, easy, updates automatically when you update the spreadsheet). This is the fastest path.
- **Datawrapper** (free tier allows several charts, designed for journalism, embeddable). Better looking than Google Sheets.
- **Observable HQ** (free tier, JavaScript-based, good for interactive charts). More powerful but steeper learning curve.

Embed the dashboard charts into a Substack post or, if you've moved to a standalone site, directly on the page.

**Month 4-5: Consider migrating to a standalone site.** If the campaign has traction, move from Substack to a simple static site. Options:
- **Carrd.co** (free tier for a single page, $19/year for multi-page). Good for a simple landing page + dashboard + links to social accounts. Fast to set up. Not great for blog-style content.
- **GitHub Pages + a static site generator** (free hosting, requires basic technical knowledge). Best for long-term maintainability. Use a template like Hugo or 11ty. The content lives in Markdown files. Good if you have any coding ability.
- **Keep Substack** and just add the dashboard via embedded iframes. This is the lowest-effort option and honestly might be sufficient for the entire campaign.

Recommendation: stay on Substack for the full campaign unless you hit a specific limitation. The email list is more valuable than a fancy website, and Substack's built-in email delivery is hard to replicate for free elsewhere.

### Domain setup

Register **howmany.nz** through a NZ registrar (e.g., 1stDomains, Freeparking, or Metaname). A .nz domain costs roughly $25-40 NZD per year. This is possibly the only cash expense of the campaign.

Point the domain to Substack initially. If you later build a standalone site, point it there instead.

### Integration with the data dashboard

The dashboard should be the centrepiece of the website by month 3. Every social media post that cites a number should link to the dashboard as the source. "Full data on our dashboard: howmany.nz/dashboard" becomes the standard footer. This drives traffic from social to the website, where the subscribe button captures email addresses. The email list is the most valuable asset because it can't be taken away by a platform algorithm change.

## 5. THE FIRST 30 DAYS -- GROWTH TACTICS

### Week 1 (Days 1-7): Establish Rhythm

Covered above. The goal is simply to exist, post consistently, and begin the reply engagement strategy. You're laying down a pattern for the algorithm and for human observers.

### Week 2 (Days 8-14): Become Useful in Threads

The single most effective growth tactic for a zero-follower NZ political account is being consistently useful in other people's threads. Here is how this works in practice.

**The reply strategy:** Every day, identify the 2-3 most discussed political/housing/health stories on NZ X. Find the journalist's original post about it or the most active thread. Reply with a data point that adds context to the discussion. Always include a source citation. Never self-promote ("check out my account"). Just add value. Let people click through to your profile because your reply was informative.

Example: A journalist posts about hospital overcrowding. Your reply: "Worth noting -- Wellington's population grew 6% since that ED was last expanded. Net migration alone added roughly 8,000 people to the Wellington region in the last two years. Source: Stats NZ quarterly migration data." That reply is genuinely useful. People will click your profile. Some will follow.

**Volume target for Week 2:** 5+ substantive replies per day on X. This takes about 30-45 minutes of active engagement.

**Content posting:** Continue the 4-5 posts per week rhythm from week 1. Two Stat Drops, two Reframes, one lighter piece. Continue TikTok (2 per week) and Facebook (3-4 per week).

**Target followers by end of week 2:** 100-400

### Week 3 (Days 15-21): Start Getting Noticed

**Content upgrade:** Post your first thread that goes deep on one topic. A 5-7 post thread on X that does a genuine analysis of, say, housing consents vs population growth in Auckland over the last five years, with data visualisations. This is the content that separates you from opinion accounts. It takes 2-3 hours to produce but it's the kind of thread that gets bookmarked and shared by journalists.

**Target Bernard Hickey.** Not directly. Find one of his posts about housing or population and reply with a genuinely useful data point he hasn't mentioned. If your reply is good enough, he'll engage. One like or reply from Hickey exposes you to his entire follower base. Do this 2-3 times across week 3. Don't be pushy. Just be useful.

**Target Michael Reddell.** Same approach. Engage with his Croaking Cassandra posts. Cite his work in your content. He'll notice. He engages with people who engage with his ideas.

**Facebook groups:** Post your first local impact content (e.g., the Rolleston or Tauranga example from the content strategy) directly into a relevant local community Facebook group. Don't post as the How Many NZ page -- post the How Many NZ page content into the group. Keep it factual, local, and non-political in tone. "Rolleston's population has tripled in ten years. Here's what happened to school capacity and GP access." If the group rules don't allow it, post to the How Many NZ page and hope members share it.

**Target followers by end of week 3:** 200-600

### Week 4 (Days 22-30): The First Content Experiment

**Content experiment 1: The question post.** Post a direct question to a specific politician on X. Not an Accountability Question (that's month 2-3 content) but a genuine, polite question. "Genuine question for @ChrisPMNZ: How many people does your government think is the right number to add to New Zealand next year? Not a trick question. Just a number." See how it performs. If it gets traction, you know the accountability format will work when scaled up later.

**Content experiment 2: TikTok format test.** Try two different TikTok formats and see which performs better: (a) text-on-screen data visuals with voiceover, and (b) face-to-camera talking head. In NZ, face-to-camera tends to build trust faster, but text-on-screen is easier to produce. See what your audience responds to.

**Content experiment 3: The comparison carousel.** On Facebook, try a multi-image post showing Australia vs Canada vs UK vs NZ population planning side by side. Carousels (swipeable multi-image posts) tend to get higher engagement than single images on Facebook.

**First OIA responses may start arriving** (if you sent on Day 2, the 20-working-day deadline falls around Day 30). If a response arrives, this is your first exclusive content. Treat it seriously. Screenshot the key findings, write a clear analysis, and post it as an OIA Reveal. If it's genuinely newsworthy, DM it to Andrea Vance or Thomas Coughlan before posting publicly -- giving a journalist a 2-hour head start on exclusive material builds the relationship.

**Start the data dashboard.** Even a simple Google Sheets dashboard with 3-4 embedded charts is enough. Make it public. Link to it from your Substack. This is your credibility infrastructure.

**Target followers by end of month 1:** 300-800 on X, 100-300 on TikTok, 200-500 on Facebook, 50-150 Substack subscribers

### Specific Tactics for Going from 0 to 500-1000 Followers on X

**Tactic 1: The reply-to-visibility pipeline.** Your replies on journalists' posts are your single best growth channel. A reply that gets 5+ likes on a journalist's post with 500+ views exposes your profile to hundreds of politically engaged NZ users. Do this 5 times a day for 30 days and you will get to 500 followers. This is not glamorous. It is the tactic that works.

**Tactic 2: Thread Tuesdays.** Post one substantial data thread per week (Tuesday or Wednesday, when NZ X engagement peaks). A thread with 5-7 posts, charts, and sourced analysis. Threads get more algorithmic reach than single posts. They also get bookmarked and referenced, which extends their lifespan.

**Tactic 3: Newsjacking with speed.** When Stats NZ or MBIE drops data, be the first to post an immigration-linked analysis. If you can post a sourced take within 2 hours of a data release, journalists scanning X for reactions will find yours. Speed matters more than polish in these moments.

**Tactic 4: Quote-tweet with value.** When a journalist posts a housing/health/infrastructure story, quote-tweet it with the population growth context rather than just liking it. The quote tweet appears in your followers' feeds AND the original poster's notifications. It's more visible than a reply.

**Tactic 5: Don't chase virality.** In NZ's small political X ecosystem, you don't need a viral post to reach 500 followers. You need 30 days of consistent, useful, data-backed presence. The political X community is small enough that regular participants notice new voices within 2-3 weeks if the content is good. Play the consistency game, not the virality game.

### When to Shift from Replies to Original Content

The shift is gradual, not sudden. Here's the timeline:

**Days 1-14:** 70% replies, 30% original posts. Your replies are doing more for your growth than your posts.

**Days 15-21:** 50% replies, 50% original posts. You now have enough followers that your original posts get some organic reach.

**Days 22-30:** 30% replies, 70% original posts. By now, your original content should be getting enough engagement that it drives growth on its own. Replies become a maintenance activity rather than the primary growth engine.

**Month 2 onwards:** 20% replies, 80% original posts. The reply strategy never stops entirely -- it's how you stay visible in conversations and build journalist relationships -- but your original content is now the main event.

### How to Get the First Journalist to Notice You

The path to journalist attention in NZ is specific and narrow.

**Step 1: Be in their replies.** Journalists notice people who consistently add useful context to their threads. Not flattery. Not "great article." Specific data that makes their story better. Do this for 2-3 weeks.

**Step 2: Produce something they can use.** Your first data thread or OIA Reveal should be genuinely useful to a journalist -- information they didn't have, presented clearly, with sources they can verify. A journalist doesn't need to quote you. They just need to think "that's a useful number" and file it away.

**Step 3: The DM.** After 2-3 weeks of being useful in replies, when you get your first OIA response back, DM the most relevant journalist: "Hi [name], I've been doing some OIA work on population planning. Got a response from [agency] that might interest you. Happy to share the documents if useful. No strings attached." Keep it short, professional, and genuinely no-strings. You're offering value, not asking for coverage.

**Step 4: Be patient.** In NZ's press gallery, trust is built over weeks, not days. A journalist might take your material and not credit you. That's fine in month 1. Credibility compounds. By month 2-3, if your data is consistently good, they'll start citing you because it adds credibility to their story to have a named source.

The most likely first journalist to engage is **Bernard Hickey** (already sympathetic to the framing), **Jenee Tibshraeny** (covers the housing-economy beat), or **Michael Reddell** (not technically a journalist but his blog citations cascade to journalists). Get one of these three to engage with your data and the rest follows.

### Benchmarks: On Track vs Failing

**Week 1-2 benchmarks:**
- On track: 50+ X followers, at least 2 posts with 500+ impressions, at least 1 journalist liked or replied to your content
- Warning: under 20 X followers, no post above 200 impressions, zero journalist engagement
- If failing: your content is probably too generic or too aggressive. Review the tone. Make sure you're leading with data, not opinion. Increase reply volume on journalist threads.

**Week 3-4 benchmarks:**
- On track: 200+ X followers, at least 1 post with 2,000+ impressions, 2+ journalists have engaged, first OIA response received
- Warning: under 100 X followers, no post above 1,000 impressions, zero journalist engagement
- If failing: try a different content format. If Stat Drops aren't working, try a longer thread with deeper analysis. If your replies aren't getting traction, you might be replying to the wrong accounts. Focus on the 5-6 most active NZ political journalists.

**Month 2 benchmarks:**
- On track: 500+ X followers, weekly impressions averaging 10,000+, at least one OIA Reveal post that got 1,000+ impressions, 5+ journalist accounts engaging regularly, Bernard Hickey or Michael Reddell has engaged at least once
- Warning: under 300 X followers, no OIA content published, fewer than 3 journalist engagements total
- If failing at month 2, the diagnosis is likely one of: (a) content quality isn't high enough -- the data analysis needs to be sharper, (b) you're posting but not engaging in conversations, or (c) the timing is off (posting outside NZ business hours when journalists are active). Adjust before month 3.

**Month 3 benchmarks:**
- On track: 1,000+ X followers, a journalist has cited or referenced your data in their work, the party scorecard has generated at least some discussion, Substack has 200+ subscribers
- Warning: under 500 X followers, no journalist citations, no party or politician engagement
- If failing at month 3, consider a strategy pivot (see below).

### When to Pivot

**Pivot trigger 1: Zero journalist engagement by day 30.** If no journalist has liked, replied, or engaged with any of your content after 30 days of consistent posting and replying, the content isn't landing. Pivot: dramatically increase the quality and depth of your analysis. Write a single definitive thread -- the best piece of immigration data analysis published on NZ X -- and put everything into it. If that doesn't generate journalist engagement, the framing may need adjustment.

**Pivot trigger 2: Audience skews heavily right-wing by month 2.** If your follower base is predominantly NZ First and ACT supporters, you're becoming a right-wing echo chamber. Pivot: immediately produce content that appeals to centre-left audiences. Housing affordability affecting young families. Migrant worker exploitation. Environmental impact of unplanned growth. GP access in low-income areas. The campaign dies if it becomes partisan.

**Pivot trigger 3: Engagement crashes after initial growth.** If you hit 300 followers quickly then flatline, you've saturated the "already convinced" audience and aren't reaching the persuadable middle. Pivot: shift from X-first to TikTok-first or Facebook-first content. The persuadable middle is on Facebook and TikTok, not political X. Increase local impact content. Target specific regional Facebook groups.

**Pivot trigger 4: You're burning out.** The 6-7 posts per week schedule is aggressive. If you're struggling to maintain quality, cut to 4 posts per week: Monday Stat Drop, Wednesday Reframe, Thursday thread or comparison, Friday Weekly Wrap. Quality over quantity. One excellent post per week beats five mediocre ones.

## FINAL REVIEW -- REALITY CHECK

I've reviewed this plan through the lens of a solo operator with zero budget in NZ's specific political media ecosystem. Here are the adjustments I've made and the honest caveats.

**What's realistic:** The X setup and follow strategy will work exactly as described. The reply engagement strategy is proven -- it's how every successful political account in NZ has grown from zero. The OIA pipeline is genuinely powerful and unique. The Substack-as-website approach saves money and complexity. The content calendar is sustainable at 4-5 posts per week (not 7).

**What's optimistic:** The follower targets. NZ political X is small. Getting to 1,000 followers in 2 months would be a strong result. Getting there in 1 month would be exceptional. The targets in this document assume things go well. If they go averagely, halve the numbers and the strategy still works -- because influence in NZ is about reaching the right 200 people, not about raw follower count.

**What's been cut for realism:** I haven't included Instagram as a primary channel because it would stretch the solo operator too thin. Instagram can be added in month 2-3 by repurposing existing content. I also haven't included paid promotion because the brief specifies zero budget, and honestly, in NZ's small market, organic reach to the political class is achievable without paid ads.

**The biggest risk:** Not the setup. Not the content. The biggest risk is week 3. That's when the initial adrenaline wears off, you've posted 15 times, you have 80 followers, and it feels like you're talking to nobody. Every political account that failed in NZ failed in week 3. The operator needs to understand this in advance: week 3 will feel like failure. It isn't. Keep posting. The compound curve kicks in around week 5-6 when the algorithm has enough signal to start recommending your content and when journalists have seen your name in their notifications enough times to pay attention.

**One thing I'd add if there was any budget at all:** $50-100 NZD on a single promoted post on X -- the best-performing organic post from month 1, targeted at NZ users interested in politics, housing, or economics. One promoted post that reaches 50,000 people would accelerate the growth curve by 2-3 weeks. But it's not essential. The organic strategy works on its own. It just takes longer.
