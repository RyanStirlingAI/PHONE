# Wave 1 Content Strategy: The Concerned Kiwi Campaign

## How Many NZ -- Zero-Budget Content Playbook

---

## 1. CONTENT PILLARS

Seven recurring content types, each with a distinct role in the ecosystem. Together they create a rhythm that builds audience, establishes credibility, and escalates toward accountability.

---

### Pillar 1: The Stat Drop

**Format:** Single-image graphic or 15-30 second text-over-footage video. One number, one comparison, one question. Works on X, TikTok, Facebook, and Instagram Reels simultaneously.

**Hook structure:** A striking number presented as something the audience doesn't know but should. The hook is always a question or a juxtaposition that makes the number feel personal. Never leads with "immigration" in the first line -- leads with the lived impact.

**Emotional trigger:** Surprise turning to indignation. The feeling of "I didn't know that, and I should have been told." This is the most important emotional sequence in the entire campaign. It positions the audience as people who have been kept in the dark, not people who are angry at immigrants.

**Call to action:** "Share this if you think we deserve a plan." No petition links, no website URLs in the early months. The CTA is always sharing, because sharing is the growth engine.

**Frequency:** 3-4 per week. This is the workhorse content. It fills the feed, trains the algorithm, and gives people a reason to follow.

**Example 1:**

> Your hospital emergency department was designed for a city of 180,000.
>
> Wellington now has 220,000 people.
>
> Nobody expanded the ED.
>
> Net migration added 130,000 people to New Zealand last year. That's a city bigger than Napier. In a single year.
>
> Not one major party has published a plan for how our hospitals, schools, and roads are supposed to cope.
>
> This isn't an immigration debate. It's a planning failure.

**Example 2:**

> It takes 87 new houses to shelter every 100 new residents.
>
> In the year to February 2024, net migration added 130,000 people.
>
> That means we needed roughly 113,000 new homes just to stand still.
>
> Housing consents that year: 34,000.
>
> We built less than a third of what population growth demanded. And then politicians act mystified that rents keep rising.
>
> Where's the plan?

**Example 3:**

> New Zealand's population grew 2.8% in a single year. The OECD average is 0.7%.
>
> We grew four times faster than the developed world average.
>
> Our infrastructure budget grew by roughly... nothing.
>
> The government is building a bigger country without building the country to match. And nobody asked you about it.

### Pillar 2: The Reframe

**Format:** Text post on X (thread of 3-5 posts), repurposed as a TikTok or Reel with screen-capture visuals. Takes a mainstream news story -- housing, health, education, traffic -- and adds the population growth dimension that the original story left out.

**Hook structure:** Opens with the headline or key claim from the original story, then pivots with "What this story doesn't mention..." or "Here's the number missing from this article." The hook exploits the gap between what the media reports and what the audience suspects is being left out.

**Emotional trigger:** Vindication. The audience already senses that something is being left unsaid about housing, health wait times, or school overcrowding. This content says it for them. The feeling is "finally, someone is connecting the dots."

**Call to action:** Tag the journalist or outlet. "I'd love to see [journalist name] include this data next time. It's publicly available from Stats NZ." This is polite, specific, and creates pressure without being hostile. It also makes the content more likely to reach the journalist through notifications.

**Frequency:** 2-3 per week, timed to the news cycle. Every week produces multiple stories that can be reframed.

**Example 1:**

> Stuff reports today that Auckland hospital wait times have hit record levels. 47,000 people waiting longer than four months for specialist treatment.
>
> Here's what the article doesn't mention: Auckland's population grew by 52,000 in the last year alone. That's the equivalent of dropping the entire city of Blenheim into Auckland and expecting the same hospitals, the same GPs, the same specialists to absorb them.
>
> Auckland DHB's funding increased 3.2%. Its population grew 3.6%. Every year the gap widens. Every year you wait longer.
>
> This isn't a healthcare funding problem. It's a population planning problem. We keep adding people without adding the services they need.
>
> Stats NZ migration data is public. MBIE visa approvals are public. Hospital capacity data is public. Why doesn't anyone put these three things next to each other?

**Example 2:**

> NZ Herald front page: "First-home buyers frozen out as median house price hits $850,000."
>
> Some context that didn't make the article.
>
> For every 100,000 net migrants, New Zealand needs roughly 38,000 new dwellings. That's not my number -- it's from Stats NZ household size data.
>
> In 2023-24, net migration was 130,000. Required dwellings from migration alone: around 49,000. Actual consents nationwide: 34,000. We didn't even build enough for the new arrivals, let alone existing residents who already couldn't find a home.
>
> House prices aren't a mystery. They're supply and demand. And the demand side is being driven by policy decisions that no party has been willing to explain or justify.

**Example 3:**

> RNZ reports that Wellington's bus network is "at breaking point." Routes cut, drivers unavailable, commuters stranded.
>
> Wellington's population in 2018: 212,000. Wellington's population in 2025: approximately 225,000. That's 13,000 more people using a bus network that hasn't added capacity in a decade.
>
> Here's the question nobody is asking: when the government approves migration levels that grow a city by 6% in seven years, who is responsible for making sure the city can actually function? The answer right now is: nobody. There is no requirement for immigration approvals to be linked to infrastructure capacity. None. Zero.
>
> The buses aren't broken. The planning is.

### Pillar 3: The OIA Reveal

**Format:** Screenshot of the actual government document with key lines highlighted, accompanied by a 200-400 word post explaining what it means. Also works as a 30-60 second video where you show the document on screen and talk through the finding. This is your exclusive content -- nobody else has it.

**Hook structure:** "I asked [government department] a simple question. Here's what they told me." The hook is the implicit drama of government transparency -- you asked, they answered (or didn't), and what they revealed is newsworthy.

**Emotional trigger:** The thrill of accountability. The audience feels like they're seeing behind the curtain. Government documents carry inherent authority and a slight feeling of "leaked" information, even when they're legally obtained through a routine OIA request.

**Call to action:** "If you're a journalist and want the full document, DM me." This serves two purposes: it positions you as a source (building journalist relationships) and it signals to the audience that this is real, original information worth professional attention.

**Frequency:** 1-2 per week once the OIA pipeline is running (starting around week 4-5). This becomes more frequent over time as more responses arrive.

**Example 1:**

> I OIA'd the Ministry of Business, Innovation and Employment asking for any internal advice to ministers on the infrastructure implications of net migration levels above 100,000 per year.
>
> [Screenshot of response document, key passage highlighted]
>
> They told me the advice exists but is being withheld under section 9(2)(f)(iv) -- "to maintain the confidentiality of advice tendered by Ministers of the Crown and officials."
>
> Read that again. The government has internal advice about whether our infrastructure can handle current migration levels. They're choosing not to share it with the public.
>
> Why? What does the advice say? If the advice said "everything is fine," they'd release it. You only withhold advice that contradicts your public position.
>
> I'm appealing this decision to the Ombudsman. The public has a right to know whether the government's own advisors think our infrastructure can cope.
>
> If you think the government should release this advice, share this post.

**Example 2:**

> I OIA'd Stats NZ asking for the internal population projections they provide to government agencies for planning purposes, broken down by region.
>
> [Screenshot of document]
>
> Here's what jumped out. Stats NZ projects Auckland will add another 200,000 people by 2030. Their projections assume net migration of roughly 50,000 per year (which is conservative relative to recent actuals).
>
> I then OIA'd Auckland Council asking what infrastructure investment is budgeted based on these projections.
>
> Auckland Council's Long-Term Plan assumes population growth but does not map infrastructure investment to specific population milestones. In plain language: they know the people are coming. They have no funded plan for the roads, pipes, schools, or hospitals those people will need.
>
> This is public planning operating on vibes.

**Example 3:**

> I asked the Ministry of Education how many new school classrooms were required in 2023-24 directly attributable to population growth from migration, versus natural increase.
>
> [Screenshot]
>
> Their answer: "The Ministry does not separately attribute demand for school places to migration versus other sources of population growth."
>
> They literally don't track it. The government adds 130,000 people through migration in a single year and the Ministry of Education doesn't know how many school places that requires.
>
> This is the planning gap in one sentence. We have immigration policy over here. We have education planning over there. Nobody connects them. Your kid's overcrowded classroom is the result.

### Pillar 4: The International Comparison

**Format:** Side-by-side graphic or short video comparing NZ's approach to immigration planning with what other comparable countries do. Clean, factual, designed to make NZ's lack of a plan look like an outlier.

**Hook structure:** "Here's what [Country] does. Here's what New Zealand does. Spot the difference." The hook exploits national pride and competitive instinct. Kiwis care about how we compare to Australia, Canada, and the UK.

**Emotional trigger:** Embarrassment mixed with frustration. The feeling that NZ is falling behind, that other countries have figured out something basic that we haven't. This is distinct from the anger of the Stat Drop -- it's more "why can't we get our act together?"

**Call to action:** "Every country on this list has a population plan except New Zealand. Ask your MP why."

**Frequency:** 1 per week. This content has a longer shelf life than reactive content and works well on weekends when the news cycle is slow.

**Example 1:**

> Australia: Has an annual population statement. The Centre for Population publishes projections. Infrastructure spending is explicitly linked to population growth forecasts. Migration levels are reviewed annually with public input.
>
> Canada: Publishes an Immigration Levels Plan every year, projecting three years ahead. It states exactly how many permanent residents will be admitted and in which categories. When the numbers got out of control in 2023-24, the government cut targets publicly and explained why.
>
> United Kingdom: The Office for Budget Responsibility publishes fiscal impact assessments of migration. The Migration Advisory Committee reviews salary thresholds and skills needs. There is an annual net migration estimate that drives public debate.
>
> New Zealand: No population plan. No annual migration target. No published link between migration levels and infrastructure investment. No independent migration advisory body. The "Immigration: Fit for the Future" report in 2022 recommended most of these things. None have been implemented.
>
> We're not asking for anything radical. We're asking for what every comparable country already does.

**Example 2:**

> How do you know if your country has too many or too few migrants? In Australia, the Centre for Population runs the numbers and publishes them. In Canada, the government sets targets three years out so cities can plan. In Singapore, work permits are calibrated quarterly to economic conditions.
>
> In New Zealand, we have no target, no published modelling, and no planning link between migration approvals and infrastructure delivery.
>
> We're running a country of 5 million people with less population planning than a council does for a subdivision.

**Example 3:**

> Canada just cut its immigration targets by 20%. Australia reduced its permanent migration intake by 25,000. The UK introduced minimum salary thresholds.
>
> All three countries concluded they'd been growing faster than their infrastructure could handle. All three published their reasoning. All three faced public scrutiny and debate.
>
> New Zealand grew faster than all of them (2.8% vs OECD average 0.7%) and has done... nothing. No review. No target. No plan.
>
> When Australia, Canada, and the UK all independently reach the same conclusion and NZ doesn't even ask the question, something is seriously wrong with our policy process.

### Pillar 5: The Accountability Question

**Format:** A direct, specific, named question to a specific party leader or spokesperson. Posted publicly on all platforms. Designed to put the politician in a position where silence is damaging.

**Hook structure:** "I have one question for [Politician Name]." The hook is the specificity and the directness. Not a vague challenge. A single, precise question that requires a number or a yes/no answer. Politicians can dodge open-ended questions. They can't dodge "what number do you think is right?" without looking evasive.

**Emotional trigger:** Empowerment. The audience feels represented. Someone is asking the question they want asked. The underlying emotion is "yes, exactly, why WON'T they answer that?"

**Call to action:** "If you want an answer, share this until they can't ignore it." This directly ties sharing to political pressure -- the most motivating CTA in the playbook.

**Frequency:** 1 per week, every week, with tracking. This pillar doesn't start until month 2-3, once there's an audience to amplify it.

**Example 1:**

> One question for the Prime Minister.
>
> Stats NZ data shows New Zealand added 130,000 net migrants in a single year, growing our population by 2.8%. The OECD average was 0.7%.
>
> My question: what number of net migrants per year do you think is right for New Zealand?
>
> Not a range. Not "it depends." A number. The same way you have a target for the budget deficit, a target for inflation, a target for unemployment.
>
> What's the target for net migration?
>
> If you don't have one, please explain how cities are supposed to plan schools, hospitals, roads, and housing without knowing how many people are coming.
>
> This is the most basic planning question a government can face. Every comparable country answers it. New Zealand doesn't.
>
> I'll be posting the response -- or the silence -- next week.

**Example 2:**

> One question for the Labour housing spokesperson.
>
> Labour's housing policy proposes building 10,000 additional public homes over 10 years.
>
> In the year to February 2024, net migration alone created demand for roughly 49,000 homes.
>
> 10,000 over ten years. 49,000 needed in one year.
>
> Does Labour accept that their housing policy cannot work without an immigration policy? Or do they believe population growth has no impact on housing demand?
>
> It has to be one or the other.

**Example 3:**

> One question for the National Party immigration spokesperson.
>
> National campaigned in 2023 on reducing immigration bureaucracy and attracting skilled workers. Net migration then hit 130,000 in a single year.
>
> How many net migrants per year does National consider to be the right level for New Zealand? Is 130,000 too many? Is 50,000 right? 80,000?
>
> If National doesn't have a number, how is that different from having no policy?
>
> Serious question. I'll publish whatever answer I receive. Silence will also be published.

### Pillar 6: The Local Impact Story

**Format:** 60-90 second video or 300-500 word post that starts with a specific local situation -- a school with portable classrooms overflowing, a GP clinic with a six-week wait, a suburb where traffic has doubled -- and connects it to population growth data for that area.

**Hook structure:** Starts with the specific, local, human detail. "In Papakura, parents are being told their kids can't get into the local school." Then zooms out to the data. "Papakura's population grew by X% in the last five years. School capacity grew by Y%." The hook works because local specificity is more emotionally engaging than national statistics.

**Emotional trigger:** Recognition and frustration. The audience sees their own experience reflected. "That's MY suburb. That's MY school. That's MY commute." This is the most powerful emotional trigger in the entire strategy because it makes abstract policy personal.

**Call to action:** "If this is happening in your area too, share your story in the comments." This generates user-created content and builds community.

**Frequency:** 1-2 per week. Rotates through different regions and different services (health, education, housing, transport) to build a nationwide picture.

**Example 1:**

> Rolleston, Canterbury. Ten years ago it was a small town. Today it's one of the fastest-growing areas in New Zealand. Population has roughly tripled.
>
> What hasn't tripled: the number of GPs. The size of the school. The width of the roads.
>
> Parents in Rolleston are driving 30 minutes to get their kids to a doctor. The local school has gone from 400 students to over 1,200 with temporary classrooms covering what used to be playing fields. The main road into Christchurch is gridlocked every morning.
>
> Who planned this? In theory, Selwyn District Council and central government collaborate on growth planning. In practice, the visa approvals happen in Wellington, the people arrive, and the infrastructure catches up years later. If it catches up at all.
>
> The families who moved to Rolleston for an affordable home and a good lifestyle are now living with the consequences of growth that was approved but not planned for.
>
> This is what "no population plan" looks like at street level.

**Example 2:**

> Tauranga. The average wait for a non-urgent surgical procedure at Tauranga Hospital is now over 300 days.
>
> Tauranga's population in 2013: 120,000. In 2024: approximately 160,000. That's a 33% increase.
>
> Hospital capacity has not increased by 33%. Not even close. The same building, roughly the same number of beds, serving a city that has grown by a third. Where did those 40,000 extra people come from? Some internal migration from Auckland. Some natural growth. And a significant share from international migration flowing into the Bay of Plenty.
>
> Nobody is blaming the people who moved here. But someone approved growth of 33% without building 33% more hospital capacity. That's not the migrants' fault. That's the government's fault.

**Example 3:**

> Queenstown. Average rent: $720 per week for a three-bedroom house. Average hospitality wage: $24 per hour.
>
> Queenstown's economy runs on migrant workers. Hotels, restaurants, ski fields -- they physically cannot operate without migrants. Nobody disputes this.
>
> Here's the problem. The government approves the work visas. The businesses get their workers. But nobody builds the houses for those workers to live in. So migrant workers are living six to a room, paying half their wages in rent, in a town where building a new house costs over $800,000.
>
> This is cruelty dressed up as economic policy. It's bad for the workers, bad for the locals whose rents get pushed up, and bad for the businesses who can't retain staff because nobody can afford to live here.
>
> A population plan would match visa approvals to housing capacity. We don't have one.

### Pillar 7: The Weekly Wrap

**Format:** A 3-5 minute video or 800-1200 word written post published every Friday. Summarises the week's immigration-related news, adds context the media missed, previews what's coming. Think of it as a weekly briefing for people who want to stay informed but don't have time to track everything.

**Hook structure:** "Here's what happened this week on immigration that you probably missed." The hook is curation -- in an overwhelming news environment, the audience values someone who filters and contextualises.

**Emotional trigger:** Belonging and being informed. The audience feels part of an informed community. They can share the wrap with friends and family as a "you should know about this" resource. This is the lowest-anger content in the mix, which is deliberate -- it provides relief from the more confrontational pillars and builds the habit of regular engagement.

**Call to action:** "Follow for next week's wrap. And if there's a story I missed, let me know in the comments." This drives follows (not just shares) and builds community engagement.

**Frequency:** Every Friday, without exception. Consistency is the entire point. Miss a week and you break the habit loop.

**Example 1 (abbreviated):**

> Week in review: 14-18 April 2026
>
> Three things happened this week that connect to population planning.
>
> First, Stats NZ released updated migration estimates. Net migration for the year to March 2026 came in at [X]. That's [up/down] from the previous quarter. Here's what that means in practical terms: [comparison to housing consents, infrastructure spending].
>
> Second, the Health Minister announced $200 million for hospital upgrades. Sounds good. But I ran the numbers against population growth projections for the affected regions, and the investment covers roughly [X%] of the additional capacity needed. It's a Band-Aid on a structural problem.
>
> Third, an under-reported story: [local council] quietly updated its infrastructure plan and buried a line about population assumptions. They're now planning for growth of [X] but only funding infrastructure for growth of [Y]. The gap is [Z thousand people] who will arrive with no services waiting for them.
>
> Plus a quick international update: [Country] announced [policy change]. The trend across the developed world is toward managed migration. New Zealand remains an outlier.
>
> That's the week. If you found this useful, follow along. Same time next Friday.

**Example 2 (abbreviated):**

> Week in review: 21-25 April 2026
>
> This was the week [Party] released their immigration policy -- or what passes for one.
>
> I went through the full document. Here's what's in it, what's missing, and what it actually means for the numbers.
>
> [Party] proposes [specific measures]. Based on MBIE data, these measures would affect roughly [X] visa holders per year. Total net migration impact: probably [Y]. That would bring net migration from [current] to [adjusted], which is still [above/below] what infrastructure investment can support.
>
> Key omission: no stated target number. No link to housing or infrastructure capacity. No independent review mechanism.
>
> I scored it against our five criteria [link to scorecard]. Grade: [X]. Here's why.
>
> The scorecard now shows: [Party A: grade], [Party B: grade], [Party C: no policy released], [Party D: no policy released].
>
> We're [X] months from an election. Two major parties still haven't told us how many people they plan to add to the country. That should bother you regardless of which party you vote for.

**Example 3 (abbreviated):**

> Week in review: 28 April - 2 May 2026
>
> Quiet week on immigration in the headlines. Busy week underneath.
>
> I received three OIA responses this week. One from MBIE, two from regional councils. The standout finding: [specific revelation]. I'll do a full post on this Monday, but the short version is [one-sentence summary].
>
> Meanwhile, I've been tracking what's happening in Australia and Canada. Both countries released updated migration data this week. Australia's net migration is now [X], down from [Y] after deliberate policy tightening. Canada's is [X], trending [direction] after their cuts. New Zealand's equivalent figure: still no target, still no review, still no plan.
>
> Coming next week: I'm going to publish the full mid-year scorecard update, incorporating the OIA data. Some parties' grades are going to change. Not upward.
>
> Follow for the update. Share the wrap if someone you know should be paying attention to this.

## 2. THE CONTENT CALENDAR

### Weekly Rhythm

| Day | Content | Why This Day |
|---|---|---|
| Monday | Stat Drop + OIA Reveal (when available) | Sets the tone for the week. Monday is when political journalists plan their week. A strong stat or OIA finding on Monday morning has the highest chance of feeding into the week's coverage. |
| Tuesday | Reframe of Monday's news | Tuesday is when the week's major stories are established. By Tuesday afternoon, there are always housing/health/infrastructure stories to reframe. |
| Wednesday | Accountability Question | Mid-week, peak social media engagement in NZ. A direct question to a named politician generates discussion through Wednesday and Thursday. |
| Thursday | Local Impact Story or International Comparison | Alternating weeks. Thursday content is slightly longer-form, designed for people who engage more deeply later in the work week. |
| Friday | Weekly Wrap | Consistent end-of-week summary. People check in Friday afternoon. The wrap becomes a habit: "I'll catch up on Friday." |
| Saturday | Stat Drop (lighter, more visual) | Weekend content is simpler and more shareable. People browse casually. A single striking image or short video works best. |
| Sunday | Rest / bank content for next week | Burnout prevention. One day off. Use it to prep Monday's content. |

### How the Rhythm Builds Over Weeks

**Weeks 1-4 (Foundation):** The calendar runs at 60% capacity. Not all pillars are active yet. Focus on Stat Drops (3-4/week) and Reframes (2-3/week). The OIA pipeline is being built but hasn't produced results yet. Accountability Questions don't start yet -- there's no audience to amplify them. The goal is consistent posting that trains the algorithm and builds the first 500-1,000 followers.

**Weeks 5-8 (First OIA returns):** OIA Reveals start appearing. This is exclusive content nobody else has, which accelerates growth. The International Comparison pillar starts. The Weekly Wrap launches. Full calendar is now running. Target: 1,500-3,000 followers.

**Weeks 9-12 (Accountability launch):** The Accountability Question pillar activates. By now there's enough audience to make silence from politicians visible. The Local Impact Story pillar starts, requiring more production effort but delivering the most emotionally resonant content. All seven pillars are now active. Target: 3,000-8,000 followers.

**Weeks 13-20 (Escalation):** Content volume increases slightly (Claude Code is handling more of the production pipeline). OIA Reveals become more frequent as the pipeline matures. Accountability Questions begin tracking responses and non-responses publicly. The scorecard becomes a recurring feature. Content begins explicitly tying to the election timeline. Target: 8,000-15,000 followers.

**Weeks 21-26 (Election push):** Content peaks in volume and sharpness. Every party policy release gets an immediate Reframe. The scorecard is updated in real time. Accountability Questions become more pointed. Local Impact Stories target swing electorates specifically. The Weekly Wrap becomes a must-read for anyone following election coverage. Target: 15,000-25,000 followers.

## 3. VIRAL MECHANICS

### What Makes Immigration Content Shareable in NZ

There are five mechanics that drive sharing of political content in New Zealand specifically, and they differ from what works in the US, UK, or Australia.

**Mechanic 1: "I've been thinking this but couldn't say it."** The single most powerful sharing trigger in NZ immigration content. New Zealanders have strong social norms around not appearing racist. Many people have concerns about immigration and population growth but self-censor because they fear social judgement. Content that articulates their concern in a way that feels safe to share -- data-driven, non-racial, focused on infrastructure -- gives them permission. They share it because the act of sharing says "see, this is what I mean, and it's not racist." The content is doing emotional labour for the sharer.

This is why the framing matters more than anything else. Content that says "too many immigrants" will not be shared by the persuadable middle, even if they agree, because sharing it marks them socially. Content that says "we added 130,000 people and didn't build a single hospital" gets shared because it's a planning critique, not a racial one. Same underlying concern, radically different shareability.

**Mechanic 2: Local specificity beats national statistics.** "Auckland hospital wait times are up 40%" gets likes. "Your kid's school in Papakura now has 200 more students than it was built for" gets shares. The difference is personal relevance. People share content that describes their experience. National statistics feel abstract. Local details feel personal.

This is why the Local Impact Story pillar exists. It's designed for sharing. When someone in Rolleston sees a post about Rolleston's infrastructure gap, they share it to their local Facebook group, their family chat, their neighbours. That local sharing is worth more than any number of retweets from political commentators, because it reaches people outside the political bubble.

**Mechanic 3: Government documents and data screenshots.** In NZ, there's a deep cultural trust in official data combined with a suspicion that politicians are hiding things. Content that shows actual government documents -- OIA responses, Stats NZ tables, council reports -- gets shared because it feels authoritative and slightly transgressive. "Look what I found in the government's own data" is an inherently shareable frame. The screenshot of an official document carries more weight than any amount of text, because it looks like evidence.

**Mechanic 4: The "tag your MP" mechanic.** Content that names a specific politician and asks a specific question generates shares because the act of sharing becomes an act of political participation. "I'm sharing this because I also want an answer" is a low-effort way for people to feel politically active. This is why the Accountability Questions always name a specific person and ask a specific question. Vague questions don't generate the tagging/sharing behaviour. Specific ones do.

**Mechanic 5: The comparison frame.** "Australia has a population plan. Canada has one. The UK has one. New Zealand doesn't." This generates shares because it triggers national pride and competitive instinct. Kiwis care deeply about how we compare to peer countries, especially Australia. Content that makes NZ look like a laggard gets shared with an implicit "this is embarrassing" sentiment.

### The Critical Difference: Likes vs Shares

Likes are acknowledgement. Shares are advocacy. They are completely different behaviours with completely different triggers.

**Content that gets likes but not shares:** Hot takes. Angry rants. Preaching to the choir. Content that makes already-convinced people nod along. "Immigration is out of control" gets likes from people who already agree. It doesn't get shared because sharing it feels like picking a fight.

**Content that gets shares:** Content that makes the sharer look informed, reasonable, and brave. Content that does the emotional work of expressing a concern in a socially safe way. Content with a specific, striking fact that the sharer didn't know and wants others to know. Content that feels like a public service ("you should see this") rather than a political statement ("I believe this").

The practical implication: every piece of content should pass the "would a moderate, slightly cautious person share this on their personal feed?" test. If the answer is no, the framing needs adjustment. The goal is not to create content that the already-convinced love. It's to create content that the persuadable middle feels comfortable passing along.

### Platform-Specific Sharing Dynamics

**X/Twitter:** Retweets with comment are the most valuable action. Content should be designed to invite commentary -- leave a gap for people to add their own experience. Threads perform better than single tweets for complex content. The NZ political X community is small enough that a thread with 50+ retweets will be seen by most political journalists.

**TikTok:** Shares to DMs and other apps matter more than likes or comments. Content that people send to a specific friend ("you need to see this") outperforms content that people like passively. The hook must be in the first 2 seconds. Text on screen matters more than audio for political content in NZ (people watch without sound). Local specificity drives TikTok sharing more than national stats.

**Facebook:** Shares to groups are the key mechanic. Content posted to a personal profile that gets shared into a local community group (e.g., "Tauranga Community Noticeboard") reaches an audience that is otherwise unreachable. This is where the Local Impact Stories do their best work. Facebook audiences are older and more likely to vote, making them disproportionately valuable for political pressure.

## 4. THE DATA-TO-CONTENT PIPELINE

### Sources

Five primary data sources feed the content engine:

1. **Stats NZ migration data.** Released quarterly (International Travel and Migration statistics). Contains net migration figures, breakdowns by visa type, and regional data. This is the backbone of the entire campaign.

2. **MBIE visa and immigration data.** Published monthly and quarterly. Contains visa approval numbers by category, employer accreditation data, and sector breakdowns. More granular than Stats NZ.

3. **OIA responses.** Self-generated through the OIA request pipeline. Contains information that isn't publicly available: internal advice, ministerial briefings, planning documents, projections.

4. **Parliamentary records (Hansard).** Contains every statement made in Parliament. Searchable by topic and speaker. This feeds the accountability tracker -- logging what politicians say about immigration so contradictions can be surfaced later.

5. **Council long-term plans and infrastructure strategies.** Published by every council. Contain population projections and infrastructure budgets. The gap between projected population and funded infrastructure is a recurring content goldmine.

### The Production Process: Raw Data to Published Post

**Step 1: Monitoring (Automated with Claude Code)**

Claude Code monitors for new data releases from Stats NZ, MBIE, and parliamentary records. It flags anything immigration-related. It also monitors NZ media for stories that can be reframed. This runs continuously. When something is flagged, it enters the production queue.

Target: flag new material within 2 hours of publication.

**Step 2: Analysis (Claude Code + Human Judgement)**

Claude Code does the first-pass analysis: extracting key numbers, comparing to historical data, identifying the most striking finding, and drafting a plain-language summary. The human reviews the analysis, checks it against their growing knowledge of the issue, and identifies the editorial angle. The human decides: is this a Stat Drop, a Reframe, an OIA Reveal, or something else?

Target: analysis complete within 1 hour of flagging.

**Step 3: Drafting (Claude Code)**

Claude Code drafts the content in the appropriate format. It follows the hook structure, emotional trigger, and CTA template for the relevant pillar. The draft includes the source citation, the key number, the comparison or frame, and the call to action.

Target: first draft within 15 minutes of analysis completion.

**Step 4: Editing (Human)**

The human reviews the draft for tone (is this how a concerned Kiwi would actually talk?), accuracy (are the numbers right and the sources solid?), and strategic fit (does this serve the campaign's current phase?). The human adjusts the voice, sharpens the hook, and adds any personal or local knowledge that Claude Code doesn't have.

Target: editing complete within 30 minutes.

**Step 5: Production (Claude Code + Free Tools)**

For graphics: Claude Code generates the data visualisation specifications, which get built in Canva (free tier) or a similar tool. Simple bar charts, comparison tables, and highlighted statistics.

For video: the human records the video component (face-to-camera or voiceover). Claude Code helps with the script and generates any text overlays or data cards that appear on screen.

For text posts: Claude Code formats for each platform (X thread structure, Facebook post format, etc.).

Target: production complete within 30 minutes.

**Step 6: Publishing and Distribution**

Post to primary platform (X for data/accountability content, TikTok for video content, Facebook for local impact stories). Cross-post to secondary platforms. Tag relevant journalists and politicians where appropriate. Schedule timing based on the content calendar.

Target: publish within 4 hours of the initial data flag for reactive content. Scheduled content follows the weekly calendar.

### The OIA Pipeline Specifically

This is the campaign's most powerful original content source and it needs its own process.

**Request generation:** Claude Code maintains a master list of potential OIA requests, categorised by agency and topic. Each request is drafted using proper legal language and specific enough framing to avoid the common refusal grounds. The human reviews each request before sending.

**Volume:** Start with 10 requests in week 1. Scale to 20 active requests by month 2. By month 3, aim for 30-50 active requests across multiple agencies. Claude Code tracks every request, its deadline, and its status.

**Processing responses:** When a response arrives, Claude Code reads and analyses the documents, extracts key findings, flags anything newsworthy, compares the response to public statements by politicians, and drafts both a social media post and a longer analysis. The human reviews and decides whether to publish immediately or hold for strategic timing.

**Refusals and partial releases:** These are content too. A refusal under specific OIA grounds tells you something about what the government is protecting. Claude Code helps draft the Ombudsman complaint (free) and the social media post about the refusal.

**The compounding effect:** Every OIA response teaches you something that informs the next batch of requests. Early requests are broad. Later requests are targeted based on what you've learned. By month 3-4, the OIA pipeline is generating exclusive, newsworthy content on a weekly basis that no other source in New Zealand produces.

## 5. REACTIVE CONTENT

### The Three Categories of Reactive Opportunity

**Category 1: Data releases.** Stats NZ migration data, MBIE visa data, housing data, health statistics. These are scheduled and predictable. You know roughly when they're coming. Preparation is possible.

Response time target: within 4 hours of release, ideally within 2. The goal is to be the first credible voice providing immigration-linked analysis of the new data.

Template:
> [New data point] just dropped from [source].
>
> Here's the number that matters: [specific figure].
>
> Context: [comparison to previous period / other countries / infrastructure capacity].
>
> What this means: [one-sentence plain-language implication].
>
> What no party has explained: [the accountability gap].

**Category 2: Political statements.** A minister makes a claim about immigration. A party releases a policy. An MP says something in Parliament. These are unpredictable in timing but the response framework is consistent.

Response time target: within 2 hours. Speed is critical here because the first response frames the story. If a minister claims the government has immigration under control, and your data-backed rebuttal is the first thing journalists see in their X timeline, your frame shapes the coverage.

Template:
> [Politician] just said [quote].
>
> Here's what the data actually shows: [contradicting or contextualising fact from your database].
>
> [Source citation].
>
> This is the [Nth] time [politician/party] has [made this claim / avoided this question / contradicted their previous statement].
>
> [Link to accountability tracker or previous post documenting the pattern].

**Category 3: Immigration-adjacent news stories.** Housing crisis stories, hospital wait time reports, school overcrowding, infrastructure failures, employment data. These are daily occurrences that can be reframed.

Response time target: within 4-6 hours. These are less time-sensitive because you're adding context to someone else's story, not competing to break news.

Template:
> [Outlet] is reporting [headline summary].
>
> What the story doesn't mention: [population growth data for the relevant area / service].
>
> In [location], population grew by [X%] over [period]. [Service/infrastructure] capacity grew by [Y%].
>
> The gap: [specific number of people, houses, school places, hospital beds].
>
> This isn't a [health / housing / transport] crisis. It's a population planning crisis showing up in [health / housing / transport].

### The Reactive Content Process

1. **Alert:** Claude Code monitors data releases, political statements, and news via RSS feeds, parliamentary alerts, and keyword monitoring of NZ media sites. Flags relevant items immediately.

2. **Assessment:** Human makes a 30-second call: is this worth responding to? Not everything is. The filter is: does this connect to our core message (planning failure), does it have an audience (will people care?), and can we add something the original story doesn't have (data, context, accountability)?

3. **Production:** Claude Code pulls relevant data from the database, compares to the accountability tracker, and drafts a response using the appropriate template. Human edits for tone and accuracy.

4. **Publication:** Post and tag relevant journalists/politicians.

5. **Follow-up:** If the reactive content gets traction, Claude Code helps produce a longer follow-up piece (thread, video, or blog post) that goes deeper.

The entire reactive cycle from alert to publication should take under 90 minutes for Category 1 and 2, and under 3 hours for Category 3. The speed advantage of a solo operator with AI is real -- organisations have approval processes that take longer than your entire production cycle.

## 6. THE ESCALATION ARC

### Month 1: Establish Presence

**Theme:** "Here are the numbers nobody is talking about."

**Content mix:** 80% Stat Drops and Reframes. 20% International Comparisons. No Accountability Questions yet. No scorecard. No OIA content (pipeline is being built).

**Tone:** Curious and slightly alarmed. Not angry. Not accusatory. "Did you know...?" and "Why doesn't anyone mention...?" The voice is discovering the problem in real time, which is more engaging than lecturing about it.

**What's happening behind the scenes:** Filing the first 20-30 OIA requests. Building the data dashboard. Building the accountability tracker from Hansard records. Engaging with journalists' content on X. Identifying sympathetic voices.

**Success metric:** 500-1,500 followers. At least one journalist has engaged with your content (reply, like, or quote). The posting rhythm is consistent and feels sustainable.

**What changes:** The audience at this stage is mostly the politically engaged. The content is building a foundation of credibility.

### Month 2: Build Credibility

**Theme:** "Nobody is planning for this."

**Content mix:** 50% Stat Drops and Reframes. 20% OIA Reveals (first responses arriving). 15% International Comparisons. 15% Local Impact Stories (starting).

**Tone:** More confident. The "reluctant Kiwi" has now been doing this for weeks and has receipts. Tone shifts from "did you know?" to "here's what the government's own documents say." The authority comes from the OIA material -- you're now publishing original information.

**New elements:** The data dashboard goes live. The first OIA-sourced content posts. TikTok video content starts. The Weekly Wrap launches.

**Success metric:** 1,500-5,000 followers. At least one story you've surfaced (probably an OIA finding) gets picked up by a journalist. Other accounts are starting to cite your data.

**What changes:** The OIA pipeline is now generating exclusive content. The campaign starts to feel like a source, not just an opinion account.

### Month 3: Introduce Accountability

**Theme:** "We asked. They won't answer."

**Content mix:** 30% Stat Drops and Reframes. 20% OIA Reveals. 20% Accountability Questions (launching). 15% Local Impact Stories. 15% International Comparisons and Weekly Wraps.

**Tone:** The shift from informing to demanding. Still data-driven, still measured, but now with an edge. "The government has no plan" becomes "the government refuses to tell you whether they have a plan." The word "refuses" is important. It implies wilful evasion, not mere oversight.

**New elements:** The party scorecard launches. Accountability Questions begin, one per week, naming specific politicians. The scorecard tracks responses and non-responses. The first select committee submission is filed.

**Success metric:** 5,000-10,000 followers. At least one party has responded (even negatively) to your content or questions. Multiple journalists are engaging regularly. The scorecard is generating discussion.

**What changes:** The campaign transitions from audience-building to pressure-building. You're no longer just informing people. You're creating situations where politicians have to respond.

### Month 4: Escalate Pressure

**Theme:** "Election 2026: Who has a plan?"

**Content mix:** Accountability Questions increase to 2 per week. OIA Reveals continue. Reframes focus specifically on election-related content. Local Impact Stories target electorates where immigration-driven growth is most visible.

**Tone:** Urgency. "The election is [X] months away and [Party] still hasn't told us how many people they plan to add to the country." The clock is ticking. Time pressure makes non-response look worse.

**New elements:** Coalition pressure mapping begins. Content starts explicitly addressing what immigration policy will look like under different coalition scenarios. Candidate question packs go out to electorate candidates, not just party leaders.

**Success metric:** 10,000-15,000 followers. Immigration is being mentioned in mainstream election coverage, at least partly because of your work. Multiple parties have engaged with the scorecard. You have established relationships with at least 3-5 political journalists who treat you as a source.

**What changes:** The campaign is now part of the political conversation, not just commenting on it. Content is shaping coverage rather than reacting to it.

### Month 5: Force Responses

**Theme:** "The silence is the story."

**Content mix:** Heavy on Accountability content. The scorecard is updated weekly. Every party policy release gets an immediate assessment. Candidate confrontations at public events (filming questions and responses on camera). OIA material continues flowing and gets sharper (targeted requests based on what you've learned).

**Tone:** Firm and relentless. Not angry -- persistent. "We've been asking this question for four months. Here is every time we asked. Here is every non-response. The silence tells you everything." The pattern of asking and being ignored becomes its own narrative.

**New elements:** Video content of candidate confrontations at public events. A "mid-campaign scorecard" update that gets pitched to media as a story. Direct pitches to journalists: "I have six months of data and OIA material showing no party has a coherent population plan. Do you want an exclusive?"

**Success metric:** 15,000-20,000 followers. At least one mainstream media piece that cites your work or data. Immigration is part of the election debate. The scorecard has been referenced by a journalist or politician in a public forum.

**What changes:** The goal shifts from building audience to converting audience into political outcomes. The content now exists to support specific asks: answer these questions, release this data, publish a population plan.

### Month 6: Election Mode

**Theme:** "Vote like the plan matters."

**Content mix:** Maximum volume. Every significant campaign event gets a response. The final scorecard is published. A "voter guide" on immigration policy is released, grading every party and every local candidate who responded (or didn't). Live coverage of election-related events with real-time immigration analysis.

**Tone:** Direct and clear. "Here's where every party stands. Here's who answered and who didn't. Make your choice." No endorsements of specific parties. The power is in the data and the accountability record, not in telling people who to vote for.

**New elements:** The "immigration voter guide" -- a clean, shareable summary of every party's position (or lack thereof). Real-time analysis of debate performances and campaign statements. Targeted content for swing electorates where population growth is highest.

**Success metric:** 20,000+ followers. Immigration is a top-5 election issue in at least some polling. The scorecard and voter guide get shared widely. At least one party has adjusted their position or committed to a specific migration target in response to sustained pressure. Multiple media outlets have covered the campaign or used its data.

**What changes:** The campaign has achieved its minimum viable goal: immigration is on the election agenda. The six-month body of work -- hundreds of data posts, dozens of OIA findings, a comprehensive accountability record -- exists as a permanent resource that outlasts the election cycle.

## EDITORIAL REVIEW

I've gone through this strategy as if I were a senior editor at a major digital publisher. Here are the weaknesses I found and how I've addressed them.

**Weakness 1: Volume assumptions are aggressive.** The calendar calls for 6-7 pieces of content per week across multiple formats. For a solo operator, even with Claude Code, this is a lot. The fix: months 1-2 run at reduced capacity (4-5 pieces per week). The full calendar only activates in month 3 once the workflow is established and Claude Code is handling more of the pipeline. If production proves unsustainable, cut the Saturday Stat Drop and the International Comparison first -- they're the least critical pillars.

**Weakness 2: Follower targets may be optimistic.** NZ's political social media audience is small. 20,000+ followers in 6 months is possible but not guaranteed. The fix: the strategy doesn't actually depend on hitting those specific numbers. The mechanism of influence works through journalists and the political class, not through raw follower count. If the right 200 people (journalists, staffers, MPs) are following, the campaign can be effective at much lower total numbers. The real metric is "has a journalist cited your work," not follower count.

**Weakness 3: The OIA pipeline has a cold-start problem.** OIA responses take 20 working days, which means no OIA content for nearly a month. The fix: this is already accounted for in the escalation arc (month 1 is OIA-free). But it means months 1 content has to work harder without the exclusive material that OIA responses provide. Front-load the best publicly available data into month 1 to compensate.

**Weakness 4: Reactive content could overwhelm planned content.** If news breaks daily and you're always responding, the planned pillars get crowded out. The fix: set a rule -- maximum 2 reactive pieces per week. If more reactive opportunities arise, pick the best one and let the rest go. The planned content calendar is what builds the audience. Reactive content supplements it but should not replace it.

**Weakness 5: The strategy assumes immigration stays in the news.** If net migration drops sharply and the public mood shifts, the campaign loses urgency. The fix: the infrastructure-and-planning frame is resilient to migration number fluctuations. Even if net migration drops to 30,000, the infrastructure deficit created by the boom years persists. "We added 130,000 people in a year and we're still catching up on the infrastructure" works regardless of current flow. The argument is about planning, not about current numbers.

**Weakness 6: Risk of appearing as a single-issue obsessive.** In NZ, being seen as fixated on one thing triggers tall poppy and "who does this person think they are" responses. The fix: the Weekly Wrap and the variety of pillars help. Showing you can connect immigration to housing, health, education, transport, and wages demonstrates breadth. The International Comparison pillar shows you're informed about the wider world. And the "reluctant Kiwi" persona -- someone who'd rather not be doing this but feels they have to -- is specifically designed to counter the "obsessive campaigner" perception.

**Weakness 7: No mention of community building beyond content.** Content alone doesn't build a movement. The fix: from month 3 onwards, actively cultivate a small community of regular engagers. Respond to every substantive comment. Elevate other voices who share good data or local stories. Create a simple way for people to contribute (a shared email for local stories, a submission template for their own OIA requests). The campaign should feel like a growing community, not a broadcast channel.

---

This strategy is built for one person with a phone, Claude Code, and the willingness to show up every day for six months. It will feel like shouting into the void for the first few weeks. That's normal. The compounding mechanism takes time to activate. The person who runs this campaign needs to understand that the gap between "nothing is happening" and "something is happening" closes suddenly, not gradually. Keep posting through the silence. The infrastructure -- the data, the OIA pipeline, the accountability tracker, the audience -- compounds invisibly until it doesn't.
