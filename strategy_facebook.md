# Facebook Strategy: Making Immigration an Unavoidable Election Issue in New Zealand (2026)

## Overview

This is a campaign plan to use Facebook and Meta platforms to force immigration into the centre of New Zealand's 2026 election debate. The goal is not to advocate for a single policy position but to create such intense public demand for answers that every party is forced to publish detailed immigration policy. NZ First already owns this space. The opportunity is to make every other party -- National, Labour, ACT, Greens, Te Pati Maori -- feel the cost of staying silent.

Facebook is the right battlefield. In New Zealand, Facebook reaches roughly 3.4 million monthly active users. It dominates outside Auckland, especially in provincial towns, among over-40s, and in Maori and Pacific communities. Facebook Groups are where New Zealand actually argues about politics. X is Wellington journalists talking to each other. Facebook is where voters live.

## 1. Content Strategy

### What formats work on Facebook in NZ

**Short-form video (Reels, under 90 seconds)** is the highest-reach format on Facebook in 2026. The algorithm pushes Reels to non-followers, which is exactly what you need for awareness. But Facebook is not TikTok. The audience skews older and more patient. You can pair Reels with longer formats that Facebook still rewards in Groups and on Pages.

**The format stack, ranked by priority:**

1. Reels (60-90 seconds). Talking head or text-on-screen with a strong hook in the first 2 seconds. These are your reach engine. Every piece of content should have a Reel version.

2. Long-form text posts (300-800 words). Facebook still rewards these in Groups when they generate comments. These are your depth engine. They work because people in NZ Facebook Groups actually read and argue.

3. Shareable graphics (single image with a stat or question). These are your share engine. People share images to their personal timelines and into Groups. Design them to be self-contained -- the message must land without any caption.

4. Facebook Lives. Going live in a Group or on a Page still gets priority notification delivery. Use these for events, Q&As, and "reaction" moments when news breaks.

5. Polls and question posts. Facebook's native poll format gets engagement, but the real move is posting questions as text ("What should NZ's annual immigration target be? Drop a number below.") because comment-based engagement feeds the algorithm harder than poll clicks.

6. Carousel posts. Good for "myth vs fact" or "here's what each party says about immigration" comparison content. Swipeable, shareable, informational.

Stories and standard video posts are lower priority. Stories disappear and have low reach on Pages. Standard video posts have been deprioritised relative to Reels.

### Content themes that drive immigration discussion

The content has to connect immigration to things people already care about. Abstract policy debate does not go viral on Facebook. Lived experience does.

**Housing.** "We added 110,000 people through net migration last year. Where are they supposed to live?" This is the strongest bridge issue because housing stress is felt everywhere in NZ, and the connection to population growth is intuitive. Show local examples: "Tauranga added X people, built Y houses."

**Healthcare.** "Your ED wait is 8 hours. The population grew by a city the size of Nelson in two years. The hospital didn't." Healthcare wait times are viscerally felt and frequently discussed on local Facebook pages already.

**Infrastructure and traffic.** Provincial towns that have grown fast (Tauranga, Hamilton, Queenstown, Christchurch) have visible congestion and infrastructure strain. This is inherently local content, which is what Facebook rewards.

**Wages and jobs.** "Why are wages flat when unemployment is low? Because employers can always bring in someone cheaper." This framing works for working-class and trade audiences, which are heavily on Facebook.

**Cultural identity and community change.** This one requires careful handling to avoid xenophobia, but it is a genuine concern for many NZ communities including Maori. Frame it around pace of change, consultation, and community voice rather than who is arriving.

**What the parties won't tell you.** Accountability framing. "Labour won't say what their immigration target is. National won't say what their immigration target is. Why not?" This creates the forcing function. It positions silence as suspicious.

### Making content shareable and viral in NZ Facebook communities

The share is the atomic unit of Facebook virality. Shares push content into new networks. To get shares in NZ:

**Use local specificity.** "Immigration and housing in Tauranga" will be shared in Tauranga groups. "Immigration and housing in New Zealand" will be shared nowhere. Create regional versions of core content. This is more work but it is the difference between content that circulates and content that doesn't.

**Use questions, not statements.** "Should NZ have an annual immigration cap?" gets shared because people want to solicit opinions from their friends. A statement ("NZ should cap immigration at 50,000") gets argued with but not shared.

**Use outrage sparingly but strategically.** Facebook's algorithm has been tuned to deprioritise outrage bait, but righteous frustration still works when it feels authentic. "I've been on the public housing waitlist for 3 years while the population grew by 200,000" is not outrage bait. It's a real story.

**Use comparison graphics.** "NZ's net migration per capita vs Australia, Canada, UK" -- simple bar charts that make people say "I had no idea." Data that surprises gets shared.

**User-generated content and testimonials.** Real people sharing their own stories (video selfies, written posts) are more shareable than produced content. A nurse saying "I can't get a GP appointment in my own town" is more powerful than any infographic.

### Emotional triggers: Facebook vs X

On X, immigration content that works tends to be clever, argumentative, and framed for political insiders. On Facebook, what works is:

- **Concern for family and community.** "What kind of New Zealand are my kids going to grow up in?" Parents share this.
- **Frustration with being ignored.** "Politicians think we don't care about this. Prove them wrong -- share this post." The politically disengaged respond to feeling overlooked.
- **Local pride and protectiveness.** "Our town is changing faster than anyone asked for." Provincial identity is strong on Facebook.
- **Fairness.** "We're not anti-immigrant. We just want a plan." This framing neutralises the racism accusation and makes the content feel safe to share.

Avoid: sarcasm (doesn't land on Facebook like it does on X), partisan framing (alienates the disengaged), wonky policy language (kills engagement), anything that could be screenshotted and used to paint the campaign as racist.

### Reaching the politically disengaged

The disengaged are on Facebook. They're just not following political pages. You reach them through:

1. **Groups they're already in.** Local buy/sell groups, community pages, parenting groups, sports club pages. You don't post political content in these directly (you'll get banned). But people who are active in your immigration-focused groups also post in these spaces, and they share content naturally.

2. **Paid distribution.** Target people who are not politically engaged on Facebook (Meta lets you target by interest -- exclude people who follow political pages and media). Serve them content that connects immigration to their daily concerns without feeling "political." A Reel about hospital wait times that ends with "maybe it's time we talked about population planning" reaches people who would never click on a post from a political page.

3. **Messenger and WhatsApp.** In NZ, WhatsApp is used heavily in immigrant communities, and Messenger is universal. Content that gets shared in Messenger threads (especially short videos and graphics) reaches people who never see it in their News Feed. Design content to be Messenger-shareable: self-contained, short, mobile-optimised.

## 2. Group and Community Strategy

### The Group architecture

Facebook Groups are the engine. This is where sustained discussion happens, where people feel ownership, and where local pressure builds. The strategy uses a hub-and-spoke model.

**Hub group: "Immigration Policy NZ" (or similar neutral name).** This is the national-level group. Its purpose is to be the central clearinghouse for immigration policy discussion. It should feel non-partisan, evidence-based, and focused on demanding policy detail from all parties. Moderation is critical -- keep it civil, remove racist content immediately, and enforce a "policy not people" rule. Target: 15,000-25,000 members by election day.

**Spoke groups: Regional.** "Immigration & Housing -- Bay of Plenty," "Population Growth -- Canterbury," "Our Community, Our Future -- Waikato." These connect the national issue to local experience. They are smaller (2,000-5,000 members each) but much more engaged. Start with the regions where population growth is most felt: Bay of Plenty, Waikato, Canterbury, Queenstown-Lakes, Hawke's Bay.

**Do not create fake grassroots groups.** It gets exposed and it destroys credibility. Be transparent that these are groups for people who want immigration to be an election issue. Authenticity is the only durable strategy on Facebook.

### Should you create new groups or work within existing ones?

Both, but with different approaches.

**Create new groups** for the dedicated immigration policy discussion. You control the framing, the moderation, and the admin team. These are your owned spaces.

**Engage in existing groups** as individual participants, not as a campaign. The key existing groups in NZ are:

- Regional community pages (e.g., "Tauranga Community Noticeboard," "Christchurch NZ," "Hamilton Living"). These have tens of thousands of members. You cannot astroturf these. But you can be a genuine participant who raises immigration topics when relevant. When someone posts about housing costs or hospital waits, a thoughtful comment linking it to population growth is legitimate participation.

- Political discussion groups ("NZ Politics," "New Zealand Political Discussion," various partisan groups). Share content from your hub group into these.

- Issue-specific groups (housing, healthcare, education). These are natural allies. Immigration intersects with every one of these issues.

**The infiltration question.** Do not "infiltrate" groups in any covert sense. It is unnecessary and counterproductive. Instead, build a network of genuine advocates who participate in their own community groups under their own names. This is how grassroots politics actually works. Give people content, talking points, and confidence, and they will carry the message into every group they belong to.

### Activating provincial New Zealand

Provincial NZ is where this campaign has the most room to grow. NZ First already has some of this audience, but the opportunity is to make immigration a cross-party concern in provincial communities.

**Town-specific content.** Create content for specific towns: "Hastings added X people in 5 years. How many new houses were built? How many new GP clinics opened?" This gets shared in town-specific Facebook pages because it is about their town.

**Local champions.** Identify and support local voices in each region -- a GP frustrated with patient loads, a builder who can't keep up with demand, a first-home buyer who has been priced out. These people create content that is inherently local and credible.

**Facebook Events for town halls.** Facebook Events are underrated. Create real-world town hall events in provincial centres (see Section 4) and promote them as Facebook Events. The event itself generates discussion in the lead-up, and post-event content (video clips, key moments) feeds back into the Groups.

### Maori and Pacific community Facebook spaces

Maori and Pacific communities are very active on Facebook, often in dedicated community groups and on Pages associated with iwi, churches, and community organisations.

**Do not treat these communities as targets to be activated.** Engage respectfully and recognise that Maori and Pacific communities have their own perspectives on immigration that may differ from Pakeha provincial perspectives. For Maori, immigration intersects with te Tiriti, tino rangatiratanga, and concerns about tangata whenua voice in population planning. For Pacific communities, immigration policy directly affects family reunification and community ties.

**Content for these audiences should be co-created with people from these communities**, not imposed from outside. Find Maori and Pacific voices who care about immigration policy and support them in creating content in their own framing. A Maori perspective on immigration ("As tangata whenua, shouldn't we have a say in how many people come to Aotearoa?") is powerful and brings a dimension that Pakeha-dominated immigration discourse often lacks.

**Te reo content.** Even small amounts of te reo Maori in content signals respect and reaches audiences that English-only content misses.

## 3. Paid Strategy

### Why paid is essential

Organic reach on Facebook Pages is around 2-5% of followers in 2026. You cannot build fast enough on organic alone. Paid amplification is how you turn a small campaign into something that reaches hundreds of thousands of New Zealanders. The good news is that NZ is a small, cheap market for Facebook ads.

### Budget

For a New Zealand issue advocacy campaign, realistic budgets are:

- **Minimum viable:** $2,000-3,000/month. This gets you meaningful reach in targeted regions but limits your ability to run national campaigns.
- **Effective:** $5,000-8,000/month. This lets you run regional and national campaigns simultaneously, test multiple creatives, and sustain presence.
- **Aggressive:** $15,000-25,000/month. This puts you in front of a significant share of Facebook-active New Zealanders regularly.

For context, NZ Facebook CPMs (cost per thousand impressions) typically run $5-15 for broad targeting and $15-30 for narrow political-interest targeting. A $5,000/month budget gets you roughly 300,000-1,000,000 impressions depending on targeting.

**Phase the budget.** Start at $3,000/month in the first 30 days while you test creative and targeting. Scale to $8,000/month once you know what works. Reserve budget for spikes around key political moments (party conferences, budget, election campaign period).

### Targeting

Meta's ad targeting in NZ allows:

**Geographic targeting.** This is your most powerful lever. Target specific regions where immigration-related pressures are most felt: Tauranga/Western Bay of Plenty, Hamilton/Waikato, Queenstown-Lakes, Christchurch, Hawke's Bay/Hastings, Auckland (South and West). Regional targeting also lets you serve locally specific creative.

**Age targeting.** Focus on 30-65. This is the core Facebook demographic in NZ, the group most likely to vote, and the group most affected by housing and healthcare pressures.

**Interest targeting.** Target people interested in New Zealand politics, housing, healthcare, and local news. Also target followers of specific political party pages and politicians (this lets you reach each party's base with "ask your party where they stand on immigration" messaging).

**Lookalike audiences.** Once your Groups and Page have engagement, build lookalike audiences from your most engaged users. This is how you find more people like the ones already responding to your content.

**Exclusion targeting.** Exclude people who are already members of your Groups (they're already converted). Spend ad money on reaching new people.

**Note on political ad transparency.** Meta requires political ads in NZ to carry "Paid for by" disclaimers and appear in the Ad Library. This is fine. Transparency helps credibility. Set up the disclaimer properly from day one.

### Ad formats and creative

**Reel ads.** Highest performing format for reach and cost efficiency. 15-30 second videos with strong hooks. "Did you know NZ added more people per capita than any country in the OECD last year?" followed by a call to action (join group, visit page, sign petition).

**Image ads with stats.** Single image, bold stat, simple message. "110,000 net migration. 0 parties with a population plan. Demand better." These work for share-driving.

**Carousel ads.** "What does each party say about immigration?" with one card per party. Most cards say "Nothing yet." This format creates a forcing function because party supporters share it demanding their party fill in the blank.

**Lead generation ads.** Use Meta's native lead forms to collect email addresses and phone numbers for supporters. "Want to see every party's immigration policy before you vote? Sign up and we'll send you the comparison when they release them." This builds your direct contact list for offline organising.

### Using ads to force party responses

Run targeted ads to followers of each party page showing that their party has no immigration policy. "You support Labour. Does Labour support an immigration target? Ask them." When these ads reach party supporters, some of them actually do contact their party, and party staffers see the ads themselves because they follow their own party pages. This creates internal pressure.

Run ads in electorate-specific areas targeting voters of specific MPs. "Your MP [Name] hasn't said a word about immigration. Ask them at their next clinic." This is the most direct form of pressure.

## 4. Forcing Functions: Translating Facebook Into Political Pressure

### The fundamental problem

Facebook engagement means nothing to politicians unless it translates into something they can feel: media coverage, constituent contacts, and votes at risk. The strategy must bridge online discussion to offline pressure.

### Making politicians see constituent concern

**Tag and question campaigns.** Coordinate Group members to post questions on politician Facebook pages. "Hi [MP Name], what is your party's position on setting an annual immigration target?" When dozens of constituents post the same question, the MP's staff notices. Screenshot the non-responses and share them in Groups.

**Electorate scorecards.** Create and share graphics scoring each electorate MP on whether they have made a public statement on immigration policy. "Your MP: Silent on immigration" is a shareable, taggable piece of content.

**Petition with real signatures.** Use a Facebook-promoted petition ("We demand all parties publish detailed immigration policy before the 2026 election") and aim for 20,000+ signatures. Deliver it physically to Parliament with media present. The petition itself is a Facebook content moment (photos, video of delivery).

### Crossing over into mainstream media

NZ mainstream media (Stuff, NZ Herald, RNZ, Newshub) monitors Facebook for stories. To trigger crossover:

**Create polling.** Commission a Curia or Taxpayers Union-style poll on immigration attitudes. "72% of New Zealanders want parties to state an immigration target." This is a media story. Promote the results on Facebook. Media covers the poll, which drives more Facebook engagement, which creates more media stories.

**Organise events that are inherently newsworthy.** A town hall in Tauranga on immigration with 300 attendees is a news story. Film it. Post highlights on Facebook. Media picks it up.

**Provide spokespeople.** Media needs talking heads. Have two or three articulate, media-trained spokespeople who can represent the campaign on radio and TV. Their media appearances become Facebook content (clips shared in Groups).

**Track and publish party silence.** Maintain a public tracker (updated weekly on Facebook) showing which parties have published immigration policy and which haven't. The tracker itself becomes a media story as the election approaches. "With 6 months to go, only NZ First has published an immigration policy."

### Organising real-world action through Facebook

**Town halls.** Use Facebook Events to organise town hall meetings in 6-8 provincial centres. Invite local MPs (they'll either attend and be asked hard questions, or decline and be publicly noted as declining). Film everything. Post-event content drives the next wave of online engagement.

**Candidate forums.** As the election approaches and candidates are confirmed, organise or co-organise candidate forums specifically on immigration. Use Facebook to promote, livestream, and distribute clips.

**Local action kits.** Provide Group members with downloadable resources: template letters to MPs, questions to ask at electorate clinics, printable flyers for community noticeboards. Distribute these through Facebook Groups and Messenger.

## 5. Automation and Code

This section covers what can be built with code, including tools you could create using Claude Code (Anthropic's AI coding tool). Everything described here works within Facebook's Terms of Service and Meta's API policies.

### Content scheduling and management

**Tool: Meta Business Suite API integration.** Build a Python script that schedules posts across your Page, Groups, and Instagram account via the Meta Graph API. This lets you batch-create a week's content and schedule it for optimal posting times.

```
# Core components:
# - meta_scheduler.py: Posts content via Graph API at scheduled times
# - content_calendar.py: Reads from a spreadsheet/Airtable of planned content
# - regional_variants.py: Takes a template post and generates regional versions
#   (swapping in local stats for each region)
```

**Claude Code application:** Use Claude Code to build a content generation pipeline. Feed it a data source (Stats NZ population data, housing data, health wait time data by region) and have it generate regional variants of template posts. "In [Region], the population grew by [X] while [Y] houses were built" -- with real numbers pulled from data files.

### Ad optimisation

**Tool: Meta Marketing API scripts.** Build scripts that:
- Automatically pause underperforming ad sets (below threshold CTR or CPM)
- Scale budget toward top-performing creatives
- Generate weekly performance reports
- A/B test ad copy variants systematically

```
# Core components:
# - ad_monitor.py: Polls Meta Marketing API every 6 hours, pauses/scales ads
# - ad_reporter.py: Generates weekly PDF/HTML performance report
# - creative_tester.py: Manages systematic A/B testing of ad variants
```

### Engagement monitoring and response

**Tool: Group and Page engagement tracker.** A dashboard that monitors:
- Post performance across all your owned Groups and Pages
- Comment volume and sentiment (using Claude API for sentiment analysis)
- Member growth rates
- Top-performing content themes and formats
- Alerts when a post goes unusually viral or when negative sentiment spikes

```
# Core components:
# - engagement_tracker.py: Pulls engagement data via Graph API
# - sentiment_analyzer.py: Uses Claude API to classify comment sentiment
# - dashboard.py: Streamlit or Flask web app showing real-time metrics
# - alert_system.py: Sends Slack/email alerts on unusual activity
```

### Politician tracking

**Tool: MP immigration statement tracker.** A semi-automated system that:
- Monitors public Facebook pages of all NZ MPs and party pages
- Flags any posts mentioning immigration, population, migration, visa, etc.
- Logs these in a database with date, MP, party, and content summary
- Uses Claude API to classify whether the statement constitutes a "policy position" or just commentary
- Feeds the public-facing "party policy tracker" graphic

```
# Core components:
# - mp_monitor.py: Tracks public posts from MP/party Facebook pages via Graph API
# - statement_classifier.py: Uses Claude to classify immigration-related statements
# - policy_tracker_db.py: SQLite database of all tracked statements
# - tracker_generator.py: Generates the weekly "policy tracker" graphic
```

**Note on data collection:** Only collect data from public pages and public posts. Do not scrape private Groups or personal profiles. Use the official Graph API. This keeps you within Meta's Terms of Service.

### Comment moderation

**Tool: AI-assisted moderation bot.** For your owned Groups:
- Automatically flag comments that may be racist, threatening, or violate group rules
- Use Claude API to assess flagged comments
- Auto-hide clearly violating comments and queue borderline ones for human review
- Generate daily moderation reports

This is important because immigration discussions attract bad-faith actors and racists. Fast moderation keeps your Groups credible and safe.

### Data dashboard

**Tool: Campaign command centre.** A single Streamlit web application that brings together:
- Facebook Page and Group analytics
- Ad performance metrics
- Petition signature count
- MP statement tracker
- Media mention tracker (via Google Alerts or media monitoring API)
- Email list growth
- Event RSVPs and attendance

Build this iteratively with Claude Code. Start with the Facebook analytics module, then add components as the campaign develops.

### What Claude Code is particularly good for here

Claude Code is ideal for:
- Rapidly building the data pipeline scripts (pulling Stats NZ data, generating regional content variants)
- Creating the Streamlit dashboard
- Writing the Graph API integration code
- Building the sentiment analysis and comment classification systems
- Iterating on ad copy by generating and testing variants
- Parsing and structuring data from public Facebook pages into your tracking database
- Building the petition and email signup infrastructure

The key advantage is speed. A solo operator or tiny team can build in a weekend what would otherwise require a developer. Claude Code can scaffold the entire project structure, write the API integrations, build the dashboard, and help debug issues as they arise.

### Recommended tech stack

- **Language:** Python 3.11+
- **Web framework:** Streamlit (for dashboard) or FastAPI (for webhook endpoints)
- **Database:** SQLite for simplicity, PostgreSQL if scaling
- **APIs:** Meta Graph API, Meta Marketing API, Claude API, Stats NZ API
- **Hosting:** Railway or Fly.io (cheap, simple deployment)
- **Scheduling:** cron jobs or APScheduler for Python
- **Monitoring:** Sentry for error tracking

## 6. Campaign Architecture

### Team structure

This campaign can be run by a very small team because of automation:

- **Campaign lead (1 person).** Strategy, political relationships, media, overall direction. This person makes the judgment calls.
- **Content creator (1-2 people).** Creates Reels, graphics, and written content. Ideally someone who understands NZ Facebook culture and can create content that feels native, not produced.
- **Community manager (1 person).** Manages Groups, moderates, responds to comments, coordinates with regional volunteers. This is the most time-intensive role.
- **Technical/automation (1 person, part-time or contractor).** Builds and maintains the tools described in Section 5. Could use Claude Code extensively to reduce this to a very part-time role.
- **Regional volunteers (6-10 people).** One or two people in each target region who are genuine community members, active in local Facebook Groups, and willing to be local champions for the campaign.

Total: 3-4 paid team members, 6-10 volunteers. Budget: $12,000-18,000/month for team plus $5,000-8,000/month for ads. Total operational cost: roughly $20,000-25,000/month for an effective national campaign. This is extremely cheap by political campaign standards.

### Phase 1: Foundation (Days 1-30)

**Objective:** Build the infrastructure, start generating content, establish presence.

Actions:
- Set up the central Facebook Page and hub Group. Brand it clearly as non-partisan issue advocacy.
- Set up 3-4 regional Groups (Bay of Plenty, Waikato, Canterbury, Queenstown-Lakes).
- Build the content pipeline. Create 30 days of content in advance. Focus on housing-immigration connection as the opening theme because it has the broadest resonance.
- Set up Meta Business Suite and start running ads ($3,000 budget, focused on Group growth and Page likes in target regions).
- Build the first version of the engagement dashboard and MP tracker using Claude Code.
- Recruit initial regional volunteers through personal networks and early Group members.
- Begin posting daily in all Groups: mix of data posts, questions, local stories, and shareable graphics.

Metrics targets by Day 30:
- Hub Group: 3,000 members
- Regional Groups: 500-1,000 members each
- Facebook Page: 5,000 followers
- Email list: 1,000 signups
- First batch of content tested, top performers identified

### Phase 2: Escalation (Days 31-60)

**Objective:** Build engagement momentum, start creating forcing functions, get first media coverage.

Actions:
- Scale ad budget to $5,000-8,000/month. Shift budget toward top-performing content and targeting.
- Launch the "Party Policy Tracker" -- a weekly graphic showing which parties have published immigration policy. Promote it heavily.
- Commission and publish a poll on NZ immigration attitudes. Use this as a media hook.
- Begin the MP tagging campaign. Coordinate Group members to ask their MPs about immigration on Facebook.
- Launch the petition.
- Plan the first town hall event (target: Tauranga, where housing/population pressure is most visible).
- Expand to 2-3 more regional Groups based on where organic interest is strongest.
- Start creating video testimonials from real people in affected communities.

Metrics targets by Day 60:
- Hub Group: 8,000 members
- Total regional Group membership: 5,000+
- Facebook Page: 12,000 followers
- Petition: 5,000 signatures
- At least one mainstream media story about the campaign or the poll results
- At least one party responds to the policy tracker (even if just to criticise it, that's engagement)

### Phase 3: Pressure (Days 61-90)

**Objective:** Translate online momentum into real political pressure. Make immigration unavoidable.

Actions:
- Hold the first town hall in Tauranga. Film it, distribute clips across all platforms.
- Scale to second and third town halls in other regions.
- Increase ad spend on electorate-targeted "your MP is silent" campaigns.
- If any party has released immigration policy, publicly acknowledge it and shift pressure to the remaining parties. "National has put their position on the table. Labour, ACT, Greens -- where's yours?"
- Deliver the petition to Parliament (aim for 15,000+ signatures by this point).
- Launch Instagram as a secondary channel, repurposing Reel content for younger demographics.
- Build relationships with sympathetic journalists who want to cover immigration policy as an election issue.

Metrics targets by Day 90:
- Hub Group: 15,000 members
- Facebook Page: 20,000 followers
- Petition: 15,000 signatures
- 3+ mainstream media stories
- At least 2 parties have made substantive immigration policy statements (even if just in response to pressure)
- Town hall attendance: 200+ at first event

### Phase 4: Election Campaign (Days 90 to election)

**Objective:** Make immigration one of the top 3 election issues. Ensure every party has a published position.

Actions:
- Shift content to comparison mode: "Here's what each party is promising on immigration." Side-by-side graphics and videos.
- Organise candidate forums in key electorates.
- Run "vote on immigration" ad campaigns targeting undecided voters in swing electorates.
- Publish a final "immigration policy report card" grading each party's position on specificity and ambition.
- Coordinate with other platforms: share Facebook content to X for media/political audience, use YouTube for longer town hall recordings, use TikTok for youth reach.

### Integration with other platforms

Facebook is the primary platform, but it should feed and be fed by activity elsewhere:

- **X (Twitter):** Post key graphics, poll results, and media coverage. X is where journalists and political insiders are. Tag journalists, parties, and MPs. Use X to amplify Facebook content into media circles, not as a primary organising tool.
- **Instagram:** Repurpose Reels and graphics. Targets younger demographics (18-35) who are less active on Facebook. Use Instagram Stories for event promotion and behind-the-scenes content.
- **TikTok:** If you have capacity, repurpose short-form video for TikTok. The audience is youngest here and least likely to vote, so it is lowest priority but useful for long-term narrative building.
- **YouTube:** Upload full town hall recordings and longer-form video content. YouTube is good for search (people Googling "NZ immigration policy 2026" should find your content).
- **Email:** Weekly newsletter to your email list with campaign updates, new content, and calls to action. Email is how you mobilise people for specific moments (petition pushes, town hall attendance, MP contact campaigns).
- **WhatsApp:** Create a broadcast channel for supporters. WhatsApp is increasingly important in NZ for community organising, especially in Pacific and immigrant communities.

### Key metrics to track throughout

- **Reach:** Total impressions across organic and paid content
- **Engagement rate:** Comments, shares, and reactions as a percentage of reach
- **Group growth:** Weekly new members across all Groups
- **Share rate:** The most important organic metric. If content is being shared, it is working.
- **Petition signatures:** Cumulative and weekly growth
- **Media mentions:** Count and quality of mainstream media coverage
- **Party responses:** The ultimate metric. Track every public statement by every party on immigration policy.
- **Cost per result:** For paid campaigns, track cost per Group join, cost per petition signature, cost per email signup
- **Sentiment:** Track whether the overall tone of discussion in your Groups is constructive or toxic. Toxic Groups kill campaigns.

## Key Risks and Mitigations

**Risk: Branded as racist/xenophobic.** Mitigation: Rigorous moderation, non-partisan framing, prominent Maori and migrant voices in the campaign, focus on "we want a plan" not "we want fewer immigrants." Have clear public messaging ready: "This campaign is about planning, not prejudice."

**Risk: Facebook algorithm changes reduce reach.** Mitigation: Diversify across Groups (algorithm affects Pages more than Groups), maintain email list as a Facebook-independent channel, maintain presence on other platforms.

**Risk: Political parties ignore the campaign.** Mitigation: This is actually fine for a while -- "they're ignoring you" is powerful content. The pressure compounds. As the election gets closer, silence becomes more costly.

**Risk: Astroturfing accusations.** Mitigation: Be transparent about who is behind the campaign. Publish funding sources. Use real names. Authenticity is the only defence.

**Risk: Meta ad policy changes or political ad restrictions.** Mitigation: Stay strictly within Meta's ad policies from day one. Don't rely exclusively on paid reach -- build organic Group infrastructure that works without ads.

1. Register a Facebook Page with clear non-partisan branding. Set up Meta Business Suite.
2. Create the hub Group with detailed rules emphasising "policy not people."
3. Write and design the first 14 days of content (mix of formats).
4. Set up the Meta advertising account and political ad disclaimers.
5. Begin building the engagement dashboard and MP tracker with Claude Code.
6. Identify and contact 10 potential regional volunteers.
7. Create the first two regional Groups.
8. Launch the Page and Groups. Start posting.
